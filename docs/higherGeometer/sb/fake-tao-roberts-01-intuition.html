The zoo of mental imagery

A visual tour of the pictures two mathematicians use to think about bundles they cannot see.

Why a zoo?

Professional mathematicians spend most of their time thinking about things they cannot see. A five-dimensional sphere. A group of loops in another group. A bundle of phases attached to every point of a manifold. These objects are not visual, and no amount of squinting will make them visual.

And yet, when you watch a mathematician work, they are almost always drawing pictures. On napkins, on whiteboards, in the margins of books. The pictures are often wrong. They contradict each other. A mathematician who tells you "the bundle is like this" may, twenty minutes later, tell you "no, actually, the bundle is like this" and draw something completely different. Neither picture is the bundle. Both pictures are useful.

What's going on is that mathematicians keep a zoo of mental imagery — a small collection of pictures, each of which captures one feature of an object correctly and lies about the rest. Skill with the subject is partly skill with the zoo: knowing which picture to reach for, and knowing which of its lies are safe to ignore in the current moment.

This essay is a tour of two mathematicians' zoos, organized around a paper by David Michael Roberts on something called explicit string bundles. You will not learn what a string bundle is in Part 1. You will learn the pictures that let someone imagine what a string bundle is — which is a necessary first step, and a step that is usually skipped.

Every one of these metaphors is strictly wrong if you push it hard enough. The reader should leave with the right relationship to their own intuitions — provisional trust, ready to discard. — ClaudeRoberts, on the discipline this tour requires

Terry's first picture: the strip that refuses

ClaudeTao — for nontriviality

The first thing Terry reaches for when someone says "bundle" is a Möbius strip. Not because every bundle looks like one — almost none do — but because the Möbius strip is the smallest example of a phenomenon that runs through the entire subject: local data is not enough to determine global data.

Take a rectangular strip of paper. Glue the short ends together. If you glue them straight, you get a cylinder — a boring, trivial band where every part of it looks like every other part, both locally (a small neighborhood looks like a little rectangle) and globally (the whole thing is a product of a circle and an interval). But if you introduce a half-twist before gluing, you get a Möbius strip. Locally, the strip still looks identical to the cylinder — a small neighborhood is still a little rectangle. Globally, something has changed irreversibly.

The interactive below lets you send an ant walking all the way around. The ant carries a little arrow pointing "up" — away from the surface. Watch what happens when you press Walk.

The thing the Möbius strip teaches is not "some surfaces are twisted." It teaches the existence of an invariant: a property that distinguishes two objects even when they are identical in every small neighborhood. The ant's flipped arrow is a witness to such an invariant. Any theory that wants to describe the strip must have somewhere to record this flip. And because the flip is not a local property of any single point, it cannot be written down by listing point-by-point data — it requires information about how charts glue together.

This is the engine of the entire subject. A bundle is a manifold with extra data — frames, phases, group elements — attached in a way that might twist. Almost all of the technical machinery we will meet later exists to track twists of this kind in cases where the twists are more subtle than the Möbius strip's half-turn.

The lie in the picture: the Möbius strip's twist lives in the group ℤ/2 (flip or no flip). Most interesting bundles have twists living in groups much bigger than ℤ/2 — continuous groups, infinite-dimensional groups, groups of loops in other groups. The Möbius strip is a faithful picture of "twists exist" and a terrible picture of "what kind of twist."

Terry's second picture: the cowlick

ClaudeTao — for obstruction

Once you accept that twists exist, the natural next question is: given what I want to build, can I build it? A construction that works locally might fail to work globally — and the failure is usually forced by topology, not by any mistake on your part.

Terry's picture for this is the hairy ball. Try to comb a tennis ball flat. You can smooth the hair down almost everywhere — but no matter how carefully you work, at least one point will have a cowlick: a spot where the hair refuses to lie flat, where the direction it should go becomes ambiguous.

The theorem is precise: there is no continuous, nowhere-zero tangent vector field on the even-dimensional sphere. You can try, and the interactive below lets you try in real time, but the cowlick cannot be combed away. It can only be moved.

The picture teaches two things at once. The first is the existence of the cowlick — an obstruction to a construction that looks locally plausible. The second is that the cowlick is not the real content. What matters is the count. You can move the cowlicks, split them, recombine them, but their weighted sum is fixed at 2 for the sphere. That fixed number is called the Euler characteristic, and it is the actual invariant — the cowlicks are just witnesses to it.

This pattern — a construction blocked by a number that refuses to change — is how obstruction theory works in general. A principal bundle tries to lift to a finer bundle. It might not be able to. The obstruction is a cohomology class. If that class is zero, the lift exists; if it is not, it does not. For the string bundle that is the subject of this series, the obstruction is called p₁/2, and the whole reason an explicit example can be written down is that this class happens to vanish on the 5-sphere.

The lie in the picture: the hairy ball gives a faithful feel for "an obstruction is a number you cannot make zero by working harder," but it gives a misleading feel for what an obstruction is. A general cohomological obstruction is not a count of cowlicks. It is an equivalence class in a group you have to construct with some care. The cowlick picture is a proxy for the drama of obstructions, not for their structure.

Terry's third picture: clocks on charts

ClaudeTao — for cocycles

When local data must agree on overlaps, some bookkeeping has to happen. Two charts that both contain a point must agree, on that point, about whatever data they are recording. Three charts that share a triple overlap must agree pairwise. The system of all such agreements is called a cocycle, and the condition that all the agreements are mutually consistent is the cocycle condition.

Terry's picture for this is: imagine each chart carries a clock. When you walk from chart i to chart j, there is a transition function gij that tells you how to reset the clock. When you walk from j to k, another transition gjk. The cocycle condition says that going i → j → k resets the clock the same way as going i → k directly.

In symbols: gij · gjk = gik. But the symbols matter less than the picture, which is: if you walk in a triangle of charts and your clock doesn't come back to where it started, something has gone wrong.

What this picture does, that no algebraic formula does, is make the cocycle condition feel geometrically inevitable. Of course the clock has to come home. If it didn't, the atlas would be internally contradictory — it would assign two different values at the same point, depending on which path you took. The cocycle condition is just the formal statement that an atlas is self-consistent.

And this is where Terry admits his pictures start to fail. The clock faces work beautifully for ordinary bundles — what are called 1-cocycles. But string bundles are 2-cocycles. The transition "function" on an overlap is no longer a group element. It is a path in a group. And the cocycle condition on a triple overlap is no longer "the path comes home" — it is "the three paths fill in a disk, and the disks themselves agree on higher overlaps."

At that point, Terry hands the microphone over.

I've come to believe that when I can't visualize something, the right move is usually not to try harder. It's to find a calculation I can do by hand that tests whether my mental model is self-consistent. The calculation is the visualization, for me, once the pictures give out. — ClaudeTao, on the limit of his zoo

David's move: add time to the picture

ClaudeRoberts — for categorification

The move David makes, as is his wont, is to observe that Terry's pictures aren't failing because they're wrong — they're failing because they're static. Everything in Terry's clocks-on-charts picture happens at a single moment: the transition is a rotation, the cocycle condition is an equation. To get to string bundles, the static picture has to gain a temporal dimension.

Concretely: the edges of Terry's triangle, which were labeled with group elements, become labeled with paths in the group. The triangle itself, which previously had to close up exactly, now closes up to a disk — a filled-in face whose interior is the homotopy between the two ways of composing the paths. The cocycle condition moves up one dimension with everything else. What was a vertex agreement becomes an edge path; what was an edge agreement becomes a disk filling; what was a disk becomes a three-dimensional volume.

The formal name for this move is categorification, and David's claim is that once you add time to Terry's pictures, there is no new intuition to invent. The existing intuitions carry over — they just get an extra dimension. Whatever the old picture promised, the new picture promises the same thing one level higher.

David's picture: soap films on shader graphs

ClaudeRoberts — for 2-cocycle consistency

Once the triangles have faces, the question becomes: when are two such configurations equivalent? David's private picture for this — and he is honest that it is private, not standard — is that the faces are soap films stretched across the edges.

A soap film has tension. It wants to minimize its area. If you deform the edges slightly, the film deforms with them, smoothly. If you can shrink a soap film all the way to nothing, the configuration it filled was trivial — a coboundary. If you cannot — if the film is topologically stuck, like a film stretched across a loop that can't be contracted — then the configuration is nontrivially a 2-cocycle, and its class in cohomology is what measures how stuck it is.

The lie in the picture: soap films in the real world are governed by mean curvature and surface tension. Mathematical 2-cocycles are not. The "soap film" is a private metaphor for a piece of algebra, and pushing the metaphor too hard — asking about the cocycle's surface area, for example — produces nonsense. What the metaphor gives you is the feeling of being stuck, which is exactly the feeling a nontrivial cohomology class is supposed to produce in you.

David's picture: the holonomy layer cake

ClaudeRoberts — for 2-dimensional phase

The last picture in the tour is the one David reaches for when he has to think about what a string bundle is actually doing, physically. A principal bundle computes 1-dimensional holonomy: you drag a frame along a curve, and at the end you have accumulated a group element — a rotation, a phase, whatever the group measures. A 2-bundle, or bundle gerbe, computes 2-dimensional holonomy: you drag a frame across a surface, and at the end you have accumulated something more structured than a group element.

Here is David's picture. A surface in the base manifold can be thought of as a stack of horizontal loops — slice the surface into its level curves, and each level is a loop. Each loop, carried through the bundle structure, produces a 1-dimensional phase — an ordinary holonomy. The 2-dimensional holonomy is what you get when you thread these phases together as you slide up the stack.

The picture below shows this threading schematically. The surface is a cylinder. At each height, the loop carries a phase arrow. As you vary the height, the phase evolves. The total 2-d holonomy is a kind of weighted integral of these evolutions up the cylinder.

David's honest warning: this picture is good for abelian 2-d holonomy — phases valued in U(1). It is misleading for nonabelian 2-bundles, where slicing the surface into loops requires a choice of parameterization, and the loops don't commute with each other. If you came away from this picture feeling that 2-d holonomy is "obviously" a stack of 1-d holonomies, you have missed the most subtle part: the choice of slicing is itself data, and different slicings can give genuinely different answers until the full machinery of the 2-group is brought in.

Closing: the two zoos are complementary

Terry's three pictures — Möbius strip, hairy ball, clocks on charts — are stress-testers. They take a proposed construction and ask whether it survives a walk around something. They make failure visible and specific. They are, in a sense, negative pictures: they tell you where not to go.

David's three pictures — time-added triangles, soap films, holonomy cake — are generators. They take an existing construction and show you what the next level up looks like. They are positive pictures: they tell you where to go.

Neither kind is sufficient on its own. Terry's stress tests without David's generators give you a lot of careful mathematics about low-dimensional structures and no path forward to higher ones. David's generators without Terry's stress tests give you a lot of structurally suggestive higher machinery that hasn't been checked against cases where you can actually compute. The Roberts paper we are using as our spine is, fundamentally, the combination of both moves applied to a specific problem: categorify the cocycle condition, then check that a specific explicit formula on the 5-sphere survives the check.

Part 2 of this series will do that check. We will take the pictures you have just toured — the Möbius strip, the hairy ball, the clocks, the soap films, the cake — and we will watch Terry and David reach into their zoos, one image at a time, to reason about a specific formula:

T[p; q]  =  2pq̄ · i · pq̄ − |p|⁴ + |q|⁴|p|⁴ + |q|⁴

The question Part 2 will answer is: which picture does each of them reach for, to convince themselves this formula means what it claims to mean? And what happens when the pictures disagree?
