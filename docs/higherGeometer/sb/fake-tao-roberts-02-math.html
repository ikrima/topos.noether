The cake was lying

Opening — ClaudeRoberts

Part 1 ended on a warning. The holonomy cake — the picture where a surface in a group G becomes a stack of horizontal loops, each carrying a phase — works beautifully as long as G is abelian. For U(1) itself the cake is exactly the integral of curvature over a surface and there is no subtlety to find.

For nonabelian G the cake lies, in a specific and locatable place. Slicing a surface into loops requires a choice: which direction do you slice along? The choice is arbitrary. A different direction gives a different stack of loops — and in a nonabelian group the loops do not commute with each other, so composing their phases in order gives a different answer depending on the order you chose.

The interactive below is the cake from Part 1, with one new control: a slicing-angle slider. Drag it.

The drift you are producing is not numerical error. It is the honest answer to the question "what is the 2-holonomy of this surface?" — and the honest answer is that there isn't one, until you add more structure. Which is the move we make next.

The rescue: a central extension

ClaudeRoberts — with apologies to the reader for how abstract this sounds

Here is the trick. Take the space of loops in G — call it ΩG. It is itself a space (infinite-dimensional, but a space). Over ΩG, build a U(1)-bundle. That bundle records, for each loop, a phase. Two different fillings of the same loop by disks produce two different phases, and their difference is an integer — a winding number counting how the two disks, glued along the common loop into a sphere, wrap around the generator of π3(G) = ℤ.

That integer quantization is the whole point. The ambiguity is not a continuous cloud of possible phases; it is a discrete, countable, topological ambiguity. The central extension Ω̂G → ΩG is the machinery that lives upstairs of the loop space and selects a specific phase for each loop, in a way that makes the surface's 2-holonomy a well-defined element of Ω̂G once you commit to the lift.

The interactive below shows the essential picture. A loop γ in G — drawn on a stylized 2-sphere representing G — can be filled by a disk in more than one way. Toggle the filling and watch the phase (the integral of the Wess–Zumino 3-form over the filling) change. The difference between the two fillings is an integer.

Everything David said in Part 1 was true. The layer-cake picture was always implicitly choosing a filling — the one determined by your slicing direction — and as long as you never compared two slicings, you never noticed the choice being made. The moment you compare two slicings, the central extension becomes necessary. It isn't new technology. It is the technology that was quietly doing the work the whole time.

The setting: a 5-sphere that almost lifts itself

Exposition — ClaudeTao

With the cake rescued, we have the tools to talk about the subject of the paper. The object of interest is the frame bundle of the 5-sphere — at every point of S5, attach the space of all orthonormal bases of the tangent space. This assembles into a principal bundle with structure group SO(5), or equivalently, after double-covering, Spin(5).

Now climb the Whitehead tower. Each rung kills the lowest nontrivial homotopy group of the structure group and produces a finer bundle. Spin kills π1. The next rung, String, kills π3 — the group that is always ℤ for a compact simple Lie group, and the group we just spent the previous section worrying about.

The question the paper answers is: can the frame bundle of S5 be lifted up the Whitehead tower to a String bundle, and if so, can it be written down explicitly, with actual formulas that a computer could evaluate?

The existence part is abstract and quick. The obstruction to a String lift is a class p1/2 ∈ H4(S5, ℤ). That cohomology group is zero — S5 has no nontrivial 4-cohomology, for the simple reason that its 4-skeleton is contractible. So the obstruction vanishes. So the lift exists. Done.

The explicit part takes a paper.

The exceptional isomorphism Spin(5) ≅ Sp(2) is doing a lot of work in the previous paragraph. Its presence is the reason the whole construction is writable. The map we need is a map S4 → Sp(1), representing the nontrivial class in ℤ/2. S4 has a second description as the quaternionic projective line ℍP1 — homogeneous coordinates [p; q] with p, q ∈ ℍ. So we need a formula in quaternions. And the paper produces one.

The formula that lets us compute

Climax — ClaudeRoberts

Here it is, the main object of the paper, the point around which everything else rotates:

T[p; q]  =  2 p q̄ · i · p q̄ − |p|4 + |q|4|p|4 + |q|4

This is a smooth function from ℍP1 = S4 to Sp(1), the unit quaternions. You can evaluate it. You can differentiate it. You can hand it to a shader. The numerator contains a quaternionic expression p q̄ · i · p q̄ — the pure-imaginary result of conjugating i by the quaternion p q̄, doubled — plus a real scalar correction that makes the whole thing a unit quaternion. The denominator is the scalar that normalizes it.

The fact that |T| = 1 for all [p; q] is a small exercise in quaternion algebra. The fact that T's homotopy class in π4(Sp(1)) is nontrivial — that is, that T is not deformable to a constant map — is the content that makes the paper nontrivial. We cannot verify that fact visually. What we can do, and what the interactive below does, is feel the way T moves as we move its input, and notice a doubling pattern that hints at the origin of the nontriviality.

In the picture below, we fix q = 1 and restrict p to the plane spanned by the quaternion units i and j — so p = pi · i + pj · j. Under this restriction the output of T lies on a 2-sphere inside Sp(1) (its k-component vanishes), which we can draw directly. Drag the input on the left.

What you just did — dragging a point, watching its image trace a doubled circle on a sphere — is, in miniature, the content of the paper's main proposition. The formula has been verified. The map is nontrivial. The String bundle over S5 has an explicit transition function.

The paper's trick consumes the special geometry of S5. Quaternions, 4-connectivity, small homotopy. Without all three it would not work. This is the warm-up case, the one with genuine coordinates, and that is what makes it the right case to build a renderer around. — ClaudeRoberts, on why this particular formula and not another

Assembly: the cocycle on the hypercover

Denouement — ClaudeTao

One more step and the String bundle is in hand. The transition function T[p; q] we just met is the classical data — the 1-cocycle that defines the frame bundle. To lift to String we need a 2-cocycle, which is four pieces of data living on the two-level hypercover from our earlier conversation: two charts D+, D− on the base S5, and two charts H+, H− on the overlap S4 = ℍP1.

The four pieces below each correspond to a region of the hypercover. Click one to see what lives there and why it exists.

The structural shape here is worth sitting with. The first two pieces, T+ and T−, are structure — the designer chooses them, subject to the constraint that they lift the classical transition function T[p;q]. The last two, TΩ and TΩc, are property — forced by the first two and required to satisfy specific conditions.

This distinction between chosen structure and verified property runs through the paper and through higher geometry generally. You pick paths; you check that loops behave; you pick lifts of loops; you check that the lift is canonical. At each level, some things are up to you and some things must be true. Getting the two categories straight is most of the work of reading this literature.

What we've earned

At the end of two essays and a zoo of mental images, here is what the paper delivers that the pre-paper literature did not.

Before the paper: there exists a String lift of the frame bundle of S5. An assertion, verified via obstruction theory, not evaluable at any point.

After the paper: the same assertion, plus four explicit formulas — the classical transition function T[p;q], the two path-valued lifts T±, the loop TΩ, and the central-extension lift TΩc — each of which can be written in code, differentiated, plotted, fed into a renderer, and checked against the cocycle equations at specific points. The gap between "lives on a 5-sphere in theory" and "lives in a shader on an RTX card" has been closed.

The warm-up character of the S5 case should be taken seriously. The generalization to other homogeneous spaces — G2/SU(3) = S6, Spin(7)/G2 = S7, and the octonionic F4/Spin(9) = ℝP2 that the paper sets as an exercise — is genuinely open. The trick consumes the quaternionic structure of S5. Other spaces need other tricks, and in the octonionic case, so far as we're aware, nobody has written them down.

That is the invitation this essay series ends on. The paper is finished; the tools are ready; the zoo has been built. The next explicit string bundle that appears in the literature is one you could write.
