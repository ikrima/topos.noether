Roberts' note was written in quaternions for a reason. Quaternions want to be drawn.

§1   $\mathrm{Sp}(1) \cong S^3$ in the 3-ball

Stereographic projection from $-1 \in S^3$ sends the unit quaternion sphere to $\mathbb{R}^3$. The basepoint $1$ goes to the origin, the equator $\{i, j, k, \ldots\}$ goes to the unit sphere, and the antipode escapes to infinity. Below is that ball, with three reference great circles — the $(1,i)$, $(1,j)$ and $(1,k)$ equators. These are the three coordinate directions of $S^3$ thought of as a Lie group.

§2   The transition function $T : S^4 \to S^3$

$T[p;q] = \dfrac{2\,p\,\bar q\,i\,q\,\bar p \;-\; |p|^4 + |q|^4}{|p|^4 + |q|^4}$

Roberts' formula takes homogeneous coordinates $[p:q]$ on $\mathbb{HP}^1 = S^4$ and returns a unit quaternion. Below you can drag $p$ and $q$ — you'll see the output point move inside the $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$ ball. The sweep button sends a loop in $\mathbb{HP}^1$ around the $(1,j)$-circle of $q$ and draws the image loop in $S^3$. That image is what carries the nontrivial class in $\pi_4(\mathrm{Sp}(1))$.

§3   Path lifts $T_+$ and $T_-$ as curves in $S^3$

The String cocycle doesn't want a quaternion — it wants a path in $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$, based at $1$. Roberts writes down two such families, one per chart of the refined cover:

$$T_+(q)(s) = \frac{|q|^4 - s^2 + 2 q i \bar q}{|q|^4 + s^2}, \qquad T_-(p)(s) = \frac{|p|^4 s^2 - 1 + 2 p i \bar p}{|p|^4 s^2 + 1} \cdot \Big(\tfrac{s-i}{s+i}\Big)^{\!2}.$$

Below, each is drawn as an actual 3-curve starting at the basepoint (amber) and ending somewhere in $S^3$. Drag the parameter slider to see the whole family of paths flow. Toggle the chart to compare $T_+$ against $T_-$.

§4   The loop $T_\Omega(q)$ and its deformation

The difference $T_- T_+^{-1}$ is a closed loop in $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$, based at $1$, depending smoothly on the parameter $q \in \mathbb{H}^\times$ on the overlap. Roberts' formula:

$$T_\Omega(q)(s) \;=\; \frac{(s + Q)(sQ - 1)}{(s - Q)(sQ + 1)} \cdot \Big(\tfrac{s - i}{s + i}\Big)^{\!2}, \qquad Q = q i \bar q.$$

In 3D it is striking how the loop moves as $q$ varies — it sweeps across the ball, threading through the great circles, never breaking, always passing through $1$. Hit play to watch $q$ walk along a circle in $\mathbb{H}^\times$; the loop does not come back to its starting shape trivially — and that is what the central extension is tracking.

§5   What the central lift is remembering

Each loop $T_\Omega(q)$ is contractible in $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$ — $\pi_1(S^3) = 0$, so there's nothing topological stopping us. The question is whether there's a coherent choice of contraction, one that varies continuously with $q$. That coherence is the data of an element in $\widehat{\Omega\mathrm{Sp}(1)}$, and it's the reason a naïve lift fails: the central $\mathrm{U}(1)$ phase picked up as $q$ moves around the overlap is the essential new information.

Roberts' trick — pick any path $q_t$ in $\mathbb{H}^\times$ from $1$ to $q$, set $\widehat{T_\Omega}(q) = [T_\Omega(q_t), 1]$ — is valid because the obstructions vanish in this particular setting, and because the quotient by $\widetilde{\Omega^2 \mathrm{Sp}(1)}$ kills the path-dependence. What you were watching in Scene 4 is essentially the family over which that homotopy is being built.
