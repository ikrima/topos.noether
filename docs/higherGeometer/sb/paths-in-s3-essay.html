Paths in S³

A visual warm-up for Explicit String Bundles

Based on arXiv:2203.04544 by David Michael Roberts. Interactive essay built with three.js.

You already know that a unit quaternion is a rotation. Every time you slerp a camera or blend skinning weights, you are computing inside the 3-sphere S³ of unit quaternions. What graphics use of quaternions rarely insists on is that the path you took through S³ to reach your current rotation carries information that the rotation alone has thrown away — and that information, tracked carefully through several floors of abstraction, is the entire content of what mathematicians call a String bundle.

This essay builds the intuition for that claim in five interactive stages, each earning the vocabulary for the next. Every diagram is live: drag the sliders, scrub the parameters, and watch the geometry respond. Nothing appears before you can touch it.

Stage 1 — The plate trick

A unit quaternion q acts on a frame by rotation. As q moves through S³, the frame rotates. But S³ double-covers the rotation group SO(3): the quaternions q and −q produce the same rotation. A 2π rotation corresponds to a path from q = 1 to q = −1 in S³ — not a loop, just a path, because 1 and −1 are distinct points that happen to map to the same rotation. Only a 4π rotation — going all the way around S³ — closes up into a genuine loop. The ribbon records this: one turn twists it; two turns untwist it.

Stage 2 — Two paths, one homotopy

If the path matters, not just the endpoint, we need to ask: when are two paths "the same"? The answer is homotopy — a continuous deformation of one path into the other, keeping endpoints fixed. On S³, which is simply connected, every pair of paths with the same endpoints is homotopic. The left view shows two such paths; the right view shows their difference loop (path A forward, path B backward), which contracts to a point because S³ is simply connected.

Stage 3 — Loops in loop space

The space of loops in S³ is called ΩS³. Its points are entire loops, and a path in ΩS³ is a one-parameter family of loops sweeping through S³. Unlike S³ itself, ΩS³ is not simply connected — it has π₁ ≅ ℤ, detected by how many times a family of loops wraps around S³. This integer is the seed of the central extension the paper builds its String 2-group from.

Stage 4 — The actual formula

We leave the scaffolding behind. The transition function for the frame bundle of S⁵, from page 2 of the paper, is:

T(p) = (2 p i p̄ − |p|⁴ + 1) / (|p|⁴ + 1)

At p = 0, T = 1 (identity). As |p| → ∞, T → −1 (antipode). We slice S⁴ at each radius r — the sphere |p| = r in H — and plot the image of a 2-parameter sub-slice under T. As r grows, the deformed sphere sweeps from the identity toward the antipode.

Stage 5 — The cocycle, live

Page 3 defines two path-valued functions T₊(q) and T₋(p). For each fixed q, these are paths in Sp(1) parameterised by s, with the same endpoints. Their difference T_Ω(q) is therefore a loop — and that loop is what the central extension lifts. Every symbol on page 3 corresponds to a colour on screen.

T₊(q) = s ↦ (|q|⁴ − s² + 2 q i q̄) / (|q|⁴ + s²)
T₋(p) = s ↦ ((|p|⁴s² − 1 + 2 p i p̄) / (|p|⁴s² + 1)) · ((s−i)/(s+i))²

What you've seen

A rotation is a point in S³. A path in S³ records how you got there. Two paths differ by a loop. A family of loops sweeps out a surface whose wrapping integer is the homotopy class. The central extension ΩSp(1)^ remembers that integer as a U(1) phase. The paper's formulas T₊, T₋, T_Ω are the explicit, coordinates-in-hand instantiation of this entire ladder for the frame bundle of S⁵. A String bundle is what you get when you insist on tracking all of this data — paths, loops, wrapping phases — coherently across a manifold. The word "Explicit" in the paper's title is a complaint about the prior literature as much as a description: you can build String bundles by hand, with formulas you can compute, and you have just watched them compute.
