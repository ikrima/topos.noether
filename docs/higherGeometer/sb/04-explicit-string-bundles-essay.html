Explicit String Bundles

an interactive reading of a note by David Michael Roberts
after arXiv:2203.04544 · higher geometry, made touchable

Most accounts of higher bundles never quite point at one. The objects live in the rafters of homotopy theory — classified, characterised, obstructed — but rarely written down in coordinates a physicist could hand to a machine. Roberts' note is a gentle rebellion against that. He takes the simplest interesting case, the frame bundle of the 5-sphere, and writes its String lift as a formula you can type. This essay is that formula, laid out as something you can drag.

The Challenge. Write down a nontrivial 2-bundle. Equivalently, produce a Čech cocycle taking values in a nontrivial crossed module $(K \xrightarrow{t} H,\; H \times K \xrightarrow{\alpha} K)$, satisfying $$h^\alpha_{ij} h^\beta_{jk} = t(k^{\alpha\beta\gamma}_{ijk})\, h^\gamma_{ik}, \quad t(h^\alpha_{ij}, k^{\beta\lambda\varepsilon}_{jkl})\, k^{\alpha\varepsilon\delta}_{ijl} = k^{\alpha\beta\gamma}_{ijk} k^{\gamma\lambda\delta}_{ikl}.$$

The double index — Roman $i,j,k$ and Greek $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ — is the first clue that something is being refined. We return to it below.

§1   A 5-sphere, covered by two hats

Saemann's question, reworded: lift the frame bundle of (conformally compactified) $\mathbb{R}^{5,1} \cong S^5 \times S^1$ to a String bundle. The circle is inert, so the whole problem lives on $S^5$. Since $S^5$ is 4-connected, the Stiefel–Whitney class $w_1$ and the fractional Pontryagin class $\tfrac{p_1}{2}$ both vanish — the lift exists. The work is in writing it down.

Cover $S^5$ by two open discs $D^+$ and $D^-$, overlapping in a thickened equator $\sim S^4$. Drag the slider to open the cover and see the overlap emerge.

FIG. 1 — $U = D^+ \sqcup D^- \to S^5$. The nontrivial part of $U^{[2]} = U \times_{S^5} U$ is $D^+ \cap D^- \simeq S^4 \simeq \mathbb{HP}^1$.

§2   A map $S^4 \to \mathrm{Sp}(1)$, written in quaternions

The frame bundle $FS^5$ is classified by a clutching map $S^4 \to \mathrm{SO}(5)$. Lift through $\mathrm{Spin}(5) \cong \mathrm{Sp}(2)$; the nontrivial class in $\pi_4(\mathrm{Sp}(2)) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ is represented by a map landing in $\mathrm{Sp}(1) \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Sp}(2)$. So we need a single smooth map

$$T : S^4 = \mathbb{HP}^1 \longrightarrow \mathrm{Sp}(1) \cong S^3, \qquad T[p;q] \;=\; \frac{2\, p\,\bar q\, i\, q\,\bar p \;-\; |p|^4 + |q|^4}{|p|^4 + |q|^4}.$$

This is the formula. Drag the quaternionic point $[p:q]$ below. Its image under $T$ is a unit quaternion — plotted as a point on $S^3$, stereographically projected into the ball you see on the right.

FIG. 2 — Left: the input $[p:q]$ as two quaternions. Right: the output $T[p:q] \in \mathrm{Sp}(1) \subset \mathbb{H}$, shown in stereographic coordinates from $-1$. Sweeping a loop in $\mathbb{HP}^1$ traces a loop in $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$ — this is the nontriviality.

"Represents the non-trivial class of $\pi_4(\mathrm{Sp}(1))$" is a statement about homotopy. The formula is a statement you can run.

§3   The truncated globular hypercover

A good cover of $S^5$ would have many open sets and many overlaps. Roberts strikes a bargain: keep $U$ small (two discs) but allow the overlap to be refined further. Cover the nontrivial part of $U^{[2]} \simeq \mathbb{HP}^1$ by the two standard quaternionic charts $H_\pm$ (where $p$ or $q$ is nonvanishing). This gives a second layer of open sets — the Greek indices from the cocycle — and a little 2-groupoid

$V^{[2]}$
⇒
$V$
⇒
$U$

called a truncated globular hypercover. Roman indices live on $U$, Greek indices live on $V$. That is all the double index meant. Hover the diagram below to see the refinement in action.

FIG. 3 — Top: $U = D^+ \sqcup D^-$ covers $S^5$. Middle: $V = H_+ \sqcup H_-$ covers the nontrivial component of $U^{[2]}$. Bottom: $V^{[2]}$, where the cocycle's K-values live. A Čech cocycle for $\mathrm{String}(3)$ is literally a 2-functor $(V^{[2]} \rightrightarrows V \rightrightarrows U) \to B\,\mathrm{String}(3)$.

§4   Lifting to paths: $T_+$ and $T_-$

The String 2-group is modelled by the crossed module

$\widehat{\Omega\mathrm{Sp}(1)}$
→
$P\mathrm{Sp}(1)$

Paths based at $1$ on the right; a central $\mathrm{U}(1)$-extension of the based loops on the left. A cocycle into $B\,\mathrm{String}(3)$ therefore needs, on each chart of $V$, a path in $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$ — not just a group element. Roberts writes them down:

$$T_+(q)(s) = \frac{|q|^4 - s^2 + 2\,q\,i\,\bar q}{|q|^4 + s^2}, \qquad T_-(p)(s) = \frac{|p|^4 s^2 - 1 + 2\,p\,i\,\bar p}{|p|^4 s^2 + 1}\cdot\Big(\tfrac{s-i}{s+i}\Big)^{\!2}.$$

Each is a smooth function of $s \in [0,1]$ tracing a path from $1$ to the value we need. Drag $s$ to watch the path unfold — and drag the chart parameter to see a whole family.

FIG. 4 — Stereographic projection of the path $T_\pm(\cdot)(s)$ in $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$. Each chart gives a path from $1$ (origin, amber dot) to an endpoint on the equator; their difference is the loop of §5.

§5   The loop $T_\Omega$ on the intersection

On $H_+ \cap H_- = \mathbb{H}^\times$ — where both charts are defined — take the difference of the two paths. The result is a based loop in $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$ depending on $q \in \mathbb{H}^\times$:

$$T_\Omega(q)(s) \;=\; \frac{(s+Q)(sQ-1)}{(s-Q)(sQ+1)} \cdot \Big(\tfrac{s-i}{s+i}\Big)^{\!2}, \qquad Q = q\,i\,\bar q.$$

The loops are what the K-valued part of the cocycle secretly is. Below, drag $q$ and watch the loop deform in $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$. Notice — the loop winds. That winding is precisely what the central extension $\widehat{\Omega\mathrm{Sp}(1)}$ was invented to remember.

FIG. 5 — The loop $s \mapsto T_\Omega(q)(s)$ in $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$, stereographically projected. The amber dot is the basepoint $1$; the curve is closed. As $q$ moves through $\mathbb{H}^\times$, the loop's homotopy class in $\pi_1(\mathrm{Sp}(1)) = 0$ is trivial — but its lift to the central extension is not.

§6   Lifting the loop: a class in a central extension

The final step is the one that actually uses the "higher" structure. We have a loop $T_\Omega(q)$ in $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$; we need to lift it through the central extension

$$\mathrm{U}(1) \longrightarrow \widehat{\Omega\mathrm{Sp}(1)} \longrightarrow \Omega\mathrm{Sp}(1).$$

Using the presentation $\widehat{\Omega\mathrm{Sp}(1)} \cong (P\Omega\mathrm{Sp}(1) \rtimes \mathrm{U}(1)) / \widetilde{\Omega^2\mathrm{Sp}(1)}$, the lift is surprisingly clean. Pick any path $q_t$ in $\mathbb{H}^\times$ from $1$ to $q$. Set

$$\widehat{T_\Omega}(q) \;=\; \big[\; T_\Omega(q_t),\; 1 \;\big] \;\in\; \widehat{\Omega\mathrm{Sp}(1)}.$$

The result is independent of the chosen path and smooth in $q$. The cocycle equations are now automatic — the globular hypercover has so few open sets that functoriality collapses to a handful of identities one can verify by hand. And that is it: an explicit, typeable, coordinate-level Čech cocycle for a nontrivial String bundle on $S^5$.

A 2-bundle you can drag, built out of a formula in quaternions, two discs, two charts, and a path in $\mathbb{H}^\times$.

§7   Beyond the 5-sphere

Roberts points out that this construction is one instance of a much more general pattern. The total space of $FS^5$ is the homogeneous bundle $\mathrm{SU}(3) \to \mathrm{SU}(3)/\mathrm{Sp}(1) = S^5$, and the String 2-group sits inside $\mathrm{String}_{\mathrm{SU}(3)}$ as a stabiliser. The same homogeneous-space trick gives explicit String 2-bundles on

  • $\mathrm{String}_{G_2} \to G_2/\mathrm{SU}(3) = S^6$
  • $\mathrm{String}_{\mathrm{Spin}(7)} \to \mathrm{Spin}(7)/G_2 = S^7$
  • $\mathrm{String}_{\mathrm{Sp}(2)} \to \mathrm{Sp}(2)/\mathrm{Sp}(1) = S^7$
  • $\mathrm{String}_{F_4} \to F_4/\mathrm{Spin}(9) = \mathbb{OP}^2$

And via the Eckmann–Hilton argument, pointwise powers $t^n$ of a generating transition function realise every class in $\pi_k(G)$. So: in principle, every String bundle on a sphere admits a Čech cocycle description as concrete as the one above.

The moral of Roberts' note — and the thing that makes it faintly radical — is that higher structures need not be opaque. With the right choice of cover, they reduce to a handful of smooth functions and a single winding number. That is how you bring categorified geometry down to Earth.
