From Hamilton's Discovery to Spin Groups, Determinants, and the Octonion Frontier

I. The Quaternion Group Q₈

In 1843, William Rowan Hamilton carved \(i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1\) into the stone of Brougham Bridge, birthing the quaternions. The eight elements \(\{1, -1, i, -i, j, -j, k, -k\}\) form a group under quaternion multiplication — the quaternion group \(Q_8\).

This is a group of order 8 where every element besides \(\pm 1\) has order 4. It is non-abelian: \(ij = k\) but \(ji = -k\). Click any cell to inspect the multiplication.

Cayley Table of Q₈
Which pairs commute? You'll find that \(\pm 1\) commute with everything (they're in the center), and that any element commutes with its own inverse and negation. But \(i, j, k\) fail to commute with each other — and the failure is always a sign flip.

II. Abelianizing Q₈

To abelianize \(Q_8\), we compute its commutator subgroup \([Q_8, Q_8]\). A commutator \([g,h] = ghg^{-1}h^{-1}\) measures non-commutativity. Let's compute them all.

Commutator Explorer
Discovery: Every commutator in \(Q_8\) is either \(1\) or \(-1\). The commutator subgroup is \([Q_8, Q_8] = \{1, -1\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\). This tiny subgroup is the center of \(Q_8\) — the elements that commute with everything.

The abelianization is the quotient \(Q_8 / \{1, -1\}\). Each element gets identified with its negation: \(i \sim -i\), \(j \sim -j\), \(k \sim -k\), and \(1 \sim -1\). Four cosets remain.

The Klein four-group \(V_4 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2\) is one of the most important small groups in mathematics. It appears as the abelianization of both \(Q_8\) and \(D_4\) — two very different non-abelian groups of order 8 that become indistinguishable after abelianization. The abelianization remembers only the "quadrant" of each quaternion unit, not its sign.

III. Unit Quaternions & SU(2)

Now we pass from the finite to the continuous. The unit quaternions — quaternions \(q = a + bi + cj + dk\) with \(|q|^2 = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2 = 1\) — form a continuous group that is topologically the 3-sphere \(S^3\). This group is isomorphic to \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\), the group of \(2 \times 2\) unitary matrices with determinant 1.

What is the abelianization of \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\)?

Why SU(2) is Perfect
Every element of SU(2) is a product of commutators. The commutator subgroup is SU(2) itself.
Theorem: \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\) is a perfect group — it equals its own commutator subgroup: \([\mathrm{SU}(2), \mathrm{SU}(2)] = \mathrm{SU}(2)\). Therefore its abelianization is trivial: \[\mathrm{SU}(2)^{\mathrm{ab}} = \{1\}\] This means every homomorphism from \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\) to an abelian group is the zero map. The group is "maximally non-abelian" — there is no abelian shadow to cast.

This is a profound fact for physics. The spin group \(\mathrm{Spin}(3) \cong \mathrm{SU}(2)\) governs quantum mechanical spin, and its perfectness means that spin has no abelian residue. You cannot reduce spin to commutative phase factors — it is irreducibly non-commutative. This is intimately connected to why spin-½ particles require a 720° rotation to return to their original state.

IV. The Full ℍ× and the Norm Map

Consider the full multiplicative group \(\mathbb{H}^\times\) of all nonzero quaternions. Every \(q \in \mathbb{H}^\times\) can be written uniquely as \(q = |q| \cdot u\) where \(|q| \in \mathbb{R}_{>0}\) is the norm and \(u \in S^3\) is a unit quaternion. This gives a decomposition:

\(\mathbb{H}^\times \;\cong\; \mathbb{R}_{>0} \times S^3\)

The norm map \(|\cdot| : \mathbb{H}^\times \to \mathbb{R}_{>0}\) is a group homomorphism (since \(|pq| = |p||q|\)), and its kernel is \(S^3 \cong \mathrm{SU}(2)\). Since \(\mathbb{R}_{>0}\) is abelian and \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\) is perfect, we get:

\([\mathbb{H}^\times, \mathbb{H}^\times] = S^3 = \ker(|\cdot|), \qquad (\mathbb{H}^\times)^{\mathrm{ab}} \cong \mathbb{R}_{>0} \cong (\mathbb{R}, +)\)
Norm as Abelianization Map

Enter a quaternion \(q = a + bi + cj + dk\) and see its image under the abelianization map \(\pi : \mathbb{H}^\times \to \mathbb{R}_{>0}\).

The abelianization discards all directional information — the unit quaternion part, which encodes 3D rotations — and retains only the scalar magnitude. The norm map is the abelianization map. This is the quaternionic analogue of the fact that for \(\mathbb{C}^\times\), the abelianization map is the identity (since \(\mathbb{C}^\times\) is already abelian).

V. Split Quaternions & GL(2, ℝ)

The split quaternions are obtained by changing the signature: instead of \(i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = -1\), we set \(i^2 = -1\) but \(j^2 = k^2 = +1\). This seemingly small change produces a radically different algebra — one isomorphic to the \(2 \times 2\) real matrix algebra \(M_2(\mathbb{R})\).

The group of invertible elements is therefore \(\mathrm{GL}(2, \mathbb{R})\) — the general linear group. Its abelianization is governed by the determinant:

\([\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{R}), \mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{R})] = \mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{R}), \qquad \mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{R})^{\mathrm{ab}} \cong \mathbb{R}^\times\)

The commutator subgroup is \(\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{R})\) — all matrices with determinant 1, the area-preserving linear transformations. The abelianization map is the determinant itself.

Beautiful parallel: For ordinary quaternions \(\mathbb{H}^\times\), the abelianization map is the norm \(|q|\). For split quaternions \(\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{R})\), it's the determinant \(\det(A)\). Both are multiplicative scalar invariants that "see" only the abelian shadow. The commutator subgroup in each case consists of the "norm-1" or "determinant-1" elements — the rigid motions, the area-preserving maps.

VI. Octonions: Beyond Groups

The octonions \(\mathbb{O}\) are the next (and last) normed division algebra after \(\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}, \mathbb{H}\). They are 8-dimensional, non-commutative, and — crucially — non-associative. This means the unit octonions \(S^7\) do not form a group under multiplication.

Obstruction: Since octonion multiplication is not associative, the unit octonions form a Moufang loop, not a group. The classical notion of commutator subgroup and abelianization does not directly apply. We must look elsewhere for analogous structures.

There are two productive ways to salvage the story:

The Automorphism Group: G₂

The automorphism group \(\mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{O}) = G_2\) is a perfectly good Lie group — the smallest of the five exceptional Lie groups. It is 14-dimensional and acts on the 7-dimensional space of imaginary octonions. \(G_2\) is simple (and hence perfect): \([G_2, G_2] = G_2\), so its abelianization is trivial, just like \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\).

Moufang Loop Abelianization

For the unit octonion Moufang loop, there is a generalized notion of "associator subloop" and quotients. The nucleus (elements that associate with everything) of the unit octonions is just \(\{\pm 1\}\), and the commutant (elements that commute with everything) is also \(\{\pm 1\}\). The loop-theoretic abelianization identifies elements whose commutators and associators are trivial — but for the octonions, essentially everything fails to associate, so the resulting quotient is quite degenerate.

VII. Split-Complex Numbers

The split-complex numbers (also called hyperbolic numbers or motor algebra) are \(\mathbb{R}[j] / (j^2 - 1)\) — they adjoin an element \(j\) with \(j^2 = +1\) instead of \(j^2 = -1\). Every split-complex number has the form \(a + bj\).

The key structural fact is that the split-complex numbers decompose via the idempotents \(e_+ = \frac{1+j}{2}\) and \(e_- = \frac{1-j}{2}\) as:

\(\mathbb{R}[j]/(j^2-1) \;\cong\; \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}\)

The invertible elements form the group \(\mathbb{R}^\times \times \mathbb{R}^\times\), which is already abelian. Therefore:

\((\mathbb{R}[j]/(j^2-1))^{\times,\;\mathrm{ab}} \;\cong\; \mathbb{R}^\times \times \mathbb{R}^\times \quad \text{(unchanged — trivial commutator subgroup)}\)
Contrast: The split-complex numbers are a fixed point of abelianization — they're already commutative and their multiplicative group is already abelian. The signature change \(j^2 = -1 \to j^2 = +1\) destroys the non-commutativity before it can even arise. Where \(\mathbb{C}^\times\) is abelian because complex multiplication is commutative, the split-complex invertible group is abelian for the same reason — but its topology is completely different (two disjoint hyperbolas instead of a circle minus a point... actually ℝ× × ℝ× has four connected components).

IX. Applications in Physics & Geometry

The abelianization of quaternionic groups isn't merely an algebraic curiosity — it has direct physical and geometric meaning. The pattern that emerges is: the abelianization map extracts a scalar invariant, while the commutator subgroup carries the "rotational" or "gauge" content.

The Abelianization–Physics Dictionary

Why Perfectness Matters

The fact that \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\) is perfect — and hence has trivial abelianization — has a direct physical consequence. In quantum mechanics, a global phase \(e^{i\theta}\) is unobservable (it acts trivially on the projective Hilbert space). The abelianization of the symmetry group determines which global "charges" (abelian quantum numbers) can be extracted. Since \(\mathrm{SU}(2)^{\mathrm{ab}}\) is trivial, there is no abelian charge associated with spin — spin is purely non-abelian.

Contrast this with \(\mathrm{U}(1)\), which is its own abelianization. The abelian charge here is electric charge itself. And for \(\mathrm{U}(2) = \mathrm{U}(1) \times_{\mathbb{Z}/2} \mathrm{SU}(2)\), the abelianization is \(\mathrm{U}(1)\) — only the electric charge survives; the weak isospin (the \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\) part) is killed.

Determinant in General Relativity

The abelianization \(\mathrm{GL}(n, \mathbb{R})^{\mathrm{ab}} \cong \mathbb{R}^\times\) via the determinant plays a fundamental role in differential geometry. The determinant of the Jacobian measures volume distortion; the commutator subgroup \(\mathrm{SL}(n, \mathbb{R})\) preserves volume. The abelianization extracts exactly the "volume-scaling factor" from a general linear transformation — this is why the metric determinant \(\sqrt{|g|}\) appears everywhere in general relativity.

G₂ Holonomy and M-Theory

The exceptional Lie group \(G_2 = \mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{O})\) is perfect, so \(G_2^{\mathrm{ab}}\) is trivial. In M-theory (11-dimensional supergravity), compactification on 7-dimensional manifolds with \(G_2\) holonomy yields \(\mathcal{N}=1\) supersymmetry in 4 dimensions. The perfectness of \(G_2\) ensures there are no abelian moduli from the holonomy alone — the moduli space geometry is entirely governed by the non-abelian structure of the exceptional group.

X. Coda

We've traced a single algebraic operation — abelianization — across a landscape that stretches from Hamilton's bridge carving to string theory. The recurring theme is a factorization:

\(\text{Full group} \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{scalar invariant}\;}\; \text{Abelian shadow} \qquad \text{with kernel} = \text{rigid/gauge part}\)

For \(\mathbb{H}^\times\), the scalar invariant is the norm. For \(\mathrm{GL}(n)\), it's the determinant. For \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\) and \(G_2\), the abelian shadow is trivial — they are entirely rigid, entirely non-abelian, and this is precisely why they govern the most irreducible physical symmetries: spin and exceptional holonomy.

The octonions sit at the boundary of this story, where the group-theoretic framework itself dissolves into loop theory and exceptional structures. But even there, the abelianization principle persists in a generalized form: the quest to separate the commutative from the non-commutative, the scalar from the rotational, the charge from the gauge.

Hamilton would have been astonished to see how far his bridge carving would reach.
