A Knot on a Donut

Take a torus — the mathematical name for a donut shape. Now imagine drawing a closed curve on its surface, one that winds around the hole of the torus some number of times while simultaneously looping around the tube itself. If the curve closes up perfectly to form a single loop, you have a torus knot.

Every torus knot is described by two integers, p and q. The parameter p counts how many times the curve wraps around the axis of symmetry (the long way around the donut), while q counts how many times it passes through the hole (the short way around the tube). For the curve to form a true knot — a single connected loop — these two numbers must be coprime, meaning they share no common factor other than 1.

If p and q do share a common factor d, the result isn't a single knotted loop but rather d separate interlinked loops — a torus link with d components.

The Fundamental Domain

There is a beautiful way to see the structure of a torus knot. A torus can be "unrolled" into a flat rectangle by cutting along two circles. On this rectangle, the knot becomes a collection of straight, parallel line segments. Glueing the edges back together — top to bottom, left to right — recovers the torus and the line segments reconnect into the knot.

When p and q Share a Factor

If gcd(p,q) = d > 1, the parametric curve doesn't form a single loop. Instead, it produces d separate, interlinked curves — a torus link. Each component is itself a T(p/d, q/d) torus knot (or unknot), and the d components are braided together symmetrically around the torus.

Try setting both sliders to even numbers — say p = 4, q = 6 — and watch the 3D view. You'll see two separate loops intertwined, because gcd(4,6) = 2. The visualization renders each component in a different color.

Why Torus Knots Matter

Torus knots appear throughout mathematics, physics, and biology. They arise naturally as the links of isolated singularities of complex curves, connecting knot theory to algebraic geometry. In physics, knotted field configurations — including knotted light beams and knotted vortices in fluids — often take the form of torus knots.

In molecular biology, DNA can form knotted configurations inside viral capsids, and torus knots are among the most commonly observed DNA knot types. Understanding their geometry helps researchers analyze the three-dimensional structure of genetic material.

Torus knots also played a central role in one of knot theory's landmark results: the proof of the Milnor conjecture by Kronheimer and Mrowka, which established that the unknotting number of a T(p,q) torus knot equals (p−1)(q−1)/2 — exactly the genus of the knot's minimal Seifert surface.

The Double Helix Connection

From torus knots to DNA — torsion, supercoiling, and filling space with helices

The Helix: A Torus Knot Unrolled

A circular helix — the simplest space curve with constant curvature and constant torsion — is intimately related to torus knots. Consider a torus knot T(1,q): it wraps once around the hole and q times around the tube. If we imagine stretching the torus into a long, thin cylinder by increasing the major radius R toward infinity, this (1,q) curve becomes a helix with q turns.

Conversely, every helix can be thought of as the "unrolled" limit of a torus knot. The parametric equations converge: as R → ∞ while r stays fixed, the torus knot curve approaches a helix with radius r and pitch determined by q.

DNA: A Torus Link in Nature

Watson and Crick's double helix is, topologically, a T(2, n) torus link — two helical strands winding around a common axis, interlinked n times where n depends on the length of the molecule. In B-form DNA, the helix makes one full turn every 10.5 base pairs, so a circular DNA molecule of N base pairs has a linking number Lk ≈ N/10.5.

Each strand traces a helix on the surface of an imaginary torus whose axis follows the DNA centerline. The two strands are the two components of the link, just as our T(2,q) visualization shows two intertwined curves when gcd = 2.

Curvature & Torsion Along the Curve

The Frenet–Serret frame assigns three orthonormal vectors to each point of a space curve: the tangent T, the normal N, and the binormal B. As the frame travels along the curve, its rotation is governed by two scalar functions: the curvature κ (how sharply the curve bends) and the torsion τ (how much it twists out of its osculating plane).

For a helix, both κ and τ are constant — the frame rotates uniformly. For a torus knot, they oscillate periodically. The pattern of these oscillations encodes the curve's topology.

Cable Knots: Helices of Helices

A cable knot C(K; p,q) is constructed by taking a knot K and replacing it with a T(p,q) torus knot drawn in a thin tubular neighborhood of K. If K is itself a torus knot, the result is an iterated torus knot — a helix wound around a helix.

This is exactly what nature does with DNA. The double helix (level 0) wraps around histone proteins to form nucleosomes (level 1). The nucleosome chain coils into a 30nm solenoid (level 2). Each successive level of coiling is a cable construction — a helix whose axis is itself a helix.

Cable Knot Construction

At depth 0, you see a simple circle — the unknot. At depth 1, the circle becomes a helix: a torus knot wound around the original path. At depth 2, that helix becomes the axis for a finer helix wound around it. At depth 3, we get a helix-of-a-helix-of-a-helix — and the curve begins to fill an ever-larger volume of the space around the original circle.

Each level of cabling multiplies the total length of curve contained within a bounded region, and the ratio of curve length to enclosing volume grows at each iteration. In the limit, the iterated construction approaches a space-filling curve — a 1-dimensional object whose image has Hausdorff dimension 3.

Twist, Writhe, and Linking Number

For a closed ribbon — like the two strands of circular DNA — the linking number Lk counts how many times one strand winds around the other. White's theorem (Călugăreanu–White–Fuller) decomposes this topological invariant into two geometric quantities:

The twist Tw measures the helical winding of the ribbon about its own centerline. The writhe Wr measures the coiling or crossing of the centerline itself. The key insight: Lk is fixed (a topological invariant that cannot change without cutting), but twist and writhe can freely interconvert.

This is the fundamental constraint governing DNA topology. A circular DNA molecule has a fixed linking number set at the time the circle is closed. When the molecule is relaxed, nearly all the linking appears as twist (the normal helical winding). When the molecule is supercoiled — wound tighter or looser than its relaxed state — some twist converts to writhe. Negative supercoiling (underwinding) produces the compact, right-handed interwound structures seen in bacterial plasmids.

Topoisomerases control this balance. Type I topoisomerases change Lk by ±1 (cutting one strand). Type II topoisomerases change Lk by ±2 (passing one double helix through another). Both enable the cell to regulate twist and writhe independently — essential for replication, transcription, and compaction.

Filling Space with Torsion

The human genome contains approximately 6 billion base pairs — about 2 meters of DNA — packed into a nucleus roughly 6 μm across. This 300,000-fold compaction is achieved through iterated helical winding: each level of organization is a helix whose axis follows the helix of the previous level, exactly like the cable knot construction above.

Chromatin Packing Hierarchy
The B-form double helix: two right-handed helical strands with 3.4 nm pitch, 2 nm diameter.

At each level, the same geometric principle operates: a 1-dimensional curve (the previous-level fiber) is wound around a new axis, sweeping out a toroidal neighborhood. The double helix wraps around histone octamers 1.65 times — a left-handed T(1,−2) cable. The nucleosome chain coils into a solenoid — roughly 6 nucleosomes per helical turn. These solenoid fibers form loops anchored to a protein scaffold, and the loops themselves coil and fold further.

The result is a structure whose topology is an iterated torus knot — a cable of a cable of a cable — where each level of cabling contributes additional torsion and compaction. At each stage, the effective dimension of the curve increases. A single helix, while still 1-dimensional, sweeps out an area proportional to its length. The helix-of-helix sweeps out a volume. The iterated construction produces a dense, space-filling arrangement — a 1-dimensional curve that effectively fills three-dimensional space.

Recent Hi-C chromosome conformation data suggests that chromatin at the megabase scale organizes as a fractal globule — a knot-free, maximally dense packing that preserves the ability to unfold any local region. This is precisely the topology one obtains from iterated torus-knot cabling: dense, self-similar, and unknotted at every scale.

And so the torus knot — a simple curve on the surface of a donut — turns out to be the fundamental motif of life's most essential molecule. From the helical winding of Watson and Crick's double helix, through the supercoiled topology managed by topoisomerases, to the hierarchical cable-knot architecture of the chromosome, torsion is the recurring theme: a one-dimensional thread twisted, wound, and wound again, until it fills the three-dimensional world.
