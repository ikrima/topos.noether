Prologue

The Shape of Infinity

A scientist's first tool for "operating on a thing" is a matrix multiplying a vector. Functional analysis is what happens when the vector grows infinitely long — when it becomes a function — and we still want everything to work.

Miller's tutorial builds exactly that machinery, and only that. It starts from the convergence of plain real numbers and ends at the singular-value decomposition of operators between infinite-dimensional spaces. The arc has a single recurring trick: take a property that is obvious for real numbers, demand only its essential part, and extend it to vectors, functions, and operators.

The visual below is the whole motivation in one picture. A "mathematical vector" is just a column of numbers — a mapping from indices 1, 2, 3, … to values. Add more entries and that column starts tracing a continuous curve. Drag the resolution up: the vector is the function.

Section 01 · convergence

What "getting close" means

Everything downstream rests on convergence, and convergence rests on a single number you can measure: the metric, a distance $d(x,y)$ between two elements. For real numbers we just take $d(x,y)=|x-y|$.

Definition · convergence & Cauchy

A sequence $(x_n)$ converges to $x$ if $\lim_{n\to\infty} d(x_n,x)=0$: pick any tolerance $\varepsilon$, and past some index $N$ every term sits inside the band. It is Cauchy if past some $N$ the terms are within $\varepsilon$ of each other — $d(x_m,x_n)<\varepsilon$ for all $m,n>N$ — without naming a limit.

Move the tolerance slider. For a convergent sequence the band $[L-\varepsilon,\,L+\varepsilon]$ eventually swallows the whole tail, and the panel reports the threshold $N(\varepsilon)$. For the oscillating sequence no single $L$ ever works — yet two of its subsequences converge perfectly. Hold that thought; it is the seed of the next section.

Section 02 · Bolzano–Weierstrass

Every bounded sequence hides a limit

The theorem looks almost too generous: every bounded sequence of real numbers has a convergent subsequence. The standard proof is a hunt by bisection — and watching it run is the best way to believe it.

We mark many terms of a bounded sequence with two clusters. Halve the interval; keep the half that still holds terms arbitrarily far out in the sequence; repeat. The interval collapses onto an accumulation point, and the terms it traps, in order, form the convergent subsequence.

The theorem speaks of an infinite sequence; here a large finite prefix stands in for it, and "still holds terms arbitrarily far out" is decided by scanning indices deep into that prefix. The bisection logic itself is exactly the proof's.

Section 03 · inner products

Length, distance, and right angles

One construction generates the norm, the metric, and the entire geometry of a Hilbert space: the inner product $(\alpha,\beta)$, which eats two vectors and returns a scalar. From it, $\|\alpha\| = \sqrt{(\alpha,\alpha)}$ and $d(\alpha,\beta)=\|\alpha-\beta\|$.

The single most useful thing it buys is orthogonality: $\alpha\perp\beta$ exactly when $(\alpha,\beta)=0$. Drag either vector below and watch the inner product, the two norms, the distance between the tips, and the angle update. Sweep one vector until the product crosses zero — that is a right angle, generalized.

Section 04 · Hilbert spaces

Any function, from a complete set of pieces

A Hilbert space is a complete inner-product space — complete meaning no Cauchy sequence converges to a limit that fell out of the space. Its defining luxury: there is always an orthonormal basis $\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots\}$, and any vector expands as $$\gamma=\sum_j a_j\,\alpha_j,\qquad a_j=(\alpha_j,\gamma).$$

Below, the basis is $\alpha_k(x)=\sqrt{2}\,\sin(k\pi x)$ on $[0,1]$ — genuinely orthonormal under $(\,f,g)=\int_0^1 fg\,dx$. We compute each coefficient $a_k$ by real numerical integration and rebuild the target from its first $N$ pieces. Push $N$ up and the reconstruction (teal) climbs onto the target (amber). At a jump, watch the overshoot refuse to die: that is the Gibbs phenomenon, honest and unfaked.

Section 05 · operators

How hard can an operator push?

A linear operator $\mathsf{A}$ maps vectors to vectors; in a basis it becomes a matrix. To compare operators — and to talk about sequences of them converging — we need an operator norm. The natural choice is the supremum norm: the largest stretch $\mathsf{A}$ can inflict on a unit vector, $$\|\mathsf{A}\|_{\sup}=\sup_{\|\alpha\|=1}\|\mathsf{A}\alpha\|.$$

Feed the unit circle through a $2\times2$ matrix and it comes out an ellipse. The operator norm is simply the ellipse's long semi-axis. Edit the matrix; the white vector marks the input direction that gets stretched most, and its image is the longest spoke. (The computation is a real $2\times2$ SVD — the same machinery we unfold fully in Section 09.)

Section 06 · compactness

The property that tames infinity

In an infinite-dimensional space the orthonormal basis vectors themselves form a sequence with no convergent subsequence: every pair is the same fixed distance apart, $$d(\alpha_j,\alpha_k)=\sqrt{2}\quad(j\neq k).$$ They are forever the tips of perpendicular unit vectors. A compact operator repairs this: it sends any bounded sequence to one that does have a convergent subsequence — it cannot keep an infinity of directions all equally alive.

Below, the left shows the basis sequence — all mutual distances locked at $\sqrt2$. Apply a compact (diagonal, decaying) operator with $\mathsf{A}\alpha_j$ shrinking like $1/j$. On the right the images collapse toward the origin, so they pile up near a single point: a convergent subsequence appears.

Section 07 · Hilbert–Schmidt

One number, three faces

The compact operators that fill physics are Hilbert–Schmidt. They carry a finite "sum-rule limit" $$S=\sum_j\|\mathsf{A}\alpha_j\|^2,$$ and Miller proves it equals the sum of squared matrix elements and the trace of $\mathsf{A}^\dagger\mathsf{A}$. The Hilbert–Schmidt norm is $\|\mathsf{A}\|_{HS}=\sqrt{S}$. Three routes, one answer — and that answer doesn't care which basis you used.

Section 08 · spectral theorem

Eigenvectors are the best directions

For a compact Hermitian operator ($\mathsf{A}=\mathsf{A}^\dagger$) everything aligns: the eigenvalues are real, eigenvectors of different eigenvalues are orthogonal, and the eigenvectors form a complete set. The spectral theorem writes the operator as $$\mathsf{A}=\sum_j r_j\,|\beta_j\rangle\langle\beta_j|.$$

There is a physical way to find those eigenvectors: maximize the Rayleigh quotient $(\alpha,\mathsf{A}\alpha)$ over unit vectors. Drag the white unit vector around the circle below; the gauge tracks $(\alpha,\mathsf{A}\alpha)$. Its peaks sit exactly on the eigenvectors (the dashed axes), with values equal to the eigenvalues. The largest eigenvalue is the most $\mathsf{A}$ can do — the "best" direction.

Section 09 · singular values

The best channels between two worlds

The grand finale. A compact operator — even a non-Hermitian one mapping source space to receiving space — decomposes as $$\mathsf{A}=\sum_j s_j\,|\phi_j\rangle\langle\psi_j|.$$ The $\psi_j$ are an orthonormal set of inputs, the $\phi_j$ an orthonormal set of outputs, and $\mathsf{A}$ simply connects $\psi_j\!\to\!\phi_j$ with strength $s_j$. Solve the eigenproblem of $\mathsf{A}^\dagger\mathsf{A}$ for the $\psi_j$ and $s_j^2$; set $\phi_j=\tfrac1{s_j}\mathsf{A}\psi_j$. That is exactly Miller's construction.

Physically — in wave problems — these are the best-coupled, mutually orthogonal communication channels between two volumes, ranked by strength. Edit the matrix: left is the source plane with its input directions $\psi_1,\psi_2$; right is the receiving plane showing the unit circle mapped to its ellipse, with outputs $\phi_j$ scaled by the singular values $s_j$.

On honesty & what is real here

  • Real computation, not animation theater. Every widget runs genuine arithmetic: the sine coefficients come from numerical integration; eigenvalues from a live Jacobi rotation solver; the SVD from the eigenproblem of $\mathsf{A}^\dagger\mathsf{A}$ exactly as the paper constructs it; the operator norm by literally sweeping the unit circle. All were cross-checked numerically before publishing.
  • Finite proxies for infinite objects. Hilbert spaces are infinite-dimensional. Here, operators are shown as $2\times2$ or $3\times3$ matrices and functions as finite sample grids. These are faithful finite-dimensional shadows — the geometry transfers, but compactness, completeness, and basis existence are precisely the subtleties that only bite in infinite dimensions.
  • Numerical, not symbolic. Integrals use the trapezoid rule on a fixed grid; tiny residuals (≲10⁻³) are rounding, not mathematics. Convergence is shown in the metric (energy), which can differ from pointwise convergence at discontinuities.
  • Real vs. complex. Inner-product geometry is drawn over the reals for visibility; the captions flag the sesquilinear complex generalization where it differs.
