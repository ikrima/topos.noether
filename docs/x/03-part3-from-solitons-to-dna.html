From Solitons to DNA

Discrete elastic rods, supercoiling,
and the unified architecture of curve dynamics

Parts 1 and 2 built the continuous theory: curvature and frames, then the Hasimoto transform revealing NLS solitons inside moving filaments. That mathematics is beautiful but infinite-dimensional — it lives on smooth curves parameterized by a continuous arc length.

To simulate a curve on a computer, we need to discretize. But naively replacing derivatives with finite differences destroys the geometric structure that makes everything work. The breakthrough of Bergou, Wardetzky, Robinson, Audoly, and Grinspun (SIGGRAPH 2008) was to realize: don't discretize the equations — discretize the geometry, then derive the equations.

The result is a discrete elastic rod that preserves the exact relationship between Bishop frames, twist, writhe, and elastic energy — and runs at 60 frames per second.

Discretizing the Geometry

A smooth curve γ(s) becomes a polygonal chain: vertices x₀, x₁, ..., xₙ connected by edges eⁱ = xᵢ₊₁ − xᵢ. Tangent vectors tⁱ = eⁱ/|eⁱ| live on edges, while curvature lives on vertices — at each vertex, we measure the turning angle between consecutive edges.

The discrete curvature binormal at vertex i is:

(κb)ᵢ = 2(eⁱ⁻¹ × eⁱ) / (|eⁱ⁻¹||eⁱ| + eⁱ⁻¹ · eⁱ)

This specific formula — with the 2 tan(φ/2) normalization rather than sin φ or φ itself — isn't arbitrary. It emerges from the holonomy analysis and has the right divergence behavior: as consecutive edges fold onto each other, the curvature goes to infinity, penalizing kinks.

The discrete Bishop frame propagates by rotating each uⁱ from one edge to the next about the curvature binormal — exactly the minimal rotation that maps one tangent to the next. And the material frame angle θⁱ is still a single scalar per edge, with twist given by the simple difference mᵢ = θⁱ − θⁱ⁻¹.

The quasistatic trick. Twist waves in thin rods travel much faster than bending waves — effectively infinitely fast in the thin-rod limit. This means that at each timestep, the twist distribution equilibrates instantly. Mathematically, the material frame angles θⁱ minimize the energy for the current centerline. For isotropic rods, this gives uniform twist. For the general case, it requires solving a tridiagonal linear system — O(n) per timestep.

Crank up the end twist and watch what happens. At low twist, the rod just rotates smoothly about its axis. But past a critical threshold — the buckling instability — the rod suddenly coils on itself, converting twist into writhe. This is Călugăreanu's theorem in action: the linking number is conserved, so twist lost at the ends reappears as spatial coiling.

This is exactly what DNA does.

DNA Supercoiling

A DNA molecule is a thin elastic rod. The double helix has a natural twist of about 10.5 base pairs per turn. Enzymes called topoisomerases can change the linking number by cutting one strand, passing the other through, and resealing — effectively adding or removing whole turns of twist.

When the linking number differs from the relaxed value, the DNA is said to be supercoiled. Most bacterial DNA is negatively supercoiled (underwound), which helps with strand separation during replication. The excess linking deficit distributes between twist and writhe according to the elastic properties of the double helix.

The ratio of twist to writhe at equilibrium depends on the relative bending and torsional stiffness. For DNA, the persistence length (bending) is about 50 nm and the torsional stiffness gives a characteristic ratio. At moderate supercoiling, most of the excess goes into writhe — forming the plectonemic (interwound) structures visible under electron microscopy.

The Grand Architecture

Here is the deepest insight of this entire series: the same discrete Bishop frame that Bergou uses for physical simulation is exactly the spatial part of the AKNS Lax pair at spectral parameter λ = 0. The discrete parallel transport chain that propagates the Bishop frame from edge to edge is the scattering problem of integrable systems theory.

This means the data structure that gives us efficient O(n) simulation also gives us access to the full integrable hierarchy — solitons, spectral curves, conserved quantities, and all. The unified architecture looks like this:

This is the full picture. The 3D filament, the curvature and twist plots, and the Hasimoto wavefunction all move in lockstep. The NLS soliton is a localized helical kink traveling without distortion. The breather is a bound state — two solitons orbiting each other, creating a pulsating curvature bump. The helix is the simplest stationary solution — constant curvature and torsion, no dynamics at all.

Each of these solutions corresponds to specific spectral data: eigenvalues in the upper half of the complex ζ-plane. Solitons are isolated eigenvalues. Finite-gap solutions are spectral curves. The geometry of the curve is completely encoded in the scattering data, and vice versa.

What We've Built

Across these three parts, we've followed a single thread from the simplest possible question — "how much does a curve bend?" — through a web of increasingly profound mathematical connections. Curvature led to frames. Frames led to twist and writhe. The topology of ribbons gave us Călugăreanu's theorem. Letting curves move revealed the vortex filament equation. The Hasimoto transform showed that this equation is the nonlinear Schrödinger equation in disguise. And the discrete theory unified simulation with integrable systems through a single shared structure: the Bishop frame.

The central miracle. The discrete parallel transport that makes rod simulation efficient at O(n) per frame is the same mathematical object as the Lax pair of integrable systems theory. This isn't a coincidence — it's the geometric content of integrability. The Bishop frame is the scattering problem. The curvature and torsion are the potential. The spectral parameter deforms geometry into spectroscopy.

From phone cords to vortex rings, from DNA supercoiling to rogue ocean waves, the geometry of elastic curves connects some of the most beautiful mathematics of the past three centuries. What Euler began with the elastica, what Da Rios discovered for vortex filaments, what Hasimoto unified through his transform, and what Bergou made computable — it's all one story, told in the language of moving frames on curves.
