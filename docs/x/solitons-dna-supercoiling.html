§1 The Framed Space Curve

Model the DNA centerline as a smooth, arclength-parameterized curve $\gamma : [0,L] \to \mathbb{R}^3$ with $|\gamma'(s)|=1$. At each point, attach an orthonormal frame — the material frame — representing the cross-section orientation. The configuration lives in the special Euclidean group $\mathrm{SE}(3) = \mathbb{R}^3 \rtimes \mathrm{SO}(3)$.

The frame evolves along the rod via the Darboux vector $\boldsymbol{\kappa} = (\kappa_1, \kappa_2, \tau)$, where $\kappa_1, \kappa_2$ are bending curvatures and $\tau$ is the twist rate. The elastic energy is

$E[\gamma, \Phi] = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^L \bigl[ A(\kappa_1^2 + \kappa_2^2) + C\tau^2 \bigr]\,ds$

For DNA, $A \approx 50\,k_BT\!\cdot\!\mathrm{nm}$ (bending) and $C \approx 75\,k_BT\!\cdot\!\mathrm{nm}$ (torsion), giving the important ratio $A/C \approx 2/3$.

Three Framings Compared

The choice of frame matters enormously for both theory and computation. The Frenet frame fails at inflection points. The Bishop frame (parallel transport) is everywhere smooth, and the material frame is the physical DNA cross-section, related to Bishop by a twist angle $\theta(s)$.

§2 Călugăreanu–White–Fuller: Topology Constraining Geometry

Model the double helix as a ribbon: a curve $\gamma$ plus a normal $\mathbf{u}$. For closed (circular) DNA, three quantities are locked together:

$\mathrm{Lk} = \mathrm{Tw} + \mathrm{Wr}$

Linking number (Lk) is a topological invariant — it can only change when a strand is cut (topoisomerase action). Twist (Tw) measures helical winding; Writhe (Wr) measures visible supercoiling. When enzymes change Lk, the change partitions: about 75% goes into writhe, 25% into twist.

§3 Kirchhoff's Elastic Rod ≅ Euler's Spinning Top

Kirchhoff's great insight: the equilibrium equations for an elastic rod have exactly the same mathematical structure as Euler's equations for a spinning top under gravity. Arclength plays the role of time.

The top solutions map directly to rod shapes: precession → helix, large nutation → plectonemic supercoil, and the separatrix (homoclinic orbit) — the orbit connecting the unstable equilibrium to itself — is the soliton: a single localized kink.

§4 The Hasimoto Transform

Hasimoto's 1972 construction compresses the complete intrinsic geometry of a space curve into a single complex function:

$\psi(s) = \kappa(s)\,\exp\!\Bigl(i\!\int_0^s\!\tau(s')\,ds'\Bigr)$

The modulus $|\psi| = \kappa$ encodes bending; the phase $\arg\psi = \int\tau\,ds$ encodes accumulated torsion. Under the vortex filament equation, $\psi$ satisfies the cubic focusing nonlinear Schrödinger equation:

$i\psi_t + \psi_{ss} + \tfrac{1}{2}|\psi|^2\psi = 0$

The 1-soliton solution is the centerpiece of the entire construction:

$\psi_\mathrm{sol}(s) = 2\eta\,\mathrm{sech}\bigl(2\eta(s-s_0)\bigr)\,e^{i(2\xi s + \phi_0)}$

Four parameters control everything: $\eta$ (amplitude/width), $\xi$ (torsion/pitch), $s_0$ (position), $\phi_0$ (phase). The visualization below lets you manipulate them in real time and see the full reconstruction pipeline in action.

§5 The Lax Pair: Gauge Theory Enters

The NLS admits a Lax pair — two matrix equations (the AKNS system) whose compatibility condition is the NLS. The spatial part

$\partial_s\Psi = U\Psi, \quad U = \begin{pmatrix} -i\zeta & \psi \\ -\bar\psi & i\zeta \end{pmatrix}$

defines a flat $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ connection parameterized by the spectral parameter $\zeta$. At $\zeta = 0$, this is the Bishop frame equation in the $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ double cover. The spectral parameter continuously deforms the frame into a scattering problem.

The Hasimoto transform is revealed as a gauge transformation between the NLS and Heisenberg ferromagnet Lax pairs — Lakshmanan's 1977 observation. A soliton has three simultaneous incarnations: a pulse in $|\psi|^2$ (NLS), a spin wave in $\mathbf{S}$ (Heisenberg), and a helical kink on $\gamma$ (geometry).

§6 Inverse Scattering & Soliton Solutions

The miracle of the Inverse Scattering Transform: under NLS flow, the scattering data evolves linearly. Eigenvalues are time-independent (soliton parameters are conserved); everything else just picks up a phase. The nonlinearity is exactly linearized.

The 1-soliton gives $\kappa(s) = 2\eta\,\mathrm{sech}(2\eta(s - vt))$ — a localized bump of curvature traveling without distortion — and constant torsion $\tau = 2\xi$. On the filament, this is a straight line developing a single traveling helical loop.

Multi-soliton solutions ($N$ eigenvalues) produce $N$ helical kinks that scatter elastically — emerging from collisions with identical shapes but acquired phase shifts.

§7 Spectral Curves & Theta Functions

For periodic solutions (closed DNA), the spectral data lives on a Riemann surface of genus $g$. Each genus level adds topological complexity:

Genus 0

Plane wave → Helix

Uniform curvature and torsion

Genus 1

Elliptic → Euler Elastica

Undulating, looping, soliton limit

Genus 2

Hyperelliptic → Supercoils

Branched plectonemic structures

Calini and Ivey proved that the knot type of a finite-gap filament is determined by the spectral curve. Each "unpinching" of a double point (increasing genus by 1) corresponds to a cabling operation on the knot. The spectral curve is a control panel for curve topology.

§8 Knot Solitons & DNA Free Energy

Decomposing the tangent vector via $\phi$-mapping topological current theory reveals that the DNA free-energy density has the same structure as a two-condensate (two-gap) superconductor. Such systems support Faddeev–Niemi knot solitons — topologically stable torus knots $T(p,q)$ classified by the Hopf invariant:

$\text{Hopf invariant} = \mathrm{Lk} = \mathrm{Tw} + \mathrm{Wr}$

The torus knot $T(p,q)$ has linking number $pq$, connecting soliton topology directly to the Călugăreanu constraint on DNA.
