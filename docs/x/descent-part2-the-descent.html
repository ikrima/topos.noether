The Descent

Cocycles, Reconstruction, and the Sheaf That Refuses
A NeRF is literally a sheaf that refuses to descend. It stores the functor $T \mapsto (\text{color}, \text{density})$ — the complete set of observations from all probes — without ever constructing a mesh, a point cloud, or any geometric object on the base. The “3D scene” in a NeRF is never an object in any category. It is the coherent family of views. The sheaf is the thing. The base object is optional. — after McLarty

In Part I we built the cover: cameras as morphisms $U_i \to X$, projections as pullbacks, fibers as depth rays, overlapping views as the fiber product $U_i \times_X U_j$, and feature correspondences as the descent datum $\varphi_{ij}$. Everything was local — pairs of views, pairs of pixels.

Now we ask the question that makes descent non-trivial: when do these local data cohere globally? When does the patchwork of pairwise correspondences assemble into a single, consistent 3D model? And what happens when it doesn't?

Section 01

The Cocycle Condition

Suppose point $A$ in view $i$ matches point $B$ in view $j$, and point $B$ in view $j$ matches point $C$ in view $k$. Then point $A$ in view $i$ had better match point $C$ in view $k$ directly. If it doesn't — if the triangle of correspondences doesn't close — you have an inconsistency.

This is the cocycle condition:

$$\varphi_{jk} \circ \varphi_{ij} = \varphi_{ik} \quad \text{on } U_i \times_X U_j \times_X U_k$$

In photogrammetry, the descent datum $\varphi_{ij}$ is the relative camera pose — encoded as the essential matrix $E_{ij} \in \text{SE}(3)$. The cocycle condition says: the relative pose from camera $i$ to camera $k$ via camera $j$ must equal the direct relative pose from $i$ to $k$.

The trifocal tensor — the three-view analogue of the fundamental matrix — is literally the cocycle on the triple overlap $U_i \times_X U_j \times_X U_k$. It exists precisely because three views of the same scene must satisfy a compatibility condition that goes beyond pairwise consistency.

The Trifocal Tensor
The trifocal tensor $T_{ijk}$ is a $3 \times 3 \times 3$ array encoding the geometric relations among three views. It can be expressed in terms of the camera matrices as $T_i^{jk} = a_i b_j^{(4)} c_k - a_i^{(4)} b_j c_k$ (schematically). Its vanishing conditions are precisely the cocycle relations $\varphi_{jk} \circ \varphi_{ij} = \varphi_{ik}$.
Section 02

Bundle Adjustment: Enforcing the Cocycle

Real measurements are noisy. Feature detectors mislocalize. Camera poses carry estimation error. The cocycle condition $\varphi_{jk} \circ \varphi_{ij} = \varphi_{ik}$ will never hold exactly. So we do the next best thing: we minimize the failure.

Bundle adjustment is a global nonlinear least-squares optimization that simultaneously refines all camera poses and all 3D point positions to minimize the total reprojection error. It is the engineering name for "enforce the cocycle condition as closely as the data allow."

$$\min_{\{R_i, t_i\}, \{X_j\}} \sum_{i,j} \left\| \pi_i(X_j) - x_{ij} \right\|^2$$

Here $\pi_i(X_j)$ is the predicted projection of 3D point $X_j$ through camera $i$, and $x_{ij}$ is the observed pixel. The residual $\| \pi_i(X_j) - x_{ij} \|$ is the reprojection error for that observation.

Section 03

The Obstruction Class: Reprojection Error as $H^1$

When descent fails — when the cocycle condition cannot be satisfied — the failure is measured by a cohomology class. In algebraic geometry, the obstruction to effective descent lives in $H^1(Y/X, G)$ where $G$ is the structure group.

In photogrammetry, this obstruction is visible. It is the reprojection error — the red lines connecting where a 3D point should project to where it actually appears in the image. These red lines are the obstruction class made geometric.

When Descent Fails
Descent fails in photogrammetry when: the scene has insufficient texture (no features to match — the cover doesn't distinguish points), there is insufficient overlap (the cover isn't surjective), or there are degenerate configurations (all cameras are collinear, or the scene is planar). These are precisely the conditions that make the cover fail to be faithfully flat, or that make $H^1$ non-trivial.
Section 04

Effective Descent: 3D Reconstruction

When the cocycle condition is satisfied — when bundle adjustment converges to small residuals — we can descend. From the coherent family of 2D projections with their correspondences, we recover the 3D object on $X$.

This is effective descent: the theorem that says if your descent data satisfy the cocycle condition, there exists a unique (up to isomorphism) object on the base whose pullbacks to the cover are isomorphic to what you started with. In photogrammetry: there exists a 3D point cloud whose projections through the calibrated cameras reproduce the observed images.

Section 05

The Gauge Ambiguity: $\text{SE}(3)$ and Ground Control

The reconstructed 3D model is determined only up to a global similarity transformation: rotation, translation, and uniform scale. You can rotate, shift, and resize the entire point cloud and all camera poses simultaneously, and every reprojection error remains unchanged.

This is the gauge ambiguity. In descent theory, the descended object is unique up to the action of the structure group on the base. In photogrammetry, the structure group is $\text{Sim}(3) = \mathbb{R}^+ \times \text{SE}(3)$ — scale, rotation, translation. The reconstruction lives in a quotient.

Section 06

The Sheaf That Refuses to Descend

McLarty's observation is the one that should stop you cold.

A NeRF (Neural Radiance Field) does not reconstruct a mesh. It does not produce a point cloud. It learns a function $F: (\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{d}) \mapsto (\mathbf{c}, \sigma)$ that takes a 3D position $\mathbf{x}$ and viewing direction $\mathbf{d}$ and returns a color $\mathbf{c}$ and density $\sigma$. It literally encodes the functor of views — the map from probes to observations — without ever materializing the "object" as a geometric entity.

A NeRF is the sheaf. It is descent without bothering to descend. It stays at the level of the cover and says: the coherent family of views is all there is. No base object needed.

The Yoneda Perspective
The 3D scene plays the role of the representable functor $h_X$. Each camera view is an element of $h_X(T_i) = \text{Hom}(T_i, X)$ — a probe. The Yoneda lemma says: the scene IS the functor $T \mapsto \{\text{views from } T\}$. There is no scene "behind" the views. The scene is the name we give to the coherent totality of all possible views. And reconstruction is not "finding" a hidden object. It is constructing the object that the views collectively define. The NeRF makes this explicit: it is the functor, without bothering to find a representing object.

This is the topos-theoretic endpoint: you don't need the space. You need the site and the sheaf. The space, if it exists, is a convenience — a representing object for the functor. But the functor is primary.
