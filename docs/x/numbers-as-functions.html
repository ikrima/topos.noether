Of these four, Manin notes, only π resembles a "physical constant" that can be measured. The formula eπi = −1 encodes the fundamental quantum-mechanical principle of interference of probability amplitudes: two states with opposite phases annihilate upon superposition.

§ 2The Adelic World

Euler's product formula provides an "arithmetic" incarnation of π — but one that involves all primes simultaneously. This is the realm of adèlic geometry, where the archimedean and non-archimedean completions of ℚ are treated democratically.

$$\frac{\pi^2}{6} = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} (1 - p^{-2})^{-1}$$
Interactive Euler Product

Watch the partial product converge to π²/6 as you include more primes. Each factor represents a p-adic contribution.

This formula encodes the fact that the natural adelic measure of SL(2, 𝔸ℚ)/SL(2, ℚ) equals 1. Manin proposes that fundamental quantum physics might be connected to number theory through this "democracy of all valuations" — the exclusive use of real and complex numbers in physics is perhaps just tradition.

The p-adic exponential

For each prime p, the archimedean series for ep also converges p-adically:

$$e^p = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{p^n}{n!}$$

§ 3Periods

Kontsevich and Zagier introduced an important subring 𝒫 ⊂ ℂ containing all algebraic numbers and many numbers central to physics. A period is the real or imaginary part of an absolutely convergent integral of a rational function over a semialgebraic domain — both defined over ℚ.

Multiple Zeta Values

The multiple zeta values, first studied by Euler, form a rich algebra of periods. They arise as iterated integrals over the simplex Δ0n:

$$\zeta(n_1, \ldots, n_m) = \sum_{0 < k_1 < \cdots < k_m} \frac{1}{k_1^{n_1} \cdots k_m^{n_m}}$$

Periods as Functions

When parameters are introduced, periods become transcendental functions satisfying Picard–Fuchs differential equations. Manin's key example: the family of elliptic curves Et : Y² = X(X−1)(X−t) has periods satisfying the linear ODE:

$$L_t \omega := 4t(1-t)\frac{d^2\omega}{dt^2} + 4(1-2t)\frac{d\omega}{dt} - \omega = 0$$

This equation — and its nonlinear companion involving the differential character μ(P) — is precisely what Buium's theory translates into the p-adic direction.

§ 4Feynman Graphs & Periods

The Feynman path integral — the central computational object of quantum field theory — generates periods indexed by graphs. The perturbative expansion of the partition function is a sum over isomorphism classes of finite graphs τ, weighted by their Feynman weight w(τ).

$$\frac{\int_{\mathcal{P}} e^{\lambda^{-1}S(\varphi)} D(\varphi)}{\int_{\mathcal{P}} e^{\lambda^{-1}S_0(\varphi)} D(\varphi)} = \sum_{\tau \in \Gamma} \frac{\lambda^{-\chi(\tau)}}{|\mathrm{Aut}\,\tau|}\, w(\tau)$$

Each vertex v of valence k carries a coupling tensor Ca₁…ak, each edge e carries a propagator gu(∂e). The weight w(τ) sums over all colorings of the flags by a color set A. In physical models, this discrete sum becomes a continuous integral over momenta — and these integrals are periods.

§ 5The p-adic Derivative

The heart of Manin's survey is Alexandru Buium's theory of arithmetic differential equations. The key insight: the right analogue of differentiation in the p-adic direction is the Fermat quotient operator:

$$\delta_p(a) := \frac{\Phi(a) - a^p}{p}$$

where Φ is a lift of the Frobenius endomorphism. For integers, this becomes the familiar:

$$\delta_p(m) = \frac{m - m^p}{p}$$
Interactive Fermat Quotient Calculator

Compute δp(m) = (m − mp) / p for small integers. The result is always an integer!

Modified Leibniz Rule

Unlike a classical derivation, δp does not satisfy the usual Leibniz rule. Instead, it obeys a twisted version mediated by the Witt polynomial Cp:

$$\delta_p(x + y) = \delta_p(x) + \delta_p(y) + C_p(x, y)$$
$$C_p(X,Y) := \frac{X^p + Y^p - (X+Y)^p}{p}$$

§ 6The Grand Analogy

Buium's entire program rests on a systematic analogy between formal power series in one variable and p-adic numbers. The following table captures this dictionary:

The constants of the p-adic derivative — roots of unity and zero — are precisely the Teichmüller representatives. This observation is the gateway to 𝔽₁-geometry.

§ 7p-Jet Spaces

Just as classical jet spaces capture Taylor expansions of functions, Buium's p-jet spaces Ji(X) capture iterated Fermat quotients. A prolongation sequence is a family of p-adically complete algebras Ai with compatible lifts of Frobenius and p-derivations.

§ 8The Field with One Element

The constants of δp are roots of unity — the "scalars" of 𝔽₁-geometry. Jacques Tits observed in 1957 that combinatorial invariants of classical groups over 𝔽q have well-defined limits as q → 1, and these limits count ordinary combinatorial objects.

The Gaussian integer [n]q = (qn − 1)/(q − 1) counts points of ℙn−1(𝔽q). As q → 1, it becomes n — the cardinality of a finite set.

Gaussian Binomial Coefficients

The q-analog extends to binomial coefficients: the number of j-dimensional subspaces of 𝔽qn is the Gaussian binomial coefficient (n choose j)q, which as q → 1 recovers the ordinary binomial coefficient — the number of j-element subsets of an n-element set.

Gaussian Binomial at q → 1

Kapranov and Smirnov defined 𝔽1n as the monoid {0} ∪ μn, with vector spaces as pointed sets carrying a free μn-action. Their determinant map det : GL(V) → μn recovers the power residue symbol — a beautiful bridge between combinatorics and arithmetic.

§ 9Borger's Descent to 𝔽₁

James Borger's insight unifies the entire picture: a λ-structure on a commutative ring A — a system of commuting Frobenius lifts for all primes — should be understood as descent data from Spec ℤ to Spec 𝔽₁.

Lambda-Ring Structure Visualizer

A ψ-ring carries commuting endomorphisms ψp for each prime, satisfying ψp(x) ≡ xp mod p. Click primes to toggle their Frobenius lifts.

In this framework, the big Witt ring W(ℤ) becomes ℤ ⊗𝔽₁ ℤ, and the Weil restriction of scalars from ℤ to 𝔽₁ is an arithmetically global version of Buium's p-jet space. The entire program — differential equations in the p-adic direction, periods, F₁-geometry — converges into a single, coherent vision.

§ 10Coda

Manin's survey traces a remarkable arc: from Euler's identity through adelic geometry, periods, Feynman integrals, and Buium's arithmetic differential equations, to the geometry over the field with one element. The catchword "numbers as functions" captures the core analogy — that integers, viewed through the p-adic derivative, behave like functions of a variable that ranges over primes.

The open challenges remain profound: connecting Buium's p-adic periods with Fontaine's BdR-valued periods, understanding the archimedean prime within 𝔽₁-geometry (it "still remains first among equals"), and extending the theory to moduli of higher-dimensional abelian varieties.

"The archimedean prime still remains 'first among equals' breaking the democracy of all valuations." — Yuri Manin
