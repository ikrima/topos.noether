When Curves Come Alive

Elastic energy, solitons, and the astonishing equation
hiding inside every moving filament

In Part 1, we built the geometric vocabulary of curves — curvature, torsion, the Bishop frame, and the topological constraint of Călugăreanu's theorem. Those were static snapshots. Now the curves start to move.

What happens when you let a bent curve relax under its own elastic energy? What happens when a vortex filament in a fluid evolves according to its own curvature? The answers lead to some of the most celebrated equations in mathematical physics — and to solitons, those mysterious waves that pass through each other without breaking.

The Elastic Curve

Euler posed the question in 1744: among all plane curves of fixed length connecting two points with given tangent directions, which one minimizes the total bending energy ∫κ² ds? The answer — the elastica — is the shape a thin elastic rod naturally assumes.

The Euler–Lagrange equation for this variational problem is:

κ'' + ½κ³ = λκ

where λ is a Lagrange multiplier enforcing the length constraint. This is a nonlinear oscillator for the curvature κ(s), and its solutions are elliptic functions — the same functions that describe a pendulum. Depending on the energy level, the solutions sweep through a remarkable family of shapes.

At low energy, the curvature oscillates gently — these are undulating elastica, smooth wave-like bends. At high energy, the curvature rotates through all values — these are rotating elastica that form looping figures. But right at the boundary between these two regimes lies something special.

The separatrix — the green curve in the phase portrait — corresponds to a curvature profile that is a hyperbolic secant: κ(s) = 2η·sech(ηs). This is a single localized bend on an otherwise straight line. It's the soliton — the unique shape that balances nonlinearity against dispersion. We'll see it again very soon.

The Vortex Filament Equation

Now let's go to three dimensions. In 1906, Da Rios — a student of Levi-Civita — discovered the law governing how a thin vortex filament moves in an ideal fluid. Each point on the filament moves in the binormal direction at a speed proportional to the curvature:

γ_t = κ B

Think about this: where the filament is bent sharply (high κ), it moves fast. Where it's straight, it doesn't move at all. And the direction of motion is always perpendicular to the plane of curvature. This makes a ring vortex translate along its axis (the curvature is constant and the binormal points along the axis everywhere), while a straight filament stays still.

What's remarkable about this equation is what it preserves. The total length of the curve doesn't change. The total bending energy ∫κ² ds stays constant. In fact, it preserves an infinite family of conserved quantities. The VFE is an integrable system — it has a hidden mathematical structure that makes it exactly solvable.

Watch the soliton mode carefully. A localized helical kink — a bump of curvature — travels along the filament without changing shape. It's the 3D cousin of the elastica soliton we saw on the plane. The helix translates rigidly. The ring does too, but also precesses.

The existence of these traveling-wave solutions hints at deep structure. To reveal it, we need a change of variables that Hasimoto discovered in 1972.

The Hasimoto Transform

This is the key insight of the entire story. Hasimoto defined a complex-valued function along the curve:

ψ(s,t) = κ(s,t) · exp(i ∫₀ˢ τ(η,t) dη)

The amplitude of ψ is the curvature. The phase accumulates torsion. In the Bishop frame, this is simply ψ = κ₁ + iκ₂, where κ₁ and κ₂ are the two components of curvature in the twist-free basis. It's the most natural complex number you could build from a space curve.

Hasimoto's theorem: if the curve evolves by the vortex filament equation, then ψ satisfies the focusing nonlinear Schrödinger equation:

iψ_t + ψ_ss + ½|ψ|²ψ = 0

This is one of the great equations of mathematical physics. It describes everything from rogue waves in the ocean to light pulses in optical fibers to Bose-Einstein condensates. And here it is, hiding inside the geometry of a moving curve.

The NLS 1-soliton is ψ = 2η·sech(2η(s−4ξt))·exp(i(2ξs−4(ξ²−η²)t)). The parameter η controls the amplitude and width of the soliton — the curvature bump. The parameter ξ controls the velocity and the constant torsion — how helical the kink is.

Watch the curve in 3D as you drag the time slider. The sech-shaped envelope of |ψ| travels along the filament, and the 3D curve shows a helical kink translating without distortion. The amplitude ∫|ψ|² ds = ∫κ² ds is conserved — the bending energy is constant, as promised by the integrability of the VFE.

Why NLS? The nonlinear Schrödinger equation appears whenever you have dispersive waves with a cubic nonlinearity. In our setting, the dispersion comes from the curve's elastic resistance to bending (the ψ_ss term), and the nonlinearity comes from the geometric coupling between curvature and the frame (the |ψ|²ψ term). The balance between these two effects is what allows solitons to exist.

From Plane to Space

There's a beautiful organizing principle here. In the plane, a curve has only one intrinsic quantity: curvature κ(s). Its evolution equation is the modified Korteweg–de Vries equation:

κ_t = κ_sss + (3/2)κ²κ_s   (mKdV)

In 3D, a curve has two intrinsic quantities — κ and τ — combined into the complex Hasimoto variable ψ = κ·exp(i∫τ). The evolution equation is NLS. The mKdV equation is simply the restriction of NLS to real-valued potentials, which means zero torsion, which means planar curves.

This is the grand pattern: the dimension of the ambient space selects the integrable equation. Plane curves give mKdV. Space curves give NLS. Curves on the 3-sphere give the Ablowitz–Ladik lattice (a discrete NLS). Each step up in dimension promotes a real equation to a complex one, a scalar to a matrix, a continuous system to a discrete one.

The Miura web. The mKdV → KdV connection via the Miura transformation u = κ² + κ_s has a beautiful geometric meaning: it transforms curvature (a Euclidean invariant) into the Schwarzian derivative (a conformal invariant). The AKNS scattering system at spectral parameter λ = 0 is literally the Bishop frame equation. The spectral parameter deforms geometry into a scattering problem.
