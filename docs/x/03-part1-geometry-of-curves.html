The Geometry of Curves

How bending, twisting, and topology intertwine
in the mathematics of thin elastic rods

Pick up a phone charger cable and hold it between your hands. Now twist one end. The cable doesn't just twist — it writhes, coils, loops back on itself. Something about the geometry of the curve and the twist stored in the material are deeply coupled.

This coupling isn't an accident. It's controlled by a beautiful theorem from topology that connects three numbers: how much the curve links with itself, how much it twists, and how much it writhes in space. To understand that theorem, we first need to understand what it means for a curve to bend.

Curvature

Imagine driving along a road. At each point, you could ask: how sharply am I turning? That's curvature. A straight road has zero curvature. A tight hairpin turn has high curvature. Formally, curvature κ(s) measures how fast the tangent direction changes as you walk along the curve at unit speed.

Notice something: the osculating circle — the best-fit circle at each point — has radius R = 1/κ. Where curvature is high, the circle is small and tight. Where curvature is low, the circle is enormous, nearly a straight line.

The quantity ∫κ² ds shown in the readout is the total bending energy of the curve. This is exactly the energy that a thin elastic rod stores when you bend it. An elastic rod, left to itself, minimizes this integral — which is why a relaxed cable hangs in a smooth catenary rather than a zigzag.

The Moving Frame

Curvature tells us how much a curve bends, but in three dimensions we need more. We need to track not just the tangent direction, but an entire coordinate frame — a set of three perpendicular axes — riding along the curve. This moving frame is what lets us distinguish bending from twisting.

The classical choice is the Frenet frame: the tangent T, the normal N pointing toward the center of curvature, and the binormal B = T × N. It works beautifully — until the curve has an inflection point where curvature passes through zero. There, the normal direction is undefined, and the Frenet frame spins wildly.

In 1975, Bishop discovered a better frame. Instead of locking the normal to the curvature direction, he let it parallel transport along the curve — the gentlest possible evolution, with zero twist by construction. The result is a frame that never has singularities, even at inflection points.

With the Bishop frame {T, u, v} in hand, any other adapted frame is just a rotation by some angle θ(s) around the tangent. The material frame {T, m₁, m₂} — which tracks the actual orientation of our rod's cross-section — is encoded by this single scalar function. This is the key insight from Langer and Singer (1996) that makes discrete elastic rod simulation so efficient: instead of tracking a full 3-vector frame, we only need one angle per edge.

The twist of the material frame is simply m = dθ/ds — how fast that angle changes along the curve. Use the θ slider above to rotate the material frame and watch it wind around the tangent.

Twist, Writhe, and the Linking Number

Now for the deep result. Take a closed ribbon — a curve with a material frame, which you can think of as a strip of paper bent into a loop. This ribbon has three numbers associated with it:

The linking number Lk counts how many times the two edges of the ribbon wind around each other. It's a topological invariant — you can't change it without cutting the ribbon. The twist Tw measures the total winding of the material frame around the tangent: Tw = (1/2π)∫m ds. And the writhe Wr measures how much the curve's centerline coils in space — a signed count of self-crossings as seen from every viewing direction.

Călugăreanu's theorem (1961) states that these three are locked together:

Lk = Tw + Wr

This is profound. If you hold the linking number fixed (no cutting allowed) and reduce the twist, the writhe must increase to compensate. This is exactly what happens when you twist a phone cord — the stored twist converts into spatial coiling. It's also the mechanism behind DNA supercoiling.

Play with the sliders above. When you add twist while keeping the curve circular, Wr ≈ 0 and all the linking goes into twist. But push the writhe amplitude up and the curve begins to coil — writhe increases and twist decreases, keeping Lk = Tw + Wr balanced.

The ribbon test. If you've ever tried to straighten a coiled garden hose, you've felt Călugăreanu's theorem. The hose wants to conserve its linking number. Pulling the coils straight (reducing writhe) forces the hose to twist in your hands — the twist increases to compensate.

This completes our geometric toolkit. We have curvature κ measuring bending, torsion τ measuring the frame's rotation out of the osculating plane, the Bishop frame giving a singularity-free reference, and Călugăreanu's theorem connecting twist and writhe topologically. In Part 2, we'll see what happens when these curves start to move — and discover that the equations governing their motion are among the most beautiful in all of mathematical physics.
