Metric Spaces

Exploring the geometry of distance - from familiar Euclidean space to abstract mathematical universes where "distance" takes on surprising new forms.

Chapter 2 from "An Infinitely Large Napkin" by Evan Chen

What is a Metric Space?

A metric space is a set equipped with a notion of "distance" between any two of its points. This distance function, called a metric, must satisfy a few natural properties that capture our intuition about what "distance" should mean.

Definition

A metric space is a pair (M, d) where M is a set and d: M × M → ℝ≥0 is a function satisfying:

  1. Non-negativity: d(x, y) ≥ 0, with equality iff x = y
  2. Symmetry: d(x, y) = d(y, x)
  3. Triangle Inequality: d(x, z) ≤ d(x, y) + d(y, z)

The beauty of metric spaces lies in how this simple abstraction encompasses everything from Euclidean geometry to function spaces to discrete structures. Let's explore these ideas through interactive visualizations.

Understanding the Metrics

The Euclidean metric gives us the familiar "straight-line" distance - the one you'd measure with a ruler. The taxicab metric (also called Manhattan distance) measures distance as if you could only travel along a grid of streets. The Chebyshev metric measures the maximum of the horizontal and vertical distances - like a king moving on a chessboard.

The Triangle Inequality

The triangle inequality is perhaps the most important property of a metric. It says that the direct path is always the shortest - you can never get closer to your destination by taking a detour through a third point.

Triangle Inequality

For any three points x, y, z in a metric space, we have:

d(x, z) ≤ d(x, y) + d(y, z)

The direct distance from x to z is at most the distance through y.

Neighborhoods and Balls

The shape of a "ball" in a metric space - all points within distance r of a center point - depends entirely on the metric. This is one of the most visually striking ways to see how different metrics create different geometries.

Definition

The open ball of radius r centered at p is:

Br(p) = { x ∈ M : d(x, p) < r }

Convergence in Metric Spaces

A sequence converges in a metric space if the distances from the sequence terms to a limit point get arbitrarily small. This generalizes the familiar notion of limits from calculus.

Definition

A sequence (xn) in a metric space (M, d) converges to L if:

∀ε > 0, ∃N : n > N ⇒ d(xn, L) < ε

Continuous Functions

In metric spaces, continuity has a beautiful characterization: a function is continuous if nearby inputs produce nearby outputs. This is the famous epsilon-delta definition.

Definition

A function f: (M1, d1) → (M2, d2) is continuous at p if:

∀ε > 0, ∃δ > 0 : d1(x, p) < δ ⇒ d2(f(x), f(p)) < ε

The Key Insight

For a continuous function, given any ε > 0 (how close we want outputs), we can find some δ > 0 (how close inputs need to be) that works. For discontinuous functions, there will be some ε for which no δ suffices.

Open and Closed Sets

The concepts of open and closed sets are fundamental to topology, and metric spaces give us a concrete way to understand them through neighborhoods.

Definition

A set U ⊆ M is open if for every point p ∈ U, there exists r > 0 such that Br(p) ⊆ U.

A set is closed if its complement is open.

Key Properties
  • The empty set and whole space are both open AND closed
  • Arbitrary unions of open sets are open
  • Finite intersections of open sets are open
  • Open balls are always open sets

Homeomorphisms: When Spaces Are "The Same"

A homeomorphism is a continuous bijection with a continuous inverse. Two spaces are homeomorphic if one can be continuously deformed into the other - the famous "rubber sheet geometry."

The circle and square are homeomorphic - we can continuously deform one into the other while preserving the topology.

What Homeomorphisms Preserve

Homeomorphisms preserve topological properties: connectedness, compactness, the number of "holes," etc. They do NOT preserve metric properties like distance or area. The famous joke: a topologist can't tell a coffee mug from a donut!

Important Fact

All three product metrics induce the same topology on X × Y. They produce homeomorphic spaces, even though the distances differ. This illustrates how topology cares about qualitative structure, not quantitative distances.

Exotic Metric Spaces

Beyond ℝn, metric spaces appear in surprising contexts. Here are some examples that stretch our geometric intuition.

Discrete Metric

d(x,y) = 0 if x=y, else 1. Every set becomes a metric space where all points are equally far apart!

Function Space C[0,1]

Continuous functions on [0,1] with d(f,g) = sup|f(x)-g(x)|. Convergence = uniform convergence.

Hamming Distance

For binary strings: count positions where they differ. Used in coding theory and bioinformatics.

p-adic Metric

Numbers are "close" if their difference is divisible by high powers of p. Ultrametric: d(x,z) ≤ max(d(x,y), d(y,z))!
