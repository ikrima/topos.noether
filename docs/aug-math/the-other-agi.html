I. Origin

The Summer Project That Lasted Sixty Years

In the summer of 1966, MIT professor Seymour Papert wrote a memo assigning an undergraduate research project. Its goal: build a system that could look at a scene and describe what it saw. He estimated the task would take one summer.

He was off by about sixty years, depending on how you count.

This is not a story about a professor being wrong. It is a story about a category error that has been faithfully reproduced, at scale, every decade since: the confident announcement that human-level AI is close, followed by the quiet retreat, followed by a new announcement a few years later with a new deadline.

"The problem of computer vision was initially considered so trivial that it could be assigned as a summer project." â€” Attributed to Marvin Minsky, MIT AI Lab, c. 1966

The Minsky fumble is worth dwelling on â€” not to mock, but to understand. The researchers of the 1960s weren't foolish. They understood logic, mathematics, search, pattern-matching. What they systematically underestimated was the depth of the iceberg: the vast, invisible machinery that biological intelligence uses to do what looks, from the outside, like simple recognition.

What a Summer Project Actually Contains

When Papert assigned "solve computer vision," he imagined an edge detector and a shape classifier. What actually emerged, over fifty years, was one of the richest fields in science.

The pattern generalizes. Every component of human intelligence â€” perception, language, reasoning, planning, social cognition, creativity â€” has this structure: deceptively simple from outside, bewilderingly complex from inside. "Solve language" became computational linguistics, corpus linguistics, NLP, large language models, alignment research. "Solve reasoning" became automated theorem proving, probabilistic inference, knowledge representation, causal inference.

This is not a reason for pessimism. The work has been immensely productive. We have edge detectors, and depth cameras, and object recognizers that outperform humans on specific benchmarks. The summer project, distributed across thousands of researchers, has borne enormous fruit.

But the fruit is not AGI in the original sense. And this matters, because the failure to recognize what we actually built has caused us to badly undervalue it.

III. Reframe

The Mirror, Not the Oracle

The standard framing of AI is oracular: a system you ask questions, which produces answers. The oracle framing generates two fears simultaneously â€” that the oracle will be wrong (hallucination), and that the oracle will be right (replacement).

Both fears are real. Both, in the oracular framing, are paralyzing. And the framing is wrong.

A better framing: AI is a cognitive mirror. Not a mirror that reflects your face â€” a mirror that reflects your thinking, sharpened, extended across time and domains you haven't visited.

IV. Practice

What Augmented & Genuine Intelligence Looks Like in Practice

The reframing has practical implications. If AI is a cognitive mirror, then the question is not "what can it do for me?" but "what does it show me about my own thinking that I couldn't see before?"

The loop is a concrete implementation of augmentation rather than replacement. At every stage, the human is the author â€” the mathematician, the researcher, the teacher. The AI is the mirror that makes the author's thinking more precise, more connected to existing knowledge, and more accessible to others.

The Council of Luminaries methodology doesn't generate mathematics. It maps the conceptual territory around a mathematical intuition, so the mathematician knows which door to walk through. The Victor/Ciechanowski methodology doesn't write essays. It provides the structural template so that a mathematician with domain expertise can build interactive essays without also needing to be a pedagogical designer. The Braggoscope pipeline doesn't think for you. It finds what you've already thought, so you don't have to hold it all in working memory.

None of these replace the mathematician. All of them make the mathematician more capable.

V. Conjecture

The Minsky Conjecture, Inverted

We propose a name for the pattern: the Minsky Conjecture. The original Minsky fumble was assuming that hard problems are easy. The inverted form is the one that matters now.

The Inverted Minsky Conjecture: We have systematically undervalued what AI can already do, because we have been measuring it against a standard (human-equivalent general intelligence) that it does not meet, while ignoring the standard it does meet (a cognitive mirror of unprecedented breadth and recall).

Every tool in the history of augmentation has been undervalued at first. Writing was decried as a threat to memory â€” it was, and we got libraries. The printing press threatened scholarly authority â€” it did, and we got science. The calculator threatened arithmetic skill â€” it did, and we got computational science.

In each case, the tool changed what it meant to be expert. The expert stopped being someone who could hold everything in their head and became someone who knew how to use the tools to think beyond what unaided heads could reach.

AGI â€” Augmented & Genuine Intelligence â€” is that tool. It is already here. It is already transformative. The reason we haven't noticed is that we've been looking for the oracle and missing the mirror.
