Each blueprint follows from the philosophical commitments of its authors, shaped by three days of vigorous dialectic. They are not variations on a single template. They are genuinely different answers to what a school of argumentation is for, which produces genuinely different structures, timelines, and measures of success.

The reader should attend not only to what each program includes but to what it omits — the omissions reveal the philosophical commitments as clearly as the inclusions.

The Five Programs

Program Blueprints

Program I

The Way of Stillness

A Program in Receptive Inquiry — Laozi & Shunryu Suzuki

Core Thesis: The deepest argumentation emerges from the deepest listening. Most of what passes for debate training is the accumulation of techniques that obstruct genuine understanding.

Governing Metaphor: Water — it takes the shape of whatever contains it. It yields to every force. And it carved the Grand Canyon.

Months 1–3 · Emptying the Cup

Students are paired. One speaks for five uninterrupted minutes. The other listens — not to respond, not to evaluate — simply to hear. Then the listener must restate what was said to the speaker's satisfaction. No argumentation theory is introduced. No terminology is taught. The cup must be emptied before it can be filled.

Months 4–6 · The Container Emerges

Weekly circle practice. A moment of silence to begin, a reflection to close. One person makes a claim about something that genuinely matters. The group's task is not to attack or defend but to explore — to ask questions that help the speaker discover what they actually think. This is closer to Socratic elenchus than competitive debate, but without the Socratic agenda.

Months 7–9 · Water Finds Its Course

The professor does not introduce competition. If students discover tournaments and express interest, the professor supports this — but as their initiative. Guidance: "You will meet people who are faster, louder, and more technically skilled. Your advantage is that you can actually hear what they are saying." One public inquiry event on a campus issue.

Months 10–12 · Roots and Water

The professor withdraws. Senior students facilitate. The question is whether the quality of attention persists. If it does, the program is real. If it does not, the honest response is to accept this.

What it produces

People whose first instinct when encountering an opposing view is curiosity rather than defense. People who have discovered that the most powerful thing you can do in an argument is actually hear what the other person is saying.

What it does not produce

Competitive debaters, in the conventional sense. Students may struggle with formats that reward speed, aggression, and technical proficiency.

Year-End Measure: Do students listen before speaking? Can they hold genuine disagreement without attacking or capitulating?

Program II

The Academy

A Program in Systematic Formation — Aristotle, Plato & Marcus Aurelius

Core Thesis: The capacity for rigorous argumentation is an excellence (aretē) that requires systematic cultivation across three domains: the intellect (Aristotle), orientation toward truth (Plato), and character (Marcus Aurelius). A program training only one produces clever sophists, impractical idealists, or well-intentioned incompetents.

Governing Metaphor: The gymnasium — the Greeks trained body and mind in the same space because excellence is cultivated through structured practice.

Months 1–4 · Foundations

Month 1 — The Organon: Claim, warrant, impact. Valid vs. sound. The categorical syllogism. Marcus adds: weekly reflection — "What do I believe? Why? What would change my mind?"
Month 2 — The Topoi: Definition, comparison, cause, testimony, analogy. Generate arguments from four distinct topoi in ten minutes. Plato adds: Socratic elenchus in pairs.
Month 3 — Rhetoric: Ethos, pathos, logos. Adapt the same argument to three audiences. Marcus adds: "Where did I speak from conviction? Where did I perform?"
Month 4 — Integration: Full practice debates with triple debrief: Aristotle's question (well-constructed?), Plato's question (truth or victory?), Marcus's question (how did you handle pressure?).

Months 5–8 · Deepening

First tournament (month 5). Dialectical core: primary philosophical texts as positions to defend and attack. Every student argues positions they disagree with. Plato's emphasis: the courage to follow the argument wherever it leads. Month 8 — The Art of Changing Your Mind: each student identifies a belief they no longer hold and explains what changed. The Academy's distinctive contribution — no other faction emphasizes this.

Months 9–12 · Mastery & Transmission

Multiple tournaments. Public symposium on campus issue with faculty judges. Course proposal to administration. Written handbook capturing not just what but why. Three-part year-end assessment: valid argument? (Aristotle) / truth over victory? (Plato) / equanimity under pressure? (Marcus)

What it produces

Practitioners of structured reasoning with both competitive skill and intellectual integrity. People who can win a debate round and honestly assess whether the position they defended was correct.

What it does not produce

Spontaneous, intuitive arguers. Students may lack the radical ego-dissolution that Contemplative and Warrior traditions cultivate, relying instead on disciplined self-management.

Program III

The Way of the Warrior

Strategic Mastery Through Direct Engagement — Musashi, Sun Tzu & Tsunetomo

Core Thesis: Argumentation is combat — not metaphorical but actual, in which real things are at stake: reputation, conviction, the right to be heard. A program that pretends otherwise produces practitioners who collapse at the first moment of genuine pressure.

Governing Metaphor: The dojo — you do not learn swordsmanship by studying swords. You learn it by picking one up and facing someone who is trying to cut you.

Months 1–3 · The Forging

Week 1: combat begins. No orientation period. Students debate on the first day and perform badly. This is necessary.
Daily drills: Speed (30→15→10 seconds to generate arguments), cross-examination (controlled demolition), flowing (real-time battlefield tracking), refutation (destroy and rebuild in 60 seconds).
Sun Tzu: concurrent intelligence — formats, judges, dominant programs, gaps to exploit.
Tsunetomo: before every round, visualize total humiliation until the emotional charge dissipates. The Practice of Dying.

Months 4–7 · The Campaign

First tournament (month 4). Exhaustive post-tournament analysis — every loss reveals information. Adaptive training based on battlefield intelligence. Selective recruitment: "Do they have the stomach? Can they be trained? Are they willing to fail completely?" Month 7 — Art of the Stratagem: "Win without your strongest argument." "Concede the best point and still win." "Argue the position you believe is wrong."

Months 8–12 · Dominance & Legacy

Full competitive season — training intensifies, does not slow. Month 11 — The Challenge Round: any student on campus can debate a team member on any topic. Musashi: the warrior must be ready. Sun Tzu: demonstrates strength, recruits. Tsunetomo: the team member who loses to an untrained challenger and handles it with grace has demonstrated the deepest warrior virtue.
Transmission through sparring, not lecturing. Documentation: a playbook, an opponent database, a drill manual.

What it produces

Competitors who perform at peak under pressure. Strategists who position to win. And — if Tsunetomo's teaching takes root — warriors who fight with total commitment and zero attachment.

What it does not produce

Philosophers. The program assumes mastery reveals its own meaning through practice. Students may develop extraordinary competitive skill while remaining unreflective about what that skill is for.

Program IV

The Ciceronian Program

The Complete Orator — Marcus Tullius Cicero

Core Thesis: The orator is the person of wide learning, moral seriousness, and rhetorical mastery who can make wisdom persuasive in the civic arena. The school exists to produce citizens capable of shaping public life through the power of articulate thought.

Governing Metaphor: The Forum — all roads lead to the place where decisions are made.

Months 1–4 · Foundation & The Five Canons

Liberal Foundation: wide reading across all disciplines — the debater who knows only debate runs out of things to say by round three. Voice training: projection, pacing, silence, physical presence. The Five Canons: Inventio, Dispositio, Elocutio, Memoria, Actio — each practiced separately, then integrated. Students present the same argument five times, each focusing on a different canon.

Months 5–7 · Competition & Rhetorical Analysis

Every round is a speech, every speech a performance. Judged not only on logic but artistry — can you turn a phrase the judge remembers? Study of great speeches across history: structural choices, stylistic signatures, the moments where the speaker did something surprising. Developing taste.

Months 8–12 · The Civic Turn & Capstone

The Civic Turn: public speaking beyond tournaments — campus government, community forums, public advocacy. Mentoring based on demonstrated excellence, not seniority. Capstone: each student delivers a 10–15 minute oration on a topic of their choosing to a public audience. The Ciceronian final exam — can you stand alone and hold a room through the power of thought expressed with art?

What it produces

Eloquent, learned, publicly effective speakers who treat rhetoric as an art form and a civic responsibility. People who can walk into any room and command attention.

What it does not produce

People comfortable with silence. Fluency can become compulsion — the inability to not-speak. Cicero's orator may be the last person in the room to hear something important.

Program V

The Demosthenic Program

A Program in Dangerous Speech — Demosthenes of Athens

Core Thesis: The purpose of rhetoric is to move people to act when action is necessary. The school exists to produce people who are dangerous — dangerous to complacency, to injustice, to the comfortable silences that allow suffering to persist. The test: when they speak, does something change?

Governing Metaphor: Pebbles in the mouth, waves crashing — mastery born from desperation, refined by relentless practice, deployed with absolute commitment.

Months 1–2 · Finding the Fire

Demosthenes does not begin with technique. He begins with conviction. Each student must identify a real injustice — something that makes them angry, that makes them unable to sleep. Without this raw material, everything that follows is performance. Weekly: stand and speak about what you care about. No notes, no structure. The only question: "Did I feel it? Did your words enter my body or only my ears?"

Months 3–7 · The Forge & The Arena

Technique as instrument of conviction. Practice speaking in hostile conditions, to distracted audiences, while being interrupted. Arguments that escalate — building through intensification to a conclusion demanding action. Competition judged by: "if the judge had been a citizen, would they have done something?" Real-world advocacy at council meetings, campus governance. Study of history's pivotal speeches — not just words but situations.

Months 8–12 · The Cost of Speaking & The Campaign

The Cost: students study rhetoric's consequences — Demosthenes was exiled, Cicero murdered, Douglass beaten, Malala shot. Argue unpopular positions on your own campus for the practice of standing alone. The Campaign: the capstone is not a speech but a campaign. Each student identifies a concrete change and pursues it — op-eds, governing bodies, organizing. Year-end: did any student's advocacy change an actual outcome? If not, the program was merely educational, and Demosthenes would consider this a failure.

What it produces

Advocates. People who speak because they must, who measure success not by awards but by impact. People dangerous to every form of complacency and comfortable injustice.

What it does not produce

Balanced, dispassionate analysts. Passion can blind as easily as it illuminates. The world does not suffer from an excess of passionate truth-tellers, Demosthenes would answer. It suffers from their absence.

Section VI

Internal Faction Differences

Where the Allies Diverge

Each faction presented a unified blueprint, but the symposium's dialectic revealed significant internal tensions. These are not contradictions — they are emphases that reflect genuinely distinct understandings within shared traditions.

Within the Way of Stillness

Laozi vs. Suzuki

Core Tension: Form versus Formlessness

Laozi Suzuki
Pure formlessness Precise form as container

Laozi's Solo Program

Even less structure. Would resist regular meeting times, rituals, even the designation as a "program." Might not survive twelve months as an identifiable entity — and would not consider this a failure. The river does not need a name to flow.

Risk: dissipation.

Suzuki's Solo Program

More structure: a specific sitting practice, formal dialogue protocols, designated rotating roles. Without the container, "formlessness" becomes mere sloppiness — and students never develop the discipline that genuine openness requires.

Risk: ritual that forgets its purpose.

Within the Academy

Aristotle vs. Plato vs. Marcus Aurelius

Core Tension: A three-way disagreement about what is primary

Drag the circle to explore where the emphasis falls

Aristotle's program wins more tournaments. Plato's produces deeper thinkers. Marcus's produces better human beings. The joint blueprint compromises — each sage would argue the compromise dilutes what matters most.

Within the Way of the Warrior

Musashi vs. Sun Tzu vs. Tsunetomo

Core Tension: Direct experience vs. strategic intelligence vs. spiritual freedom

Musashi

MOST BRUTAL

Daily rounds from day one. Enter all tournaments. Highest attrition rate, smallest graduates — but those graduates are forged. Considers strategy cowardice and inner work delay.

Fastest to produce competitors. Burns out many students.

Sun Tzu

MOST CALCULATED

Extensive research. Strategic selection of everything. Every decision optimized. Best competitive record relative to resources. Considers Musashi wasteful and Tsunetomo irrelevant.

Most efficient. May produce strategists who lack conviction.

Tsunetomo

MOST RADICAL

Weeks on nothing but dissolving the ego's need to win before ever competing. Students genuinely unnerving to opponents — you cannot intimidate someone who has already accepted the worst. Considers Musashi incomplete, Sun Tzu spiritually empty.

Slowest results. Most resilient and psychologically free debaters.

Cross-Cutting Insight

The deepest disagreement in the symposium is not between factions but within them — because people who share a tradition understand most precisely where they diverge.

An Aristotelian and a Taoist may talk past each other productively. An Aristotelian and a Platonist must confront their disagreement directly, because they share enough vocabulary to know exactly what they are arguing about.

The most important conversations will not be between your program and your competitors. They will be within your own coaching staff, about what you are actually trying to do and why.

These proceedings are offered not as a single recommendation but as five distinct and genuinely different answers to the question of what a school of argumentation should be — with the conviction that any program builder who takes all five seriously will construct something more complete than any single tradition could produce alone.
