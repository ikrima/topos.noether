Borrowing
Light

How a renderer reuses light paths between pixels — and why, for years, nobody could prove it wasn't lying.

Modern path tracers get a brutal budget: often one light path per pixel. ReSTIR squeezes startling images out of that budget by letting pixels share the paths their neighbors found. It works so well it ships in games. Yet its convergence was a mystery — and in one tiny example it provably converges to the wrong answer.

This essay rebuilds the theory that fixed that — Generalized Resampled Importance Sampling — from its atom upward. Every number you'll see is computed live in your browser. Scroll, and start grabbing things.

The paper's full machinery culminates in a live path-traced renderer. We're building that as its own piece — §8 arrives separately. Everything else lives here.

Section 1 — The problem

A budget of one

Importance sampling is aiming. At one sample per pixel, you cannot afford to aim badly.

Rendering an image means estimating, at every pixel, an integral over all the light paths that could reach it. Monte Carlo does this by firing random paths and averaging. Average more paths and the noise falls — but only as 1/√N. Halving the noise costs four times the rays.

It's: aim each sample better. That is importance sampling, and the rest of this essay is about getting good aim almost for free.
Section 2 — The atom

Resampling by hand

Keep the candidate you wish you'd drawn. Do it enough and a crude sampler starts behaving like a perfect one.

Resampled Importance Sampling (Talbot 2005) is one move. Draw a handful of candidates from a cheap, mediocre proposal. Score each by a target p̂ — how much you wish you'd drawn it. Then keep exactly one, chosen in proportion to its score. The survivor's distribution drifts toward the normalized target p̄ = p̂/‖p̂‖.

The contribution weight
The survivor Y has no tractable density, but it carries an unbiased contribution weight $W_Y = \frac{1}{\hat p(Y)}\sum_i w_i$ that stands in for $1/p_Y(Y)$. Then $\mathbb{E}[f(Y)\,W_Y]=\int f$. That single weight is what makes everything afterward composable.
Section 3 — The villain

The trap

Reuse looks like free samples. It isn't — and ignoring that can converge you, confidently, to the wrong number.

ReSTIR's idea: don't throw a pixel's hard-won path away — let neighbors and the next frame reuse it. Effective sample count seems to explode at linear cost. But each iteration only injects a fixed handful of genuinely new samples; the rest are re-selected duplicates. And if the resampling weights don't honestly account for who could have produced each sample, the estimate drifts.

Two estimators race on the same integral. Honest Monte Carlo keeps adding independent samples. Naïve reuse recycles with constant weights. Watch the error.

Section 4 — The key

The one criterion

Strip away the proofs and GRIS rests on a single demand. Make the variance of the summed weights vanish.

The generalized theory proves that the survivor's distribution converges to the target p̄ — and, if p̂ ∝ f, that integration variance vanishes — under one condition:

Convergence criterion (Theorem A.2)
$$\operatorname{Var}\!\Big[\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{M} w_i\Big] \xrightarrow{\,M\to\infty\,} 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{\bar p(Y)}{p_Y(Y)}\to 1.$$ Everything practical — proper MIS weights, canonical samples, M-capping — is just engineering to drag this one number to zero.

So this becomes the gauge we watch for the rest of the essay (it's now live in the sidebar). Below: draw good independent samples and push M. As Var[Σwᵢ] falls, the estimate's error falls in lockstep. They are the same story.

Section 5 — The generalization

Borrowing across pixels

A path that belongs to your neighbor can be bent into one of yours — through a bijection, paid for with a Jacobian.

Classic RIS assumed every candidate lived in the same domain. The generalized theory drops that: a path sampled for pixel A can be shifted into pixel B by a map T, as in gradient-domain rendering. The shift warps probability density, so its Jacobian |∂T/∂x| rides along inside the resampling weight.

Geometry is exact; the BSDF and contribution meter are a stylized model to make the lobe-mismatch visible.

A shift must be invertible: if A's path maps to B's, B's must map back. When it can't — occlusion, a grazing angle that makes the Jacobian blow up, a reconnection segment too short — the honest move is to return "undefined" and drop the path. Neglecting that doesn't lose a sample; it injects bias.

Section 6 — The mechanism

Reservoirs

All of this has to run in constant memory, in a single pass over a stream. One little box does it.

A reservoir holds exactly one chosen sample, a running sum of weights, and a confidence count. As each new sample streams past, the reservoir replaces what it holds with probability wᵢ / Σⱼ≤ᵢ wⱼ. After the last arrival, the held sample is distributed in exact proportion to the weights — RIS, in one streaming pass, with three numbers of memory.

Section 7 — Driving the gauge to zero

Two levers

With shift maps and reservoirs in hand, two controls decide whether Var[Σwᵢ] actually reaches zero.

Lever one — canonical samples. A canonical sample is drawn directly for your own pixel; it's guaranteed to cover the whole target. The theory needs their count |R| to grow faster than √M. Break that — reuse from an ever-wider neighborhood with a single canonical sample — and something surprising happens.

The U-curve (Figure 8). More reuse helps — then hurts. With one canonical sample, growing the window makes it ever less likely to be picked, so the region only it can reach gets starved. The estimate stays unbiased (proper Talbot MIS weights), but variance bottoms out near M≈√-ish and climbs back. Past the threshold, the convergence guarantee is gone. This is a live RIS simulation — the U is measured.

Lever two — M-capping. Temporal reuse pulls in last frame's sample. Its confidence keeps growing, so its relative weight creeps toward 1 and new samples can't refresh the chain — correlations never decay, the gauge never reaches zero, and a still scene freezes on a wrong image. Cap the confidence at M_c and correlations forget themselves.

The convergence recipe (Section 5, boxed in the paper)
Use correct MIS weights during reuse · keep f/p̂ bounded · drive Var[Σwᵢ]→0 · keep enough canonical samples · and when reusing across frames, cap the confidence. Satisfy these and ReSTIR becomes a consistent, unbiased estimator — even through glass.
Section 9 — The frontier

Where it went

GRIS turned a beloved hack into a theory. The theory immediately became a launchpad.

By giving sample reuse a rigorous foundation — unbiased contribution weights, shift maps between domains, and the Var[Σwᵢ]→0 criterion — the 2022 paper unlocked a fast-moving line of work that keeps extending what a single reused path can carry:

  • 2022 · GRIS / ReSTIR PT
    The foundation. Correlated reuse from arbitrary domains, made unbiased; reuse of full paths, even through glass.
  • 2023 · Conditional RIS
    Resampling conditioned subpaths — prefixes and suffixes — for richer unbiased path reuse.
  • 2024 · Area ReSTIR
    Reservoirs over each pixel's 4D ray space, robustifying reuse against high-frequency detail, antialiasing, and depth of field.
  • 2025 · ReSTIR PG · BDPT · subsurface
    Path guiding, bidirectional connections with caustics, and translucent materials, all on the GRIS scaffold.
  • 2026 · ToF ReSTIR · ReSTIR PT Enhanced
    Time-of-flight transients with path-length-aware shifts; sharper reconnection criteria for faster, more robust reuse.

The original paper's own open problems remain live: color noise (resampling on grayscale targets ignores chroma), MIS between shift maps, and undersampling of tiny geometry and sharp caustics in screen space.
