A real-time volume renderer that shades exactly one light path per pixel — and still converges. The trick isn't a better path; it's a better way to choose a path, by quietly borrowing good choices from your neighbours and from the past.

01 — The wall we keep hittingA single path per pixel

Here is the whole problem, and the whole solution, side by side. A slab of heterogeneous smoke lit by a scatter of coloured emitters. On the left, an honest path tracer that draws one fresh light sample per pixel each frame. On the right, the same one-sample budget — but the samples are resampled. The far panel is the converged truth.

If you have spent twenty-five years pushing rays through media, the left panel needs no introduction: it is variance, the oldest enemy. Next-event estimation toward one emitter, a stochastic transmittance estimate for the volumetric shadow, and the integral over all the other lights left to the mercy of the random number generator. A denoiser will eventually rescue it — but only if we hand it enough temporal stability to work with, and at one sample per pixel we rarely do.

The right panel spends the same single sample. The difference is that before committing to a path, it has already looked at many candidate paths and kept the most promising one — and it found those candidates not by tracing more rays, but by reusing the work its neighbours and its own previous frames already did. That is the entire idea of ReSTIR, and this essay is about what it takes to drag it from flat surfaces into a participating medium.

Before we continue, it is worth fixing a colour vocabulary, because we will lean on it constantly. Amber marks candidate samples — the things we generate cheaply and mostly throw away. Teal is transmittance T, the costly visibility-through-fog term. Lavender is the target distribution p̂, our cheap stand-in for the true integrand. Green is the one survivor we actually shade. And red is danger — bias and fireflies.

02 — What we are actually integratingThe volume as a path integral

The volume rendering equation is an integral along the primary ray, but every point on that ray launches its own integral over directions, and every scattered direction launches another. 03 — Resampled importance samplingChoosing without knowing

Suppose we want to importance-sample by a distribution p̂ that hugs the integrand beautifully but that we have no idea how to sample from. Classical importance sampling is stuck. Resampling is the way out: draw a handful of candidates from any convenient p we can sample, then pick one of them with probability proportional to how badly p under-served p̂ there.

The weight of a candidate is the mismatch ratio w=p̂/p. Select survivor x_r from the M candidates with probability w_r/Σw, and the one-sample estimator

is unbiased, with the selected x_r distributed ever closer to p̂ as M→∞. Don't take my word for it — watch it happen. Below, the integrand is the smooth curve; candidates rain down from a poor source p; resampling keeps one green survivor per batch. The histogram is the distribution of survivors.

This is the lever. We are free to make p̂ as close to the true integrand as we can afford, because we never have to invert it or integrate it — we only have to evaluate it at a few candidate points. In volume rendering, the integrand is drowning in transmittance, so a well-chosen p̂ is worth a great deal.

04 — Weighted reservoir samplingOne slot of memory

There is a catch hiding in the last estimator: to resample M candidates you must first store M candidates. At a thousand candidates per pixel across two million pixels, that is a non-starter. Thankfully, we can solve this with a streaming trick that needs exactly one slot of memory, no matter how many candidates flow past.

A reservoir holds the current survivor and the running sum of weights. Each new candidate displaces the survivor with probability equal to its weight over the new running total. That single rule reproduces the full resampling distribution exactly. Feed it as many candidates as you like; the storage never grows.

This is the move that turns resampling from a memory hog into a real-time algorithm. And it sets up the deeper idea: if a reservoir is a compact summary of “the best path I have seen so far,” then two pixels can merge their reservoirs and pool their search — without either having kept the candidates around.

06 — The key observationBias-free shortcuts

Here is the claim that makes the whole method fast, and the one most likely to make a careful engineer suspicious: using a biased, approximate transmittance inside the target p̂ does not bias the rendered image. It only changes the variance.

The reasoning is exactly the resampling lever: p̂ is a free parameter. Push every approximation, every coarsened mip, every cheap ray-march into it, and the estimator stays unbiased so long as the source p is exact wherever the integrand is nonzero and the final throughput F uses the real T. A worse p̂ simply picks survivors less wisely — more noise, never a wrong answer.

Suspicion is healthy, so let's run the experiment. The slider coarsens the transmittance used only inside p̂, from analytic down to a blocky mip. Two meters track the consequences live: the bias against the converged reference, and the variance.

This single observation is what licenses a ladder of transmittance fidelity through the pipeline: a blocky piecewise-constant estimate to generate candidates with a closed-form PDF, a filtered ray-march to resample them at lower frequency, and a full analytic evaluation only for final shading. Cheap where it must be repeated, exact where it is paid once. Every rung of that ladder is bias-free.

07 — Spatial reuseBorrowing from the neighbours

So far a pixel only resamples its own candidates. The leap is to merge reservoirs across space: a neighbour has spent its budget searching too, and its survivor is probably useful here. One subtlety the paper is careful about: a valid path for our pixel may be one a neighbour could never have generated, so naively merging darkens the image. The fix is multiple importance sampling across the reservoirs. On surfaces, the cheap stochastic MIS of the original ReSTIR suffices; in volumes it is too noisy, because the features you would use to reject bad neighbours — normals, depth — are themselves stochastic. So here we pay for the more expensive, deterministic Talbot MIS, and accept its quadratic cost over a deliberately small neighbour set.

08 — Vertex versus directionReusing a path two ways

To reuse a neighbour's path we must re-root it at our own pixel. Both paths share the camera, and we can reuse the first free-flight distance z₁ along our own primary ray. After that, two honest options diverge — and they behave very differently.

Vertex reuse keeps the neighbour's later vertices and connects to them directly. It is cheaper — no transmittance to recompute — but it drags in an unbounded geometry term G=1/r² that detonates into fireflies when the reprojected vertex lands close to a reused one. Direction reuse keeps the neighbour's angles and step lengths instead, so each segment has a bounded length and the geometry term stays tame. Drag the primary ray and watch the geometry gauge.

So the paper reuses directions by default — a small, predictable cost in exchange for far fewer fireflies. Long paths may be generated, but RIS rarely selects them: they carry little energy, so they rarely win the reservoir, which quietly bounds the average cost of direction reuse.

09 — Temporal reuseReuse across time

Reaching into the previous frame is where the most candidates come from — and where surfaces and volumes part ways most sharply. On a surface, a motion vector tells you precisely which prior pixel to consult. In a medium, there is no single correct motion vector: the smoke in one pixel may be drifting in a dozen directions at once.

The resolution is to stop pretending there is one answer and choose probabilistically. We take the motion of x₁, the first scattering vertex of the pixel's selected path, and treat it as a particle carried by the volume's velocity field. Because x₁ was itself chosen with probability tied to the target p̂(z₁), denser, brighter media — the media that actually matters to the pixel — is more likely to donate the motion vector.

Honest scope

This reprojection is unbiased only for static volumes and camera. Under motion it conditions the target PDFs on z₁, introducing a slight, generally imperceptible bias that grows with the speed of camera or deformation and vanishes a few frames after motion stops. Lowering the temporal cap Q trades accumulated samples for less of this bias. Dynamic lighting, by contrast, adds no bias — it only raises variance near sudden changes, by lowering the value of stale temporal candidates.

A last wrinkle lives at the silhouettes. Near a volume's edge the selected path may have its x₁ on the background rather than in the smoke, so it inherits the background's motion and smears a halo along the rim. Velocity resampling fixes this: when x₁ is not in the medium, draw a fresh in-volume distance proportional to the free-flight density and take that particle's motion instead, so the reprojection follows the smoke and not the wall behind it.

10 — Putting it togetherThe whole machine

Four passes, each one a round of resampling that refines both the chosen path and the fidelity of the transmittance attached to it. Initial candidates from a blocky volume with a closed-form PDF; a temporal merge; a spatial merge; and one final, exact shade.

If you wanted to build a toy of this in Houdini

The skeleton is smaller than it looks, and every piece appeared above. Per pixel, per frame:

build-your-own · the four rungs

1 · generate — a few random walks on a downsampled, piecewise-constant density grid (regular tracking gives the closed-form PDF you need). Each vertex spawns a scattering and an emission candidate. Stream them through one reservoir.
2 · temporal — reproject via the motion of x₁, merge last frame's reservoir, cap the history at Q.
3 · spatial — merge a handful of neighbours within a small radius using direction reuse and Talbot MIS; raise the target's transmittance to a filtered ray-march here.
4 · shade — evaluate the one survivor with analytic, unbiased T. Apply the correction E=T/T̃. Hand the result to your denoiser.

Store each reservoir as the path's (zᵢ,ωᵢ) tuples, bounded by your bounce limit; the memory is fixed. Pick M=4 walks, Q=4, three neighbours in a ten-pixel radius — the paper's defaults — and tune from there.

What is worth carrying away is not any single trick but the shape of the idea. Resampling turns a distribution we cannot sample into one we can choose from; the reservoir makes that choice cost one slot of memory; spatial and temporal reuse make the candidate pool effectively enormous at near-zero marginal cost; and the freedom to put any approximation inside the target — proven bias-free above — is what lets all of it run at frame rate. A volume that once needed thousands of samples to look quiet now needs one, because the one it keeps is the one its neighbours and its own past already vouched for. Drag a light in the opening figure again, now that you know what the right-hand panel is really doing. It is not tracing better paths. It is remembering better choices.
