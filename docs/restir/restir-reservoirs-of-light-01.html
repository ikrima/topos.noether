You can write the integral for direct lighting in one line.
You cannot sample its optimum. Everything else follows from that gap.

A surface point glows because light reaches it — from emitters near and far, bright and dim, visible and hidden. To shade one pixel we must add up all of that incoming light, weighted by how the surface scatters it toward the eye. That sum is an integral, and below is its anatomy. Drag the shading point. Throw Monte Carlo darts at the lights. Watch how the choice of where to aim the darts decides whether the picture is clean or a blizzard of noise.

Left: the scene; ray brightness encodes the current integrand value toward each light. Right: the contribution of each light, term by term. With all factors on, this is the function we wish we could sample from.
  • ρ — the BSDF. How the surface scatters light from a given direction toward the eye. Here a Lambertian surface, so ρ is constant, but it folds in the cosine at the shading point: grazing light counts less.
  • Lₑ — emitted radiance. How bright the source is. The only factor a naïve “sample the bright lights” strategy looks at — and, as we will see, the most misleading one alone.
  • G — the geometry term, . Inverse-square falloff and the two foreshortening cosines. A brilliant light far away or edge-on contributes almost nothing.
  • V — visibility, 0 or 1. Is anything in the way? This is the factor that makes the problem hard: you cannot know V without tracing a ray into the scene.
§2 · Estimating the integral

Importance sampling

We cannot evaluate the integral in closed form — the geometry alone forbids it, and visibility certainly does. So we estimate it. Draw N samples from some probability density we can sample, and average the integrand divided by that density:

This estimator is unbiased for any p that is positive wherever f is — its expected value is exactly L, no matter how badly chosen p is. What a bad p costs you is variance: the dividing-by-p turns regions where p is small but f is large into rare, enormous spikes. The closer p tracks the shape of f, the flatter the ratio , the less it varies, the cleaner the image.

The whole game

Bias is about being correct on average; variance is about how much noise you see now. In real time you get a handful of samples, so variance is the image quality. The entire ReSTIR story is a series of increasingly clever answers to one question: how do we make p look like f without being able to sample f?

The single-sample estimate jitters from dart to dart. Throw a few hundred and watch the running mean home in on the true L regardless of strategy (that is unbiasedness) — but watch how fast, and how steady the noise tile is. That spread is variance, and it is the only thing that separates a usable frame from a blizzard.

§3 · The gap between desire and capability

The one you want vs.
the ones you can build

If we could sample exactly proportional to f, the ratio would be a constant and every single dart would return the exact answer: zero variance. That is the dream. Here is why it is impossible, ranked by how close each buildable strategy gets:

Variance of each strategy · the same toy scene, with occluder
Bars are relative variance (log scale); tiles are the resulting noise. Toggle visibility off and watch ∝ ρLₑG snap to zero — without V, the unshadowed product is the integrand.
  • ∝ Lₑ — power sampling. Easy: pick a light with probability proportional to its emitted power, then a point on it. But it is blind to ρ, G, and V — so a bright, distant, edge-on, or hidden light still gets all the darts. In our scene it is worse than uniform.
  • ∝ ρLₑG — the unshadowed contribution. This is the target distribution we will chase for the rest of the series. It folds in geometry and the BSDF and slashes variance by an order of magnitude. Two problems remain: it ignores V (the residual noise you still see), and — critically — for many lights or textured area emitters there is no practical algorithm to sample exactly from it. Its normalization is itself an integral.
  • ∝ f — the ideal. Zero variance. Unbuildable twice over: evaluating f requires V, i.e. a ray traced per candidate, and sampling proportional to it requires the answer we are trying to compute.
The crack we will pry open

We can evaluate at any point cheaply. We just cannot draw samples from it. Resampled importance sampling — Part II — turns that one asymmetry into an algorithm: generate cheap candidates, then resample them toward the target you can only evaluate.

§4 · When one technique is not enough

Many techniques, one estimator

Often no single p is good everywhere. Light sampling shines for small bright sources; BSDF or solid-angle sampling shines for glossy highlights and nearby geometry. Multiple importance sampling (Veach & Guibas) lets us combine strategies and stay unbiased, by weighting each sample with weights that form a partition of unity. The balance heuristic,

The mixture curve dips below both endpoints: the balance heuristic is doing real work, not averaging. This is the right answer — for two techniques.

So why not just pile on techniques? Because Veach-style MIS across many candidate strategies costs quadratically: every sample must be weighed against every technique's density, and here each density evaluation can mean tracing a ray. With a handful of strategies that is fine. With thousands of reused samples — which is exactly where ReSTIR is headed — it is fatal. Part V returns to this with a cheaper MIS built for the job.

For the probabilist — RIS as the unbiasing of a ratio estimator

The estimator we will build in Part II is, structurally, a ratio estimator — the kind used in survey statistics since the 1950s, and in graphics as weighted importance sampling (Bekaert) and ratio estimators (Heitz). A one-sample WIS estimator is ; resampled importance sampling is exactly that times a correlated correction factor , which is an unbiased Monte-Carlo estimate of 's normalization. That extra factor is what turns a consistent-but-biased ratio estimator into an unbiased one. We will derive it from scratch; flag this as the bridge back to a literature you may already know.

§5 · Make it yours

Exercises & the road ahead

The widgets above are the laboratory; these are experiments to run in them.

i
Break power sampling on purpose. In the hero widget, drag the brightest light far away and edge-on while keeping a dim light close and frontal. Predict, then read off, how much worse ∝ Lₑ gets relative to ∝ ρLₑG. Why does brightness mislead?
ii
Find the residual. Turn the occluder off in the variance ladder. ∝ ρLₑG drops to zero variance. Turn it back on. The variance that reappears is exactly the part of the integrand that cannot see. Name it.
iii
Watch unbiasedness vs. variance. In the convergence widget, stream a few hundred darts under ∝ area and under ∝ ρLₑG. Both means reach the same L. Time how many samples each needs to get within 5%.
iv
Beat both with neither. In the MIS mixer, find the weight that minimizes mixture variance. Is it 0.5? What does that tell you about which technique is carrying the scene?
