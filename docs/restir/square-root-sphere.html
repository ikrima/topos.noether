Information geometry · explorable

Every density is a point
on a sphere.

Take the square root of a probability distribution and it lands on the unit sphere of L². For a family with three outcomes, that sphere is an honest octant you can hold in your hand — and the Fisher–Rao metric becomes nothing more exotic than arc length on it. Every divergence you can write down is then a way of approximating an arc by a chord.

The claim worth seeing rather than reading: near any base point, all of those approximations — KL, reverse KL, χ², Hellinger — agree to second order. They osculate the same parabola. Below, the geometry is on the left, the agreement is a chart you can drag, and nothing is drawn that wasn't first checked numerically.

01The square-root map

Fix the sample space {1, 2, 3} with counting measure. A distribution is — a point in the 2-simplex Δ², the densities being just the Radon–Nikodym derivatives . Now send each distribution to the vector of square roots:

The constraint becomes the equation of the unit sphere, and because every coordinate is non-negative, the simplex maps exactly onto the positive octant of S². The three corners e₁, e₂, e₃ (the deterministic distributions) become the three axis points √eᵢ.

Two facts make this the right picture, not just a cute one. The angle between two square-root points is set by the Bhattacharyya coefficient, and Hellinger distance is literally the straight chord between them:

So the Fisher–Rao distance is the great-circle arc, and Hellinger is the chord that cuts beneath it. The whole story is arc versus chord on a sphere.

04Why this metric

The square-root picture explains the coincidence — every smooth divergence is a second-order probe of the same round sphere, so they must agree to second order. But it also hints at the deeper fact: this metric is not a choice, it is the choice. Čencov's theorem says the Fisher–Rao metric is, up to scale, the unique Riemannian metric invariant under Markov morphisms — stochastic maps between sample spaces.

Read at the infinitesimal level, that invariance splits exactly as you'd hope: maps coming from sufficient statistics preserve the metric (no information lost, distinguishability unchanged), while general coarse-graining kernels can only decrease it — which is the data-processing inequality. Fisher–Rao is forced by functoriality with respect to stochastic maps, not selected for convenience.

One honest caveat, since it matters: Čencov's clean uniqueness lives on finite sample spaces — exactly the categorical-3 family drawn here. The extension to infinite-dimensional families is true but heavier, and always only up to a positive scalar. The octant is where the theorem is at its most literal, which is part of why it's the right place to build intuition.

05Honesty ledger

What is exact, what is approximate, and where a convention is doing quiet work. Every numeric relation below was verified in Node before any pixel was drawn (25/25 checks).

exact

√-map onto the sphere. ‖ψ(P)‖² = Σpᵢ = 1 identically; the simplex is in genuine bijection with the closed positive octant of S². No discretization or approximation — this family's L² sphere is 3-dimensional and fully drawn.

exact

BC = cosine, Hellinger = chord, FR = arc. cos∠ = BC, ‖ψP−ψQ‖ = 2sin(∠/2) = √2·H, and d_FR = 2∠ all hold to machine precision. The factor-of-2 in d_FR = 2arccos BC is the convention gᶠᴿ = 4·(round metric); the round-sphere arc shown in the inset readout is therefore d_FR / 2.

exact

Common quadratic. Each s_f = √(2D_f/f″(1)) is tangent to y = s at the origin (verified: s_f/s → 1 for all four divergences). This is the "second-order coincidence" made literal.

subtle

Order of departure differs. The agreement is second-order for all divergences, but they peel off the diagonal at different rates: asymmetric divergences (KL, reverse KL, χ²) depart at order s²; symmetric ones (Hellinger — and Jensen–Shannon) at order s³. Measured numerically as departure exponents ≈ 2.1 vs ≈ 3.0. The chart shows this as the symmetric curve hugging the diagonal visibly longer.

scope

Čencov uniqueness is stated for finite spaces. The categorical-3 family is exactly that regime, so the theorem applies literally here. Generalization to continuous families holds but is technically heavier and always only up to a positive scalar — don't over-read the picture as a turnkey infinite-dimensional result.

viz

Indicatrix ellipses are unit balls of gᶠᴿ. Drawn at fixed metric radius r = 0.085 via the exact eigendecomposition of the Fisher matrix, mapped through the (linear) barycentric embedding. They shrink near the boundary because the metric blows up there (eigenvalues → ∞), i.e. the edges sit at infinite Fisher–Rao distance — the same reason the sphere octant's edges are a finite arc away but the open simplex never reaches them.
