Part II resampled a pool — but it had to hold the pool.
A reservoir keeps one sample, sees the candidates once, and never looks back.

Resampling a pool of M candidates means storing M candidates. For one integral that is fine; for a few million pixels, sixty times a second, it is impossible. Weighted reservoir sampling dissolves the problem. It watches the candidates stream past one at a time, holds exactly one survivor and a running weight-sum, and — with a single coin flip per arrival — keeps a survivor distributed identically to the full resampling of Part II. Watch the stream pour through the slot. The histogram it builds is the same p̂ as before; the memory it uses never grows.

Two reservoirs, each summarizing its own stream, can also be merged in constant time into one reservoir that behaves as if it had seen both streams. That single fact — proved exactly below — is what lets a pixel borrow its neighbors' work, and is the structural heart of ReSTIR.

§1 · One arrival at a time

The update rule

§2 · What the coin flips add up to

The invariant

And because each term only ever divides by the weight-sum so far, the result does not depend on the order the candidates arrived. Run thousands of reservoirs over the same weighted set and the empirical keep-rates land on the theoretical shares — reorder the stream and nothing moves.

§4 · Combining summaries

The merge

Here is the property everything has been building toward. Suppose two reservoirs each digested their own stream. We can combine them without revisiting a single original candidate: feed each reservoir's survivor into a fresh reservoir as one weighted candidate, using the weight

which — and this is an exact algebraic identity, not an approximation — equals that reservoir's own weight-sum. So merging reproduces precisely the weight-sum the concatenated stream would have had. The result is distributionally identical to having streamed both candidate sets through a single reservoir. Build two reservoirs, merge them, and compare the survivor to a single reservoir fed the same total — they are the same distribution.

The fine print that becomes Part V

This exactness assumes both reservoirs targeted the same . The instant we merge a neighbor's reservoir — whose differs, because its surface point and shadows differ — the weight-sum we reconstruct is for the wrong target, and bias creeps in. Holding that thread is the entire job of Part V.

§5 · The payoff

Why this is the whole game

Assemble the three facts. A reservoir summarizes a stream of any length in constant memory. Its survivor is exactly proportional to the target, independent of order. And two reservoirs merge in constant time into one that behaves as their concatenation. Stack those together and something remarkable becomes affordable: a pixel can merge its own reservoir with those of its spatial neighbors and with its own reservoir from previous frames — each merge folding in another stream's worth of candidates — so the effective number of candidates behind every pixel grows explosively while the per-pixel cost stays flat.

One pixel, merging across space and time
Each neighbor and each past frame is a reservoir; merging them costs O(1) apiece, yet the candidate pool behind the center pixel compounds. Part IV turns this into the spatiotemporal reuse that gives ReSTIR its name.
For the implementer — why M is clamped in practice

Temporal reuse merges this frame's reservoir with last frame's, which already merged the frame before, and so on — a geometric accumulation of M. Left unchecked, the history term's M dominates every merge and the estimator stops responding to new information (and stale, now-occluded samples linger). ReSTIR caps the temporal reservoir's M — the paper uses roughly the current frame's count — before merging, trading a controlled bias for responsiveness. It is the one place the method deliberately forgets, and it is why a moving light still produces crisp shadows rather than smears.

§6 · Make it yours

Exercises & the road ahead

The reservoir is four numbers and a coin. Convince yourself it does what the algebra claims.

i
Order cannot matter. In the invariant widget, switch between forward, reversed, and shuffled. Predict the keep-rates before each run. Why does dividing by the weight-sum so far make ordering irrelevant?
ii
Heavy and late beats heavy and early. In the step widget, build a stream whose largest weight arrives last. Watch its acceptance probability. Then make it arrive first. The survivor distribution is unchanged — but explain why the per-step coin looks so different.
iii
The identity is exact. In the merge widget, hit "one merge" repeatedly and read the identity error. It stays at machine zero. Derive from the definition of W in three steps.
iv
Merging is free candidates. Set the merge widget to M = 8 per side and confirm the merged survivor matches a single 16-candidate stream. What does this imply about merging eight neighbors at once?
