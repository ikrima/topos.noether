Reuse Across Space and Time

When sharing samples goes wrong — and why correlation breaks convergence

In Essay 1, we built a reservoir at a single pixel: draw candidates, resample toward a target, and produce a near-optimal sample. Beautiful. But one reservoir per pixel, working in isolation, still means one effective sample per pixel per frame.

What if neighboring pixels could share their reservoirs? A pixel in shadow could borrow a well-chosen light sample from its sunlit neighbor. A pixel in frame 47 could inherit the carefully refined reservoir from frame 46. This is the core insight of ReSTIR [3]: by pouring samples across spatial and temporal boundaries, each pixel effectively draws from a pool of hundreds or thousands of candidates — even though it only generates one new sample per frame.

The performance gains are extraordinary. But there's a trap hiding in this scheme, and it's subtle enough that it fooled the research community for years.

The effective candidate count grows exponentially with reuse passes: with \(k\) neighbors and \(r\) passes, each pixel draws from roughly \(k^r\) candidates. Two passes with 4 neighbors gives 16× the sample pool — from 1 candidate to 16 — for almost no additional cost beyond the resampling weights.

In rendering, this is the difference between 1 sample-per-pixel noise (unwatchable) and 16+ sample-per-pixel quality (production real-time) — achieved at 1 spp cost.

So what's the catch?

The Two-Pixel Catastrophe

Let's strip everything down to the simplest possible case: two pixels, two different integrands, unlimited iterations of mutual reuse. This is Figure 2 from the GRIS paper [3], animated so you can watch it fail in real time.

What you just witnessed: Pixel B's estimate converges — but to the wrong value. It's not noisy. It's not slowly improving. It has settled, confidently, on an incorrect answer. This is bias, not variance, and no amount of additional iterations will fix it. The estimator has fundamentally broken.

Why does this happen? The root cause is sample duplication. When pixel A shares its reservoir sample with pixel B, and then pixel B shares back with pixel A in the next iteration, the same underlying random draw circulates through the system. After many iterations, both pixels may hold the same sample — not the same sample value coincidentally, but literally the same random realization, having been passed back and forth like a rumor.

The contribution weight formula \(W_Y = \frac{1}{\hat{p}(Y)} \cdot \frac{\sum w_j}{M}\) assumes the \(M\) candidates were independent. When the "candidates" are copies of the same underlying draw, the effective sample count is 1, regardless of how many times we've resampled. But the formula thinks it's \(M\). This mismatch is where the bias enters.

$$\text{Bias} \propto \frac{\text{Var}\left[\sum w_j\right]}{M} \quad \text{(does NOT vanish when samples are correlated)}$$

Drag the overlap slider above. When the two integrands overlap perfectly (100%), reuse is safe — a good sample for A is also good for B. When they differ (low overlap), the catastrophe emerges. This is the fundamental tension: reuse helps most when integrands differ (complementary information), but fails most in exactly that case.

In the extreme (100% reuse, no fresh samples), variance doesn't decrease at all. You're just recirculating the same information forever, like an echo chamber.

Temporal Reuse: Memory and Staleness

Spatial reuse shares across pixels within one frame. Temporal reuse shares across frames at the same pixel. Your reservoir from frame \(t-1\) carries forward into frame \(t\), combining with the new sample from the current frame. Over time, the reservoir accumulates history from dozens of frames — effectively integrating over time.

This works beautifully for static scenes. But when the scene moves — when the integrand changes — old samples become stale. They were good for the old integrand but misleading for the new one. The M-cap limits how much history the reservoir remembers, trading convergence speed for adaptivity.

At zero motion, watch how higher M-cap gives better convergence — the reservoir remembers more history, and since nothing changed, all that history is valid. Now increase the motion slider. The integrand starts drifting, and suddenly high M-cap becomes a liability: the reservoir clings to stale information, and the estimate lags behind the truth.

Low M-cap (say 5-10) forgets quickly, adapting to change but never achieving deep convergence. High M-cap (50+) converges deeply but can't track motion. This is a fundamental tradeoff with no universal optimum — it must be tuned to the expected rate of scene change.

The M-cap tradeoff for the renderer: In practice, ReSTIR implementations use M-cap ≈ 20-30 for typical real-time scenes with moderate camera and object motion. For cutscenes with slow camera movements, you can afford higher values. For fast-action sequences, lower caps prevent ghosting artifacts that manifest as "sticky" bright/dark regions [3].

Why This Matters for GRIS

The two-pixel catastrophe isn't a theoretical curiosity — it manifests in every real ReSTIR implementation as bias in regions where adjacent pixels have very different integrands. Glossy-to-diffuse boundaries, shadow edges, depth discontinuities — anywhere the lighting environment changes sharply between neighbors.

The original ReSTIR papers [3] acknowledged this bias and accepted it as a practical tradeoff. GRIS asks: can we fix it properly?

The answer, as we'll see in Essay 3, requires two ingredients:

First, we need shift mappings that properly transform a sample from one pixel's domain into another pixel's domain — not just copying the value, but correctly accounting for the geometric and probabilistic differences between their integration domains.

Second, we need proper MIS weights that account for the fact that our candidates are not independent — they came from correlated reservoirs at different pixels and frames, each with their own history and their own biases. GRIS provides a framework where, if the MIS weights satisfy certain conditions, the estimator provably converges despite the correlation [3].

But first, we needed to see the problem clearly. We needed to watch convergence fail. That failure — a confident, stable, wrong answer — is what makes the GRIS theory necessary. Without the catastrophe, the theory is just overhead. With it, the theory is salvation.
