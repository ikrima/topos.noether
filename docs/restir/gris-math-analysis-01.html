§ 1 · Preliminaries

The resampling estimator

We carry in just enough machinery to read §S.1. Resampling takes a pile of \(M\) candidate samples and keeps exactly one, the survivor \(Y\), to represent the integral. Each candidate earns a resampling weight that decides its chance of being chosen.

Candidate \(i\) is a random variable \(X_i\) living in its own domain. A shift mapping \(T_i\) carries it into the shared target domain, producing \(Y_i = T_i(X_i)\); the price of that change of variables is the Jacobian \(\bigl|\partial T_i/\partial X_i\bigr|\). A distinguished subset \(R\) of indices are canonical: their domain is already \(\Omega\), their shift is the identity, and their target density is the shared \(\hat p\). Crucially, candidate \(i\) carries not a probability density but an unbiased contribution weight \(W_i\), a random variable standing in for the unknown \(1/p_i\). The resampling weight is then exactly

\[ w_i \;=\; \underbrace{m_i(Y_i)}_{\text{MIS weight}}\;\cdot\;\underbrace{\hat p(Y_i)}_{\text{target}}\;\cdot\;\underbrace{W_i}_{\text{UCW}}\;\cdot\;\underbrace{\Bigl|\tfrac{\partial Y_i}{\partial X_i}\Bigr|}_{\text{Jacobian}}\qquad\text{(Eq. 19 / S.1)} \]

Everything in Movement I turns on the first factor, \(m_i\) — the resampling multiple-importance-sampling weight. The others are given. The whole question is: what should \(m_i\) be? Tug the four factors below to feel how they compose into a single weight.

§ 2 · The premise

A demand for boundedness

A resampling scheme is only usable if no single candidate can dominate with unbounded weight — otherwise variance explodes and the survivor is chosen by a lottery a single outlier always wins. So the supplement imposes one requirement and lets it dictate \(m_i\).

Suppose each candidate's contribution is capped, \(\hat p_i(X_i)\,W_i \le C_i\). Substituting into the weight and rewriting through the shift gives the chain of §S.1.1: the target at \(Y_i\) can be re-expressed using any canonical index \(j\), because a canonical shift is the identity. This rewrites both numerator and denominator in the same currency — the pulled-back densities \(\hat p_{\leftarrow i}\). The requirement \(w_i \le \tilde C_i\) becomes a ceiling on \(m_i\):

\[ m_i(Y_i)\;\le\;\frac{\tilde C_i}{C_i}\,\frac{\hat p_{\leftarrow i}(Y_i)}{\hat p_{\leftarrow j}(Y_i)}\qquad\text{(S.6, per canonical }j) \]

Here \(\hat p_{\leftarrow i}(y) = \hat p_i\!\bigl(T_i^{-1}(y)\bigr)\,\bigl|\partial T_i^{-1}/\partial y\bigr|\) is the target density of candidate \(i\) pulled back into the shared domain — "the density \(\hat p\) as seen from \(i\)." The inequality holds for every canonical \(j\) at once. We are free to enlarge the denominator and still satisfy it. That single freedom is the whole derivation.

Why this is the right leverA larger denominator only ever relaxes the ceiling on \(m_i\). So we may replace the single canonical \(\hat p_{\leftarrow j}\) with any larger, symmetric quantity built from all the candidates — and symmetry is exactly what a fair MIS weight needs.
§ 3 · The first construction

Symmetry, and Talbot MIS

Replace the lone denominator term by the sum over every candidate. The ceiling still holds, the result is symmetric in all indices, and — setting \(\tilde C_i = C_i\) — it lands on the generalized Talbot weight.

\[ m_i(y)\;=\;\frac{\hat p_{\leftarrow i}(y)}{\sum_{j=1}^{M}\hat p_{\leftarrow j}(y)}\qquad\text{(S.7)} \]

By construction these are non-negative and sum to one over the indices that could have generated \(y\) — a genuine partition of unity. It is the balance heuristic, reincarnated for resampling: each candidate's weight is its own pulled-back density as a fraction of the whole chorus. Below, shape each candidate's \(\hat p_{\leftarrow j}\) and watch the denominator assemble and the chosen \(m_i\) resolve. Canonical candidates (teal) are pinned to the shared \(\hat p\).

§ 4 · The cheaper kin

Pairwise MIS: the cheaper kin

Talbot's denominator sums over all \(M\) candidates, for each of \(M\) weights: \(O(M^2)\) target evaluations. With thousands of reused paths that is ruinous. §S.1.2 takes the same boundedness ceiling but enlarges the denominator only by the candidate's own term plus a canonical reference — comparing each non-canonical sample against the canonical ones alone.

\[ m_i(y)=\beta_i\,\frac{\hat p_{\leftarrow i}(y)}{\hat p(y)+\alpha_i\,\hat p_{\leftarrow i}(y)}\quad(i\notin R),\qquad m_i=\tfrac{1}{|R|}\Bigl(1-\!\!\sum_{j\notin R}\! m_j\Bigr)\quad(i\in R) \]

Fixing the parameters across indices and writing \(\kappa=\sum_{i\notin R}\beta_i/\alpha_i\) yields a one-parameter family interpolating between two honest extremes: at \(\kappa=0\), always defer to a canonical sample; at \(\kappa=1\), a fundamental MIS scheme set by \(\alpha\). The i.i.d. sanity check — every candidate equally trustworthy — forces \(\alpha = M/|R|-1\) and recovers \(m_i = 1/M\). The defensive choice \(\kappa=(M-|R|)/M\) keeps canonical weights at least as large as the rest.

§ 5 · The payoff of the premise

The weight, bounded

We began by demanding a bound. Does the Talbot weight actually deliver one? §S.1.3 substitutes \(m_i\) back into \(w_i\) and collapses it, step by step, to \(C_i/|R|\). Step through the cancellation — each move is one of: invoke the pullback identity, cancel the Jacobian, split the denominator into its canonical and non-canonical halves, then discard non-negative terms.

The same destination is reached for Pairwise MIS across the feasible parameter range \(0<\alpha\le M/|R|-1,\ 0\le\kappa\le1\) (§S.1.3): for canonical and non-canonical samples alike, \(w_i \le C_i/|R|\). The bound is not a property of one scheme — it is the shared guarantee the whole family was engineered to provide.

§ 6 · A practical detour

When PDFs are tractable

Sometimes a candidate's marginal density \(p_i\) is, after all, computable. §S.1.4 swaps each intractable \(\hat p_{\leftarrow i}\) for the honest pullback \(p_{\leftarrow i}\), so Talbot becomes \(m_i = p_{\leftarrow i}/\sum_j p_{\leftarrow j}\).

Pairwise needs slightly more care: its denominator carries a bare \(\hat p(y)\) whose normalization may not match the \(p_i\). The fix is to anchor it with a fixed canonical importance sampler \(c\) satisfying \(\hat p(y)\le C_c\,p_c(y)\), replacing \(\hat p\) by \(p_c\) inside the weight. The resampling-weight bound survives, loosening only to

\[ w_i\;\le\;\frac{1}{|R|}\,\max_{j\in R} C_j\qquad\text{(S.58)} \]

covering both families at once. The lesson of the whole section: tractability is a convenience, not a requirement. The contribution weight \(W_i\) was always the load-bearing object — the densities are optional scaffolding.

§ 7 · The fine print

The constraints that keep it honest

Two short proofs (§S.7.2, §S.7.3) guard the edges. They are not afterthoughts: violate either and the estimator silently stops integrating \(f\).

Non-negativity is forced, not assumed

Selection probabilities are \(w_i/\sum_j w_j\). Dividing by a common denominator preserves sign, so all \(w_i\) must share one sign — take it positive. Since \(w_i = m_i\,\hat p\,W_i\,|\partial T/\partial X|\) and \(\hat p\), the Jacobian, and \(1/p_{X_i}\) are already non-negative, the product can only stay non-negative if \(m_i \ge 0\) and \(W_i \ge 0\). The non-negativity we relied on for the partition of unity is not a wish — it is compelled by the probabilities themselves.

MIS must be positive wherever it matters

Unbiasedness (§S.7.3) needs more than non-negativity: \(m_i(y)>0\) whenever the contribution weight \(c_i(y)\ne 0\). If \(m_i\) were allowed to vanish on part of \(c_i\)'s support, the inner sum that should collapse to one would fall short, and the estimate would land below the true integral. The slider makes the failure visible.

Movement I built the weights and proved them bounded, non-negative, and a partition of unity. We have not yet shown the resulting estimator is correct, nor what happens as \(M\to\infty\). That is the next movement.
