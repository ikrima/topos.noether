§4 · The weight that cancels

The balance heuristic

The cure is the balance heuristic — weight each source by its share of the total density at the sample:

Substitute it into the resampling weight and something quietly perfect happens. The offending that blew up the naïve estimator cancels:

The weight no longer divides by the source's own density — it divides by the sum of all densities, which is never small where any source is present. A rare sample from a near-zero source is now weighted by how plausible it was under every source, so it can never detonate. Unbiased, like naïve; bounded, unlike naïve; and — as you saw — lower variance than either. This is why ReSTIR's reuse is built on the balance heuristic and nothing else.

§5 · What it costs

The price: O(M) vs O(M²)

Nothing is free. To compute the balance weight for the survivor y, we need — every source's density evaluated at y. And in a renderer, evaluating a source's target at a point means asking whether that point's light is visible from there: a pdf evaluation is a shadow ray. For one survivor against M sources, that is M rays — per pixel.

Why the balance heuristic is the only affordable MIS here
Talbot-style MIS weights every candidate against every technique — shadow rays, infeasible at ReSTIR's sample counts. Evaluating the balance weight for only the selected survivor is . That single asymmetry — weight the survivor, not every candidate — is what keeps unbiased reuse within budget.

So the final algorithm is exactly the reuse of Part IV with one substitution: when a reservoir absorbs a neighbor, the merge weight is scaled by the balance term, and the survivor's contribution weight divides by rather than by M. A handful of extra shadow rays per pixel buys an unbiased image. The seam is gone, the shadows are crisp, the noise is low — at sixty frames a second.

Coda

The whole arc

Five steps, each forced by the failure of the last. We wanted to sample proportional to the integrand and couldn't — visibility made the optimum unbuildable. So we generated cheap candidates and resampled toward a target we could only evaluate. To do that per-pixel in real time we streamed the candidates through a single reservoir, which turned out to merge in constant time — so a pixel could borrow from its neighbors and its past, inheriting thousands of effective candidates for a few rays. And because those neighbors aimed at different targets, the borrowing introduced a bias, which a single partition-of-unity weight — the same one we started with — removed.

Reservoirs of Light · the five rungs
Importance sampling → resampling → the reservoir and its merge → spatiotemporal reuse → the unbiasing weight. Each rung earns the next; the balance heuristic bookends the climb.
i
Measure the seam. In the hero, biased mode, read the reconstruction on the right half against the target. Confirm it is halved — the you derived in §1.
ii
Watch naïve detonate. Flip near-zero on with naïve weighting and crank samples down. The variance readout jumps; the strip turns to grain on the right. Now switch to MIS — same near-zero source, quiet image. Explain the cancellation in one line.
iii
Find MIS beating naïve even when both are unbiased. Compare the variance readouts under the sharp cutoff. MIS is already lower. Why does weighting by density share help even with no near-zero tail?
iv
Count the rays. For M = 16 reused neighbors, how many shadow rays does the balance weight cost per pixel? How many would Talbot's MIS cost? That ratio is why ReSTIR exists.

From an integral no one can sample to a clean frame in fifteen milliseconds — by never solving the problem, only resampling it, remembering one sample, sharing it, and paying an honest toll to keep the sharing fair.
