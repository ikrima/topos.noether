Convergence
Under Correlation

Every theorem of Movement II rested on a quiet assumption: that the samples were independent. ReSTIR's entire premise — reuse a neighbor's path, reuse last frame's — destroys that independence. This is where the supplement earns its title, proving the estimator still converges when its inputs are entangled. Then it descends from theory to the 88 bytes that make it run.

§ 1 · The stakes

The broken assumption

Movement II's hypothesis (Eq. 58) was that \(\operatorname{Var}\bigl[\hat p(Y)\,W_Y\bigr]\to0\). Since \(\sum_{i=1}^M w_i = \hat p(Y)\,W_Y\), this is a statement about the variance of a sum of resampling weights. With independent samples it is routine. But reuse correlates them, and a sum of correlated terms carries covariances that need not vanish.

§S.2 grants convergence under three conditions — each one already in hand from the earlier movements or cheap to arrange in practice:

\[ \text{(1)}\ \ \frac{|R|}{M}\ge\gamma>0 \qquad \text{(2)}\ \ w_i\le \frac{C}{|R|} \qquad \text{(3)}\ \ \rho_{i,i+k}\le b_k,\ \ b_k\to0 \]

Condition (2) is exactly the bound Movement I built. Condition (1) says canonical samples never become a vanishing fraction. Condition (3) is the only new demand: correlation between samples \(k\) apart must decay — distant reuse must eventually forget. Under these, \(\operatorname{Var}[\sum_i w_i]\to0\), and Movement II's whole edifice stands back up.

§ 2 · Theorem of §S.2

The variance of a correlated sum

Write the variance of the sum exactly — diagonal variances plus off-diagonal covariances:

\[ \operatorname{Var}\!\Bigl[\sum_{i=1}^M w_i\Bigr]=\sum_{i=1}^M\operatorname{Var}[w_i]\;+\;2\sum_{i=1}^M\sum_{k=1}^{M-i}\operatorname{Cov}(w_i,w_{i+k}). \]

Here is the crux, and it is best felt than read. Because \(w_i\le C/|R|\) and \(|R|\ge\gamma M\), each weight shrinks like \(1/M\) as candidates accumulate. There are \(M\) of them, summing to something \(O(1)\) — but the variance of that sum is governed by whether the correlations fade. Shape the correlation decay below and watch the verdict: with decay, the sum's variance is driven to zero; without it, the variance stalls at a floor that more samples never clear.

§ 3 · The mechanism

Why it dies: Popoviciu & Cesàro

Two classical tools finish the proof. Popoviciu's inequality caps the variance of any variable bounded in \([0,C/|R|]\) by \(C^2/(4|R|^2)\), so the diagonal sum is at most \(C^2/(4\gamma^2M)\) — vanishing on its own. The Cesàro mean handles the rest: if \(b_k\to0\), then its running average \(\tfrac1M\sum_k b_k\to0\) too, and the covariance term is squeezed to nothing.

\[ \underbrace{\sum_i\operatorname{Var}[w_i]}_{\le\,C^2/(4\gamma^2 M)}\;+\;\underbrace{2\sum_i\sum_k\operatorname{Cov}}_{\le\,\frac{C^2}{2\gamma^2}\,\frac1M\sum_k b_k}\;\xrightarrow{M\to\infty}\;0 \]

Below, the left panel shows the correlation sequence \(b_k\) (bars) and its running Cesàro mean (line). The right shows the two bounded terms of the variance. With decay, both collapse. With constant correlation, the Cesàro mean — and so the covariance term — refuses to fall.

§ 4 · §S.3

What a sample really is

The theory above treats a "sample" abstractly. §S.3 grounds it: every path is a deterministic function of a primary sample sequence — a stream of uniform random numbers \(\bar u=(U_1,U_2,\dots)\). The path tracer reads these numbers and builds a path; the integral becomes an expectation over \(\bar u\).

\[ F(\bar u)=\sum_{d=1}^{\infty}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\omega_{d,n}\bigl(x_{d,n}(\bar u)\bigr)\,\frac{f\bigl(x_{d,n}(\bar u)\bigr)}{p_{d,n}\bigl(x_{d,n}(\bar u)\bigr)}\qquad\text{(S.77)} \]

Each length \(d\) and strategy \(n\) (next-event estimation, BSDF continuation) contributes, blended by an MIS weight \(\omega_{d,n}\). The point for resampling: because a path is a function of its random numbers, two pixels' paths can be compared, shifted, and reused by manipulating those numbers. Scrub the primary samples below and watch the path rebuild — the same \(\bar u\) always yields the same path, which is exactly what makes reuse well-defined.

§ 5 · §S.4

The reservoir

All of this lives in one small struct, held per pixel. §S.4's reservoir is 88 bytes — and most of it is not the sample itself but the machinery to reconnect a reused path to the base path's geometry.

The core is tiny: a confidence weight \(M\), the unbiased contribution weight \(W\), and a cached integrand value \(F\). Everything else — the reconnection vertex's seed, hit identifiers, barycentrics, outgoing direction, incident radiance, and cached Jacobian terms — exists so an offset path can be rebuilt and its shift Jacobian evaluated. Hover a field to see its role and cost.

§ 6 · §S.6

Choosing the parameters

Theory says reuse more and gain more; reality is a trade-off. §S.6 explores it. The clean part is a closed-form efficiency model for reusing \(K\) pixels (including self) at \(S\) candidates each, path length \(L\), resample cost \(\eta\):

\[ \frac{\#\text{ rays gained}}{\#\text{ rays computed}}=\frac{K\,S\,L}{S\,L+\eta\,(K-1)}\qquad\text{(S.106)} \]

Its limits are telling: with \(S=1\), efficiency is capped at \(L/\eta\) no matter how many neighbors; with \(S\to\infty\), it is bounded only by \(K\) — reuse becomes arbitrarily profitable in the ideal. Tune the model and read the asymptotes directly.

But efficiency is only half the story. The paper's rendered experiments (Figures 1–2) show variance is U-shaped in neighbor count: too few neighbors undersample, too many drag in distant pixels whose paths no longer resemble yours, and quality degrades.
