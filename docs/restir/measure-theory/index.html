On-boarding introduction

If you already know ReSTIR and GRIS, you likely think in terms of samples, weights, reprojection, neighbor reuse, and MIS correction. This series asks you to shift the viewpoint one notch higher.

The central move is:

Instead of thinking of ReSTIR as “a clever way to keep one good sample,” think of it as a system for maintaining, transporting, correcting, and compressing a tiny empirical measure over a local path-space.

That reframing pays off because it makes several scattered ideas snap together:

  • Pushforward explains what reprojection and path-shifting actually do.
  • Pullback explains how the current pixel judges transported old samples.
  • Reservoirs as empirical measures explain why support, entropy, and anisotropy matter.
  • Jacobians become density-shape correction under transport.
  • Curvature becomes a practical signal for reuse instability.
  • Residual novelty explains where fresh samples should be spent.

One-paragraph thesis

Modern real-time light transport can be viewed as the maintenance of small, weighted, transported empirical measures over a screen-time bundle of path-space fibers. Reuse moves those measures across domains; current shading queries test them; Jacobians correct density under deformation; residuals reveal unexplained evidence; and curvature reveals where different reuse paths stop agreeing. In that sense, variance is no longer just noise—it is geometry, transport failure, and novelty.

Core idea: samples move forward, questions move backward.

This is the clean entry point into the transport calculus. It introduces the key equality behind reuse: evaluating a pushed-forward old measure against a current observable is equivalent to pulling the observable back onto the old domain.

Core concepts at a glance

Pushforward
Move old sample mass into the current query domain.
Pullback
Move the current test or integrand backward so old samples can be judged correctly.
Reservoir
A bounded weighted empirical measure, not just a single winner sample.
GRIS shift map
A transport map between local sample domains or fibers.
Jacobian correction
Compensate for density deformation under transport.
Residual novelty
The part of incoming evidence not explained by transported prior belief.
Curvature of reuse
The failure of different reuse paths to agree; a signal for mistrusting reuse.
Fiber / bundle view
Each pixel-time coordinate owns its own local path-space above it.
