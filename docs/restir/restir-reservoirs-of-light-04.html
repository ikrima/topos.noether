A pixel that found a good light has done work its neighbors need.
Reservoirs let it share — and that sharing is what makes ReSTIR real-time.

A single pixel, given four candidate lights per frame out of a hundred, will almost always miss the one that matters — and flicker. But its neighbor, or its own previous frame, may have already found it. Because a reservoir merges in O(1), a pixel can fold in the reservoirs around it and behind it in time, inheriting their discoveries for almost nothing. Below is a toy renderer: a floor lit by a hundred small lights, a few occluders, imaged as a grid. The left panel is what ReSTIR produces this instant; the right is the converged truth. §1 · Sharing across the image

Spatial reuse

Adjacent pixels usually look at nearby points on the same surface, lit by the same lights. So a neighbor's chosen light is very likely a good light for you too. Spatial reuse merges k randomly chosen neighbors' reservoirs into yours — each merge the O(1) operation from Part III — and repeats for n iterations, so the reach grows with every pass.

One pixel pulling k neighbors from a radius
The center pixel merges k reservoirs sampled from a small radius. Crucially, each neighbor's sample is re-weighted by this pixel's target during the merge — the generalized combine from Part III — so a sample that was good for the neighbor is judged afresh here.
Cost vs. reach

One frame costs per pixel — linear in the handful of merges. But n iterations of pulling from k neighbors reaches pixels, each carrying its own candidates: the effective pool is . Linear work, geometric reach.

§2 · Sharing across time

Temporal reuse & the clamp

The previous frame already built a reservoir for this pixel. Unless the camera or lights moved, it is still valid — so we merge it in, frame after frame, accumulating an ever-larger effective sample count. Watch the error fall as frames accrue, and how spatial reuse compounds on top of it:

Accumulated error vs. frame · no reuse vs. full spatiotemporal reuse
The displayed image is a temporal accumulation of per-frame estimates. Both converge, but full reuse starts far lower and stays there — each frame's estimate is already excellent, so the average cleans up in a fraction of the frames. The floor is the residual reuse bias and the single visibility sample.

There is a danger in this accumulation. Temporal reuse is recursive: this frame folds in last frame, which folded in the one before. Left unbounded, the history's weight count M grows without limit and the estimator stops responding to change — a moving light would smear. So ReSTIR clamps the history's M before merging — the paper uses about the current frame's count. It is a deliberate, bounded forgetting: enough memory to denoise, little enough to keep up with motion.

§3 · Why a handful becomes thousands

The combinatorial reach

§4 · Keeping reuse honest

Visibility reuse

Reuse propagates a neighbor's chosen light — but a light visible to the neighbor may be occluded for you. If we trust the inherited sample blindly, light leaks across shadow boundaries and the umbra fills with glow. The fix is cheap and exact: when a pixel shades its final sample, it re-checks visibility itself — one shadow ray — and zeroes the contribution if the light is blocked. §5 · The honest asterisk

The catch

Visibility is not the only thing reuse glosses over. When a pixel borrows a neighbor's sample, that sample was generated for the neighbor's target — a different surface point, different geometry, different shadows. The basic combine of Part III re-weights by the borrower's target, which keeps things roughly right when neighbors are similar, as on our smooth floor. But at a depth or normal discontinuity — a silhouette, a crease — the neighbors' targets diverge sharply, and the estimator becomes biased: the same coverage failure we engineered in Part II, now arising from screen-space reuse.

What Part V repairs

The reach we just built is too valuable to give up, so we do not stop reusing — we pay a small toll to make it unbiased. Each reused sample is weighted by a multiple-importance term that accounts for which targets could have produced it, exactly the partition-of-unity idea from Part I, now defending against the coverage bias of Part II. That, and the price of the extra shadow rays it costs, is the finale.

§6 · Make it yours

Exercises & the road ahead

The renderer is a real (toy) ReSTIR. Push on it.

i
Find where reuse stops helping. In the hero render, push k and n to their maxima. Does the error keep falling, or plateau? What is the floor made of — and which later widget explains it?
ii
Temporal alone is powerful. Set k = 0 and only temporal on. Watch the error fall over frames anyway. Why does a static scene converge from temporal reuse alone, and what would break if the lights moved?
iii
Count the reach. In the reach widget, read off effective candidates at n = 0, 1, 2, 3. Confirm it tracks . How many fresh rays did that actually cost?
iv
See the leak. In the visibility widget, compare the umbra readouts. The "no check" value overshoots the truth — that excess is leaked light. One shadow ray per pixel removes it.

Colophon & honesty footer

This is a faithful toy ReSTIR, run live in your browser — not a production renderer. A floor (y=0) is imaged as a 64×64 grid; each pixel resamples a light index from a fixed scene of small lights with a few box occluders. Per frame: 4 fresh candidates → resample toward p̂ = ρ·Lₕ·G → temporal merge (history M clamped to 20×) → n spatial merges of k neighbors (generalized combine, re-weighted by the pixel's own target) → shade f(y)·W with a visibility re-check. The reuse/noise widgets use a 120-light scene; the visibility widget isolates the leak with one dominant light behind a hard occluder.

Verified against ground truth (offline, averaged over seeds): normalized RMSE of a single-frame estimate — initial 1-spp 1.27 → +temporal 0.61 → +spatial k5n2 0.54 (accumulated over frames it falls below 0.1); mean effective candidates grow 4 → 64 → 466 → 2626 while bounded by the clamp; visibility reuse on the hard-shadow scene — umbra reads ≈9.1 without the re-check (leak, +45%) vs ≈6.1 with it (truth ≈6.2). What is simulated: the toy geometry and orthographic floor. What is exact: the reservoir merge, the generalized combine, and the visibility check. The residual bias of spatial reuse across differing targets is real and is the subject of Part V.
