Shifting Paths Between Worlds

How to properly transform samples between different pixels' domains — and the cost of doing so

In Essay 2, we watched the two-pixel catastrophe unfold: two pixels sharing reservoirs, confidently converging to wrong answers. The root cause was treating samples from one pixel as though they belonged to another without accounting for the geometric and probabilistic differences between their integration domains.

The fix is conceptually simple but technically subtle: when you borrow a sample from a neighbor, you must transform it into your own domain via a shift mapping, and you must account for the distortion that transformation introduces via its Jacobian determinant [3]. This is what GRIS provides — a mathematically rigorous framework for reusing correlated samples across different integration domains.

For a renderer with 25 years of experience, shift mappings will feel familiar. You've encountered them as "path reconnection" in bidirectional methods, as "half-vector copy" in gradient-domain rendering, or simply as "replay the random numbers." GRIS unifies all these tricks under one theoretical umbrella and tells you exactly when each is safe to use [3].

Different Pixels, Different Worlds

Every pixel in your image integrates a different function over a different domain. Pixel A might see a glossy metal surface reflecting a small bright light. Pixel B, one pixel away, might see a diffuse wall receiving soft indirect illumination. Their path spaces — the set of all light-carrying paths that contribute to each pixel — can be radically different.

When the two pixels hit similar geometry (high overlap), a sample from one is likely useful to the other — it hits the same surfaces, bounces in similar directions. When they hit different surfaces or see different BRDFs, a sample from one pixel may be geometrically nonsensical in the other's context.

This is the fundamental challenge: you can't just copy a path. You need to transform it. The transformation must be invertible (bijective), and you must track how it distorts probability densities. This is a shift mapping [3].

The Shift Map Zoo

The GRIS paper studies three principal strategies for transforming a "base path" \(\bar{x}\) sampled at one pixel into an "offset path" \(\bar{y}\) valid at another pixel [3]. Each makes different tradeoffs between cost, robustness, and quality.

Reconnection is the simplest: keep the path's suffix starting from the first indirect bounce vertex, and simply connect the offset pixel's primary hit to that vertex. This is cheap — you only need to store the reconnection vertex — and works beautifully for diffuse/rough surfaces where the BRDF is forgiving [3]. But on glossy surfaces, the reconnection direction may be far from the ideal reflection, producing a path with near-zero contribution.

Random Replay copies the random numbers used to construct the base path and re-traces from the offset pixel. This produces paths that are "statistically similar" — they make the same local decisions — but can diverge geometrically, especially after specular bounces. It's most robust on glossy surfaces where reconnection fails, but cheapest when paths stay short [3].

Hybrid combines both: use random replay until you reach a vertex where reconnection is safe (both vertices sufficiently rough), then reconnect. This gets the best of both worlds — handling glossy prefixes via replay and diffuse suffixes via reconnection [3]. The GRIS paper shows this hybrid achieves 24-75% lower MAPE compared to path tracing alone.

The connectability condition: The hybrid shift reconnects at vertex \(x_{i+1}\) only if vertices \(x_i\), \(x_{i+1}\), and \(y_i\) all have GGX roughness above a threshold (typically 0.2), AND the segment length \(\|x_{i+1} - y_i\| \geq d_{\max}\) to avoid singularities near geometry edges [3]. If the test fails, random replay is used and the test repeats at the next vertex.

The Jacobian Tax

Every shift mapping distorts the integration domain. When you map a small neighborhood of paths around \(\bar{x}\) through the shift \(T\), that neighborhood may expand or contract in the target domain around \(\bar{y}\). The ratio of these volumes is the Jacobian determinant \(|J_T|\), and it appears as a multiplicative correction in the resampling weight [3].

Think of it like texture mapping: a 1×1 pixel footprint in screen space maps to some area on the surface. If that surface area is large (grazing angle), you're "stretching" — each sample covers more of the domain. If it's small (head-on), you're "compressing." The Jacobian captures exactly this stretching factor.

For the reconnection shift in solid angle parameterization [3]:

$$\left|\frac{\partial \omega^y_i}{\partial \omega^x_i}\right| = \left|\frac{\cos\theta^y_2}{\cos\theta^x_2}\right| \cdot \frac{\|x_{i+1} - x_i\|^2}{\|x_{i+1} - y_i\|^2}$$

This is the ratio of cosines at the reconnection vertex times the ratio of squared distances. When the offset pixel is much farther from the reconnection vertex than the base pixel, this ratio explodes — the shift is "stretching" too much, and the resulting weight will have high variance.

For random replay, the Jacobian in primary-sample space is always 1 — because you're literally replaying the same random numbers, there's no distortion in the random number domain. But converted to solid-angle space, it becomes the ratio of sampling PDFs [3]:

$$\left|\frac{\partial \omega^y_i}{\partial \omega^x_i}\right| = \frac{p_{\omega^x_i}(x_{i+1})}{p_{\omega^y_i}(y_{i+1})}$$

This can also blow up when the offset path ends up in a direction that has very low sampling probability — for instance, when a glossy BRDF lobe is narrow and the replayed random numbers push the offset path into its tail.

When the Jacobian explodes: A Jacobian of 100× means your shifted sample is 100× less likely in the target domain than in the source domain. The resampling weight must compensate, creating a sample with enormous contribution weight — a firefly. This is why the distance condition \(\|x_{i+1} - y_i\| \geq d_{\max}\) exists: it prevents reconnections that would create singular Jacobians near geometry edges [3].

GRIS Assembly: The Full Picture

Now we can see the complete GRIS framework. Given \(M\) candidate samples, each potentially from a different domain \(\Omega_i\) with its own (possibly correlated, possibly unknown) PDF, GRIS combines them through shift mappings to produce an unbiased estimate at the target pixel [3].

The pipeline has five stages:

1. Gather candidates from various sources — neighboring pixels' reservoirs, temporal history, fresh samples from the current pixel. Each lives in its own domain \(\Omega_i\).

2. Shift each candidate into the target domain \(\Omega\) via the appropriate mapping \(T_i: \Omega_i \to \Omega\). Compute the Jacobian \(|J_{T_i}|\) for each.

3. Compute resampling weights in the target domain: \(w_i = \hat{p}(T_i(x_i)) \cdot |J_{T_i}| \cdot m_i(T_i(x_i))\), where \(m_i\) is the MIS confidence weight.

4. Select one candidate via reservoir sampling, proportional to weights.

5. Compute contribution weight \(W_Y\) that makes the final estimator unbiased.

$$W_Y = \frac{1}{\hat{p}(Y)} \cdot \frac{1}{M} \sum_{j=1}^{M} w_j \cdot \frac{1}{m_s(Y)}$$

The MIS weight \(m_i(x)\) is the key innovation of GRIS over naive ReSTIR. It must satisfy \(\sum_i m_i(x) = 1\) for all \(x\), and it encodes "how much should sample \(i\) be trusted for this location?" [3]. The paper provides three choices: constant weights (simplest, but don't converge), generalized Talbot weights (optimal but expensive), and pairwise MIS weights (nearly optimal and practical).

The convergence guarantee (Theorem 5.6 from the paper): If the MIS weights satisfy \(\sum m_i = 1\), if \(f/\hat{p}\) is bounded, if \(\text{Var}[\sum w_j] \to 0\), and if a sufficient fraction of samples are "canonical" (independent, from the target domain), then the GRIS estimator converges to the true integral. The M-cap ensures temporal samples don't crowd out canonical samples [3].

With naive reuse, certain pixels develop persistent bias — they converge to wrong values because the shift distortion and correlation aren't properly accounted for. With GRIS, all pixels converge correctly (though some converge faster than others depending on how well their neighbors' samples match their own integrand). Independent sampling converges correctly but slowly — it's the baseline against which we measure the speed improvement.

The practical lesson: GRIS with proper MIS weights and shift mappings gives you the speed of reuse with the correctness of independent sampling. ReSTIR PT achieves convergence up to 14.4× faster than path tracing with the reconnection shift [3].
