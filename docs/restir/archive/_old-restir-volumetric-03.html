Volumetric ReSTIR

How a single path sample per pixel produces near-converged volume rendering at interactive rates.

Both images use exactly one path evaluation per pixel per frame. The left image applies the samples randomly. The right image applies them intelligently—choosing, from many candidates, the single path that matters most. This is the core promise of resampled importance sampling extended to volumetric path space [1].

How does the algorithm decide which path "matters most"? How can it evaluate many candidates without paying the full cost for each? And how does it accumulate knowledge across neighboring pixels and prior frames without introducing bias?

Let's find out.

Light in a Cloud

You already know this, but let me establish our visual vocabulary. When a ray enters a participating medium, three things can happen at any point along its path: the light can be absorbed, it can be scattered in a new direction, or it can pass through unimpeded.

In the demonstration below, you can drag the ray to see how it interacts with a simple heterogeneous medium. The green segments show transmittance—how much light survives to each point. The orange dots mark potential scattering events.

Density 0.40

The volume rendering equation integrates incoming radiance along the primary ray, weighting each contribution by the transmittance to that point and the probability of a scattering event occurring there. At each scattering event, we must evaluate the illumination arriving from all directions—another integral.

For multiple scattering, each scattered ray can itself scatter again. The dimensionality explodes: a path with K scattering events lives in a 2K-dimensional space (a distance and a direction at each bounce) [1].

With Monte Carlo, we estimate this integral by sampling random paths and averaging their contributions. But with a budget of one sample per pixel per frame—which is what real-time demands—the variance is catastrophic.

This poses a challenge—how do we get the convergence of 32 or 64 samples per pixel while only evaluating one?

The answer lies in being very clever about which single path we choose to evaluate.

Choosing Wisely

You're intimately familiar with importance sampling. If we sample proportional to our integrand, variance drops to zero. The problem is that our integrand—the full volume rendering path contribution—is expensive to evaluate. That's the very thing we're trying to estimate!

What if we could sample proportional to a cheap approximation of the integrand instead? This is the insight behind Resampled Importance Sampling (RIS), introduced by Talbot in 2005 [1].

Here's the key idea, visualized below. We draw M candidates from a cheap source distribution p̂ (shown in blue). Then we evaluate an expensive target function p̂ (shown in gold) for each candidate—but only for resampling purposes, not for the final answer. We select one candidate proportional to this target weight.

Candidates M 4

Notice that as you increase M, the selected sample (the gold ring) increasingly tends toward the peak of the true integrand. With M=1, we're back to plain importance sampling. With M=32, we're getting close to sampling perfectly proportional to the integrand—even though we only evaluate the full path for the one chosen sample.

The mathematical guarantee is elegant. The RIS estimator is unbiased regardless of M [1]:

⟨L⟩RIS = F(λr) / p̂(λr) · (1/M) Σi=1M p̂(λi) / p(λi)

Here F(λr) is the full integrand evaluated only for the chosen path, p̂ is our target (the approximation we resample toward), and p is our source distribution. The sum acts as a normalizing correction.

But this formulation was designed for direct illumination on surfaces—a 2D integral over the hemisphere. Volume rendering lives in a much higher-dimensional space. Let's see how to extend it.

Paths as Samples

In the original ReSTIR for surfaces, each "sample" is a light source position. In our volumetric extension, each "sample" is an entire path through the medium—a sequence of distances and directions [1].

The demonstration below shows how a single random walk generates multiple candidate paths. Starting from the camera, we march through the volume. At each scattering vertex, we create two candidates: one that connects to a light via next event estimation (the orange path), and one that terminates in emissive media (the red path). A walk with K bounces produces up to 2K candidates.

Max bounces K 3

From M=4 random walks with K=3 bounces, we can generate up to 24 candidate paths. We evaluate our target function p̂ for each—a cheap estimate involving approximate transmittance and unshadowed radiance—and select one via weighted reservoir sampling [1].

You may have realized that this requires a target function that's cheap to evaluate for all candidates, but it only needs to approximate the true integrand. The chosen sample then gets the full, expensive, unbiased treatment.

This is the crucial insight of the paper: applying cheap, biased techniques to approximate scattering during resampling does not add bias to the final shading, because the biased estimate only influences which sample is chosen—not the value we assign to it [1].

Reservoir Sampling

Before we continue, it may be worth pausing to appreciate the streaming mechanism that makes this practical. We can't store all M candidate paths in memory—each path is a list of vertices, and we have millions of pixels.

Instead, we use weighted reservoir sampling. As candidates stream in one at a time, we maintain a single reservoir that always holds the currently-selected sample. Each new candidate either replaces the current selection (with probability proportional to its weight) or is discarded [1].

In the demo below, watch candidates arrive one by one. The purple reservoir holds the current selection. Notice how it probabilistically converges toward the highest-weighted candidate.

Speed

The beauty is that at the end of the stream, the reservoir contains a single sample drawn with probability proportional to the target weights—and it only ever stored one sample at a time. The running sum wsum is all the bookkeeping we need [2].

Spatiotemporal Reuse

Now we arrive at what makes ReSTIR genuinely transformative. Each pixel has carefully selected its best path from M candidates. But neighboring pixels have also done this work. What if we could share?

This is spatiotemporal reservoir resampling. After initial selection, each pixel combines its reservoir with those of its neighbors—both spatial (nearby pixels in the current frame) and temporal (the same pixel in the prior frame). The effective candidate count grows dramatically without proportional cost [1].

In the demonstration below, the central pixel (outlined in purple) combines its reservoir with 3 spatial neighbors (outlined in red) and 1 temporal neighbor (outlined in blue). Watch how the effective sample count M grows.

Spatial neighbors 3 Temporal Q 4

With M=4 initial candidates, 3 spatial neighbors, and a temporal limiting factor Q=4, each pixel effectively sees M × (1 + 3) × Q = 64 candidates—yet stores only a single path and its weight sum. The effective candidate count grows exponentially with temporal reuse [1].

Unfortunately, this won't really work naïvely—you can witness this in the next demonstration. If we blindly reuse a neighbor's path without correction, the result is biased. Some paths that are excellent for one pixel may be terrible for another (because the ray direction differs, the density field varies, etc.).

Multiple Importance Sampling for Reuse

Let's see what happens when we reuse without correction. In the demo below, the left column shows the target function p̂ for the central pixel, and the right shows it for a neighbor. The neighbor's selected sample (the tall bar) might have very low weight under our pixel's target function.

MIS method

Without MIS, fireflies appear wherever a neighbor contributes a sample with high weight under its own target but near-zero weight under ours. The stochastic MIS from Bitterli et al. [2020] is O(N) but introduces excess noise in volumes. The deterministic Talbot MIS has quadratic cost but is acceptably cheap with only 3 spatial neighbors [1].

Thankfully, we can solve this problem by applying the Talbot MIS weight to each reused sample. The correction factor ensures that samples are reweighted fairly according to how likely each participating reservoir was to have produced that sample [1].

Temporal Reprojection in Volumes

There is one additional clever bit that's been hiding in plain sight. For surfaces, temporal reuse is straightforward: you have motion vectors that tell you where each surface point was in the previous frame. For volumes, this is ill-defined.

The media visible through any pixel may be moving in many different directions simultaneously. A wispy curl at the near edge might drift left while the dense core behind it drifts right. There is no single "correct" motion vector [1].

The paper's solution is elegant: use the motion vector at x1, the first scattering vertex of the currently selected path. Since x1 is sampled proportional to the target PDF p(z1|x0, ω0), denser media is more likely to provide the motion vector—which makes physical sense, as it contributes more to the pixel's radiance [1].

Frame 0

Notice that without temporal reprojection (toggle it off), halos appear around moving volume boundaries—we're reusing data from an inappropriate prior-frame location. With reprojection, the noise reduction is substantial and the halos largely disappear [1].

An important caveat: this temporal reuse introduces a slight bias under camera motion or volume deformation, because the chosen temporal reservoir depends on z1 (the first scatter event). The bias increases with faster motion but is generally imperceptible, and disappears entirely a few frames after motion stops [1].

Transmittance at Three Fidelities

Transmittance evaluation dominates the cost of volumetric resampling—it must be computed between every two path vertices, potentially dozens of times per pixel during reuse. The paper uses a brilliant three-tier strategy [1]:

Fidelity tier

Tier 1: During initial candidate generation, transmittance uses a piecewise-constant approximation T* derived from the distance sampling PDF itself—essentially free.

Tier 2: During spatiotemporal reuse, transmittance is estimated by ray marching a coarser volume (Mip 1, downsampled by half) with trilinear filtering. Cheaper than full resolution, but much more accurate than T* [1].

Tier 3: For the single final shading evaluation, transmittance uses the full analytical or unbiased estimate—residual ratio tracking on the full-resolution volume.

Because Tier 1 and Tier 2 only affect which sample is chosen (importance sampling), not the value assigned to it (the estimator), their approximations and biases do not bias the final rendering [1]. This is perhaps the paper's most powerful practical insight.

The Full Pipeline

Let me now show you the complete algorithm, working together. The pipeline has four passes, each a full-screen compute dispatch [2]:

Pipeline stage Initial

Pass 1 — Initial Sampling: For each pixel, generate M=4 random walks. Each walk produces up to 2K candidate paths. Select one via RIS using target function with T* transmittance.

Pass 2 — Temporal Reuse: Reproject to the prior frame using our stochastic motion vector. Combine the current reservoir with the temporal neighbor's. Limit the temporal contribution with factor Q=4 [1].

Pass 3 — Spatial Reuse: Sample 3 neighbors within a 10-pixel radius using a low-discrepancy sequence. Combine reservoirs using Talbot MIS. Recompute target function with Tier 2 ray-marched transmittance [1].

Pass 4 — Final Shading: Evaluate the full integrand F(λr) for the single chosen path, using unbiased Tier 3 transmittance. Output the pixel color [2].

pixel color = F(λr) / p̂(λr) · wsum / M

That's it. One path evaluation per pixel. The resampling machinery ensures it's an extraordinarily well-chosen path.

What This Achieves

The results are dramatic. On an NVIDIA RTX 3090 at 1920×1080, the method renders heterogeneous volumes with complex environment lighting at interactive rates: 37ms for single scattering, 62ms for 3-bounce multiple scattering in the Bunny Cloud scene [1].

Compared to an optimized baseline (decomposition tracking + residual ratio tracking), Volumetric ReSTIR reduces MSE by 2-4× at equal time across every tested scene. The convergence advantage persists from 100ms out to 10 seconds, suggesting applicability to previsualization as well [1].

Scene

The method does inherit some limitations. It exploits spatiotemporal coherence—high-frequency variations in lighting, density, or motion limit the effectiveness of reuse. And the scalar target function means all color channels are sampled identically, leaving color noise in scenes with highly saturated lights of different hues [1].

Toward Your Own Implementation

If you're building a toy version—say, in Houdini's CVEX or a custom OptiX kernel—here's the minimal recipe:

Data per pixel: A reservoir storing one path (list of (zi, ωi) tuples), plus wsum, M, and p̂(λr) [2].

Initial sampling (Algorithm 3 from the supplemental [2]): Fire M random walks. At each vertex, evaluate the phase function, sample a light direction, estimate shadow transmittance with a coarse ray march. Update the reservoir with weight p̂/p for each candidate.

Temporal reuse (Algorithm 5 [2]): Find the prior-frame pixel using the velocity at your selected path's first vertex. Combine reservoirs using the combineReservoir() function with the resampled target PDF.

Spatial reuse (Algorithm 5 [2]): Pick N random neighbors within a radius. For each, evaluate p̂ at their selected path from your pixel's viewpoint. Apply Talbot MIS weights. Combine reservoirs.

Final shading (Algorithm 6 [2]): Evaluate the full integrand for your final path. Divide by p̂, multiply by wsum/M. That's your pixel color.

Key implementation detail: When using direction reuse (recommended), you keep the neighbor's distances zi and directions ωi, but recompute vertex positions xi = xi-1 + zi·ωi-1 relative to your own camera ray. This avoids the fireflies of vertex reuse while bounding cost [2].

A Single Path, Chosen Well

In the early days of real-time ray tracing—around 2006, when you might have first experimented with GPU path tracing—the idea of rendering complex volumetric multiple scattering at 60fps would have seemed absurd. We could barely cast primary rays in real time.

Two decades later, the insight is not brute force but cleverness: don't trace more rays—choose better rays. The marriage of reservoir sampling (a 1982 statistics algorithm), resampled importance sampling (a 2005 rendering technique), and modern spatiotemporal reuse creates something genuinely new. A single path per pixel, but one chosen with the collective wisdom of hundreds of candidates spread across space and time.

Despite the formidable mathematics—path integrals, MIS weights, conditional PDFs—the core idea is almost embarrassingly simple. Generate candidates cheaply. Score them cheaply. Pick the winner. Evaluate it carefully. Share your winners with your neighbors.

When paired with temporal denoising, this approach preserves the high-frequency volumetric details that aggressive denoising of noisy inputs would obliterate. The smoke curls. The clouds billow. Every wisp of detail survives [2].

The next time you see a shaft of light piercing through mist in a video game—rendered in real time, at full resolution, with multiple scattering—know that somewhere in the GPU, millions of reservoirs are quietly selecting from billions of candidate paths, converging on the ones that carry light to your eyes.
