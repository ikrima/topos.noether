Every frame of a modern film or game that features smoke, fire, or clouds must solve a problem that has haunted rendering engineers for decades: how do you efficiently compute the way light scatters through participating media?

How does one sample per pixel produce such clean results? The answer lies in a clever chain of ideas: resampled importance sampling, reservoir-based streaming selection, and spatiotemporal neighbor reuse. Let me take you through each link in this chain.

The Volume Rendering Integral

Before we can appreciate what ReSTIR does for volumes, we need to understand what we're trying to compute. You likely know this intimately from years of ray marching, but let me reframe it in the language that makes resampling possible.

When a ray travels through a participating medium, three things happen continuously along its path: light is absorbed, light is scattered toward the camera, and light may be emitted by the medium itself. The interplay of these phenomena gives us the volume rendering equation.

Notice that as you increase the density, the transmittance decays more steeply—less light from the background reaches the camera. Meanwhile, the scatter events multiply, each contributing a weighted fraction of the in-scattered light.

We can write this as a path integral. Let λ denote a complete path from camera through the medium to a light source. The pixel radiance is:

L(x₀, ω₀) = ∫Λ F(λ) dλ

Where F(λ) encodes the product of transmittances between vertices, phase function evaluations at each scatter, and the incident radiance at the terminal light vertex [1]. For a path with k scattering events, this expands into a product of terms at each vertex—transmittance, scattering coefficient, phase function—all the way to the light.

The challenge is clear: this integral lives in a space whose dimensionality grows with the number of scattering events. Each bounce adds two dimensions (a distance and a direction). Even for single scattering, we must integrate over the free-flight distance and the choice of light sample.

The Sampling Problem

To estimate this integral via Monte Carlo, we need to draw path samples and weight them by their probability. The closer our sampling distribution matches the actual integrand, the lower the variance.

With just one candidate (M=1), we're stuck with whatever path we drew—there's no choice involved. As you increase M, notice how the selected path gravitates toward regions of high contribution. This is the core idea of Resampled Importance Sampling (RIS).

RIS draws M candidates from a cheap source distribution, then selects one with probability proportional to a more expensive target function that better approximates the integrand. It's importance sampling of importance samples.

Resampled Importance Sampling

Let me formalize the intuition you just built. We have M candidate paths λ₁, ..., λ_M drawn from a source PDF p̂(λ). We want to select one path whose distribution approximates a target PDF p(λ) that more closely matches our integrand F(λ).

For each candidate λᵢ, we compute a weight:

w(λᵢ) = p̂(λᵢ) / p(λᵢ)

We then select one candidate with probability proportional to its weight. The unbiased estimator for the integral becomes:

⟨L⟩ = F(λᵣ) / p̂(λᵣ) · (1/M) · Σᵢ w(λᵢ)

Where λᵣ is the selected sample. The key insight from Lin, Wyman, and Yuksel is that we can choose p̂ to make expensive terms—particularly transmittance—cancel between the weight computation and the final estimator [1]. This is what makes the whole pipeline tractable.

Notice that as the target PDF more closely matches the integrand, the selected samples cluster in high-contribution regions. The variance drops dramatically—even with the same number of candidates M.

Unfortunately, this won't really work for real-time rendering with just M=4 candidates—you can witness this by setting M back to 4 in the sampling demo above. Four candidates simply can't explore the path space well enough. We need more samples, but generating them is expensive.

This poses a challenge—how do we get the benefit of hundreds or thousands of candidates without actually generating them all?

Reservoir Resampling: Borrowing From Neighbors

Thankfully, we can solve this problem by reusing samples. Every neighboring pixel has already done its own RIS pass and selected a path. If that neighbor's path is also a valid (if improbable) sample for our pixel, we can incorporate it—effectively multiplying our candidate count without extra path generation.

This is the essence of ReSTIR: chain multiple rounds of RIS together, feeding the outputs of one pixel's reservoir into another's selection process. The effective sample count M grows exponentially with each reuse step [1].

You may have realized that combining reservoirs isn't free—we can't just blindly accept a neighbor's sample. Its probability under our pixel's target function may differ wildly from its probability under the neighbor's target. We need a correction factor.

When pixel q reuses a sample from neighbor q', the correction weight is:

wq'→q = p̂q(λq') / p̂q'(λq') · wsumq'

This ratio reweights the neighbor's selected sample by how well it serves our pixel versus the neighbor's. If a neighbor's path passes through empty space from our pixel's perspective, this ratio will be near zero—correctly downweighting an irrelevant sample.

The Firefly Problem and MIS

There is one additional clever bit that's been hiding in plain sight. When we combine reservoirs from multiple neighbors, some paths may have zero probability under certain neighbors' distributions. If we ignore this, bright fireflies appear—isolated pixels of extreme energy.

Without MIS, fireflies dominate. Stochastic MIS helps but adds noise in volumes. The deterministic Talbot MIS, while more expensive, produces clean results [1].

Let's see what happens with each approach. Without any Multiple Importance Sampling, the image is riddled with bright spots. Bitterli et al.'s stochastic MIS reduces them but introduces its own noise—acceptable for surfaces, but problematic in volumes where features are inherently stochastic [1].

The deterministic Talbot MIS multiplies each sample's weight by an additional balancing term that accounts for how well all neighbors could have produced that sample. It has quadratic cost O(N²), but since we use only 3 spatial neighbors, this remains tractable [1].

Temporal Reuse and Reprojection

Spatial reuse borrows from neighbors in the same frame. Temporal reuse borrows from ourselves in prior frames. This is tremendously powerful—it means the effective sample count accumulates over time, giving exponential improvement per frame.

But there's a catch. For surfaces, temporal reprojection is straightforward: project the surface point into the previous frame using the motion vector. For volumes, this is ill-defined. The media visible in any pixel may be moving in many different directions simultaneously.

30%
Without reprojection, temporal reuse pulls from wrong locations, causing halos. The probabilistic reprojection uses the motion vector at the first scatter vertex x₁ [1].

Lin et al. approach the problem probabilistically. They use the motion vector at x₁—the first vertex on the selected path. This vertex is chosen by importance sampling, so denser media is more likely to contribute the motion vector. This is elegant: it randomizes the choice of motion, weighted by contribution [1].

You can witness in the demo that without reprojection, halos appear around moving features as we reuse from inappropriate prior-frame locations. With reprojection, these artifacts largely vanish and temporal reuse substantially reduces noise.

An important subtlety: this temporal reuse introduces a slight bias under camera motion or volume deformation, because the chosen reservoir depends on the stochastic vertex z₁. The bias increases with faster motion but is generally imperceptible, and disappears entirely once motion stops [1].

The Transmittance Hierarchy

Before we continue, it may be worth pausing to appreciate an insight that makes this entire pipeline practical. Transmittance—the exponential attenuation along a ray segment—must be evaluated between every pair of path vertices. In a naive implementation, this dominates the cost.

The key observation is that transmittance serves different roles at different stages of the pipeline, and we can use different quality levels accordingly:

Candidates
Three levels of transmittance fidelity: piecewise-constant T* for initial candidates, ray-marched T̃ for resampling, and analytical T for final shading.

During initial candidate generation, we use a piecewise-constant approximation T* from a downsampled volume (Mip 1, with BC4 compression giving 64:1 bandwidth reduction). This is cheap but coarse [1].

During spatiotemporal resampling, we upgrade to ray-marched transmittance T̃ through the Mip 1 volume with trilinear filtering. This is more expensive but only evaluated for the few samples being reused—not all M candidates.

For the final shading of the single selected path, we compute analytical transmittance T by traversing the full-resolution volume with piecewise-trilinear regular tracking. This is expensive, but it's done exactly once per pixel [1].

This is a real engineering marvel: because biased transmittance estimates only affect the importance sampling distribution (not the final throughput computation), using cheap approximations during resampling does not bias the rendered image. It only slightly reduces the quality of sample selection.

The Complete Pipeline

Let's see how all these pieces work together. The full volumetric ReSTIR pipeline has four passes:

Scrub through the four passes: (1) Generate M=4 initial candidates and select via RIS, (2) Temporal reuse with reprojection, (3) Spatial reuse from 3 neighbors with Talbot MIS, (4) Final shading of the one selected path.

Pass 1: Initial Candidates. Generate M=4 random walks per pixel. Each walk produces K scattering paths (via NEE) and K emission paths—giving 2K candidates per walk. Select one via streaming RIS using the target PDF.

Pass 2: Temporal Reuse. Reproject to find the corresponding prior-frame reservoir using the motion vector at x₁. Combine with the current frame's reservoir via RIS.

Pass 3: Spatial Reuse. Sample 3 random neighbors within a 10-pixel radius. Combine their reservoirs with ours using Talbot MIS to avoid fireflies.

Pass 4: Final Shading. Evaluate the full integrand F(λᵣ) for the one selected path, using unbiased analytical transmittance. Output the final pixel color.

Convergence and Results

Let's see what this buys us in practice. The effective sample count after spatiotemporal reuse, with Q=4 temporal limiting and 3 spatial neighbors, grows dramatically—from M=4 initial candidates to an effective count in the hundreds within a few frames.

MSE vs. render time comparison. ReSTIR consistently outperforms the baseline (decomposition + residual ratio tracking) across all time budgets [1].

The results are compelling. On an RTX 3090, single-scattering volumes render in 55ms with dramatically less noise than the baseline at equal time. Three-bounce multiple scattering runs at 142ms—still interactive—with unprecedented convergence for a real-time volumetric path tracer [1].

The method is particularly effective for emissive volumes with multiple scattering, where the baseline struggles to find paths through dense media to reach emission sources. The spatiotemporal reuse acts as a form of path guiding, steering future samples toward high-contribution regions discovered by neighbors.

Toward Implementation

If you wanted to build a toy version of this in Houdini (or any SOP/VEX environment), here's the conceptual skeleton:

First, you need a volume representation that supports multi-resolution access—VDB's built-in mip levels are ideal. Your reservoir structure per pixel stores: the selected path (as a list of (z, ω) tuples), the running weight sum wsum, and the effective sample count M.

For each pixel: generate M=4 random walks, each producing candidate paths via distance sampling and NEE. Score each candidate against the target PDF (which includes transmittance, scattering, and incident radiance terms, but with cheap transmittance). Select one via weighted reservoir sampling—a streaming algorithm that requires only O(1) memory regardless of M.

For temporal reuse: store the previous frame's reservoir buffer. Compute the motion vector at x₁ of the selected path. Look up the corresponding prior-frame pixel. Combine reservoirs via RIS with the temporal limiting factor Q=4.

For spatial reuse: pick 3 random neighbors within a 10-pixel radius (use a low-discrepancy sequence for stability). For each neighbor, compute the Talbot MIS weight and combine reservoirs.

Finally, evaluate the full unbiased throughput F(λᵣ)/p(λᵣ) for the one surviving path, using analytical transmittance for the final answer.

Closing Thoughts

In the early 2000s, real-time volume rendering meant baked lightmaps and order-independent transparency hacks. The idea of multiple-scattering path tracing in participating media running at interactive rates would have seemed fantastical.

Yet here we are—through creative application of reservoir sampling (a 1982 algorithm from survey statistics), importance resampling (formalized in 2005 for global illumination), and the spatiotemporal reuse framework of Bitterli et al. (2020), Lin, Wyman, and Yuksel have demonstrated that a single path sample per pixel can produce coherent volumetric rendering under arbitrary dynamic lighting [1].

The elegance lies in the separation of concerns: cheap approximations where they don't bias the result (importance sampling), expensive computations only where they're unavoidable (final shading), and the clever insight that your neighbors have already done work you can leverage.

Despite the mathematical machinery—path integrals, reservoir streaming, MIS weighting—the core idea is almost poetic: instead of blindly exploring the darkness, listen to what your neighbors found in the light.
