Convergence: Five Rules for Safety

The conditions that must hold for GRIS to converge — and what happens when each one breaks

We've assembled all the pieces. Essay 1 gave us RIS and reservoirs. Essay 2 showed the power — and danger — of sample reuse. Essay 3 introduced shift mappings and Jacobians to make cross-domain reuse rigorous. Now we arrive at the final question: when does the whole system actually work?

The GRIS paper identifies five conditions that must all hold simultaneously for the estimator to converge to the correct answer [3]. Violate any single one, and the system fails — sometimes subtly (slow convergence), sometimes catastrophically (convergence to wrong values).

These aren't abstract mathematical requirements. Each one corresponds to a specific engineering decision in your renderer. Understanding them means understanding why specific implementation choices exist — why M-capping matters, why MIS weights aren't optional, why your target function can't be arbitrary.

$$\text{Var}[f(Y)W_Y] \leq \text{Var}\!\left[\frac{f(X)}{p(X)}\right] + C_f^2\sqrt{\text{Var}\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^M w_i\right]}\left(\|p\| + 2\sqrt{\text{Var}\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^M w_i\right]}\right)$$

This is Theorem 1 from the paper [3] — the master variance bound. Total estimation error has two parts: the inherent mismatch between your target \(p\) and the integrand \(f\) (which no algorithm can avoid), plus a "resampling overhead" term driven by \(\text{Var}[\sum w_i]\). Make that variance vanish, and GRIS becomes as good as perfect importance sampling. Let it grow unbounded, and all bets are off.

Below, you'll find each rule with an interactive demo that lets you violate it independently. The five condition indicators at the top of each demo show you the system's health. Enter the danger zone on any one, and watch the estimator degrade in its own characteristic way.

1
Use Correct MIS Weights

The resampling MIS weights \(m_i(x)\) must form a partition of unity: \(\sum_i m_i(x) = 1\) for all \(x\) in the support of \(p\) [3]. If this condition is violated — if weights sum to more or less than 1 — the estimator develops systematic bias.

As you reduce the slider, the weights become increasingly wrong — either overcounting (sum > 1) or undercounting (sum < 1) certain regions. The constant MIS weight \(m_i = 1/M\) is the cheapest option but only converges when all samples could have come from all domains — a condition that's rarely true in practice. The pairwise MIS weights from the GRIS paper satisfy the partition-of-unity constraint while remaining computationally tractable, achieving similar per-sample error to the theoretically optimal Talbot weights but 6-7× cheaper [3].

2
Bound f/p (Reasonable Distribution)

The target distribution \(p\) must be "reasonable" for the integrand \(f\): there must exist a finite constant \(C_f\) such that \(f(x) \leq C_f \cdot p(x)\) for all \(x\) [3]. In other words, wherever the integrand \(f\) is large, the target \(p\) must also be large. If \(p\) has zeros or near-zeros where \(f\) is significant, the ratio \(f/p\) becomes unbounded — producing fireflies with potentially infinite contribution.

3
Control Var[Σw_i] → 0

This is the central convergence mechanism of GRIS: the variance of the sum of resampling weights must decrease toward zero as the sample count grows [3]. This term \(\text{Var}[\sum w_i]\) is the "resampling overhead" — the price you pay for not being able to sample from \(p\) directly.

When samples are independent and properly weighted, this variance naturally decreases as \(O(M/|R|^2)\) where \(|R|\) is the number of canonical samples [3]. But when correlation creeps in — from reuse, from duplicate samples — this variance can stall or even grow.

The demo shows how sample correlation directly prevents \(\text{Var}[\sum w_i]\) from vanishing. At zero correlation, the sum of weights converges to a deterministic value. As correlation increases, the sum fluctuates wildly, preventing the estimator from stabilizing.

4
Sufficient Canonical Samples

Among the \(M\) input samples to GRIS, at least some must be "canonical" — independently drawn from a distribution that covers the entire support of \(p\) [3]. These canonical samples ensure that every region of the integration domain eventually gets explored, even if reused samples from neighbors happen to miss it.

The ratio \(|R|/M\) (canonical samples to total samples) must not vanish as \(M\) grows. Specifically, the paper requires \(|R| > O(\sqrt{M})\) for convergence [3]. In practice, keeping at least one canonical sample per pixel per frame (the "fresh" sample from your path tracer) satisfies this.

More reused neighbors means more candidates, but each additional reused sample dilutes the canonical fraction. Eventually, the canonical sample is almost never selected, and uncovered regions develop permanent bias.

5
M-cap for Temporal Reuse

When reservoirs carry information across frames, the "confidence weight" \(M_r\) grows without bound — the reservoir claims to represent thousands of frames of accumulated samples. The M-cap limits this: it clamps \(M_r \leq M_c\), forcing the reservoir to "forget" old information exponentially [3].

Without M-cap (\(M_c = \infty\)), the temporal sample's relative weight \(M_r/(M_r+1)\) approaches 1.0, meaning the fresh canonical sample from the current frame is almost never selected. This violates Rule 4 (insufficient canonical samples) and causes the two-pixel catastrophe from Essay 2 — convergence to a wrong, static result [3].

Below, observe the tradeoff: low M-cap gives fast adaptation but noisy estimates; high M-cap gives smooth convergence in static scenes but fails to track changes. Infinite M-cap (the rightmost position) causes the catastrophe.

The Complete Picture

Over these four essays, we've built a complete understanding of GRIS — from the single-sample foundation of RIS, through the power and peril of spatiotemporal reuse, to the shift mappings that make cross-domain reuse rigorous, and finally to the five conditions that guarantee convergence.

The beauty of the GRIS framework is that it unifies previously ad-hoc engineering decisions (M-capping, neighbor randomization, MIS weights) into a coherent theory with provable guarantees [3]. Every time you set an M-cap value, you're controlling the canonical fraction (Rule 4). Every time you choose a roughness threshold for reconnection, you're controlling the Jacobian magnitude (which feeds into Rule 2). Every time you randomize your spatial neighbor selection, you're decorrelating samples (Rule 3).

The theory doesn't just explain what to do — it explains why, and tells you exactly what you're trading off when you deviate from the ideal. That understanding is what separates a renderer that works from a renderer that works and you know why.

The five rules, condensed for implementation:
① Use pairwise MIS weights (Equation 38 of the paper) — cheap and correct [3].
② Set target \(p \propto f\) or at least ensure \(p > 0\) wherever \(f > 0\) — avoid fireflies [3].
③ Keep samples sufficiently decorrelated — randomize neighbor selection, limit reuse radius [3].
④ Always include at least one fresh canonical sample per pixel per frame — maintain domain coverage [3].
⑤ Use M-cap ≈ 20 for real-time, adjust based on scene dynamics — balance convergence and adaptation [3].
