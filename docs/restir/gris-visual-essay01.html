The Weight of a Sample

How resampled importance sampling builds near-perfect distributions from imperfect ones

Every image you've ever rendered is a lie — a plausible statistical estimate assembled from random samples. The quality of that lie depends entirely on where those samples land. Place them wisely, and a handful of rays can capture a complex scene. Place them poorly, and millions of rays still produce noise.

Importance sampling addresses this by drawing samples from a distribution that resembles the function being integrated. But what if you can't sample from your ideal distribution directly? What if the best distribution you can describe is one you can only evaluate, not sample from?

This is precisely the problem that Resampled Importance Sampling (RIS) solves — and it's the foundation upon which the entire GRIS framework is built [3].

With uniform sampling, every region of the domain gets equal attention — wasteful when the function is concentrated. Importance sampling with a matched PDF \(p(x) \propto f(x)\) reduces variance dramatically, but requires that we can actually sample from that PDF.

In rendering, the ideal sampling distribution is the full integrand — the product of BRDF, cosine term, and incoming radiance. We can evaluate this product at any point, but sampling from it directly is intractable. We're stuck with simpler distributions: BRDF sampling, light sampling, or uniform hemisphere sampling.

What if there were a way to start with easy samples and then resample them to approximate any target distribution we want?

The Two-Stage Magic

Resampled Importance Sampling works in two stages. First, draw \(M\) candidate samples \(X_1, \ldots, X_M\) from an easy source distribution \(p(x)\). Then, select one of these candidates with probability proportional to a resampling weight:

$$w_i = \frac{\hat{p}(X_i)}{p(X_i)}$$

Here \(\hat{p}\) is our target function — the distribution we wish we could sample from. It doesn't need to be normalized. It just needs to be evaluable. The selected sample \(Y\) ends up distributed approximately as \(\hat{p}\), with the approximation improving as \(M\) grows.

Notice something remarkable: we never needed to know the normalization constant of \(\hat{p}\). The selection probability for candidate \(i\) is \(w_i / \sum_j w_j\) — the normalizations cancel. This is why RIS is so powerful in rendering: your target can be the full unshadowed path contribution, an expensive-to-normalize quantity, and RIS handles it gracefully.

Key insight for the CG practitioner: Think of the source \(p(x)\) as your cheap sampling strategy (e.g., cosine-weighted hemisphere). Think of the target \(\hat{p}(x)\) as what you actually want to importance-sample (e.g., BRDF × incoming radiance). RIS bridges the gap between what's easy to sample and what's ideal to sample.

The Contribution Weight

After selecting \(Y\), we can't simply use \(f(Y)\) as our estimate — the selection process has biased our sample. To remain unbiased, we must assign a contribution weight \(W_Y\) such that:

$$\mathbb{E}[f(Y) \cdot W_Y] = \int f(x)\, dx$$

The correct weight turns out to be [3]:

$$W_Y = \frac{1}{\hat{p}(Y)} \cdot \frac{1}{M} \sum_{j=1}^{M} w_j$$

This is Theorem A.1 of the GRIS paper: \(W_Y\) is an unbiased estimator for the reciprocal of \(Y\)'s marginal density [3]. Let's build intuition for why this works.

The contribution weight \(W_Y\) compensates for the imperfect match between the actual marginal distribution of \(Y\) and our target \(\hat{p}\). When \(M=1\), no resampling occurs — we just get a standard importance sampling weight. As \(M \to \infty\), the marginal of \(Y\) approaches \(\hat{p}/\|\hat{p}\|\) perfectly, and \(W_Y \to \|\hat{p}\| / \hat{p}(Y) \cdot \hat{p}(Y)/\|\hat{p}\| = 1/p_Y(Y)\) — perfect importance sampling with zero variance in the weight itself.

The Reservoir: Streaming RIS

In practice, we don't want to store all \(M\) candidates — especially on a GPU where memory is precious. The reservoir algorithm processes candidates one at a time, maintaining a running winner. Each new arrival challenges the current champion with probability proportional to its weight. After processing all \(M\) candidates, the reservoir holds a sample with the exact same distribution as batch RIS [3].

The reservoir algorithm is beautifully simple:

Reservoir update rule: When candidate \(X_i\) arrives with weight \(w_i\):
1. Add to running sum: \(\text{wsum} \mathrel{+}= w_i\)
2. Replace current winner with probability \(w_i / \text{wsum}\)
3. After all \(M\) candidates: \(W_Y = \text{wsum} / (M \cdot \hat{p}(Y))\)

This streaming formulation is what makes ReSTIR practical for real-time rendering: a reservoir at each pixel can be updated incrementally as new candidates arrive — from light sampling, from neighboring pixels, from previous frames. Each source of candidates simply "pours" into the reservoir, and the correct sample emerges [3].

What Comes Next

We've established the single-pixel, independent-samples story. RIS takes candidates from easy distributions and reshapes them toward any evaluable target — no normalization required. The reservoir makes this streamable and memory-efficient.

But the real power of ReSTIR comes from reusing samples across pixels and frames. In Essay 2, we'll see how spatial and temporal reuse gives exponential gains in effective sample count — and we'll witness the catastrophic failure that occurs when correlated samples break the independence assumption that makes everything above work.

That failure is what motivates the entire GRIS theory [3].
