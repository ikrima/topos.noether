Moving Measures Between Pixels

Reuse is a lens. Sometimes it's the wrong one.

The weight is a lens

When you reuse a neighbour's sample, you write a weight that looks like this — and every term in it is one face of a single object.

You read this left to right without thinking: a resampling MIS weight m, the target function p̂dst evaluated at the shifted sample, the source's unbiased contribution weight W, and the shift Jacobian |T′|. Four moving parts. But to compute m with a balance heuristic you also need the inverse shift and the source-domain density of the target sample — two more parts you wrote elsewhere and stopped noticing. And the whole thing silently assumes the shift was valid: that the source could have produced this sample at all.

Bundle those together and you have what we'll call a measure lens — the apparatus that carries a measure from one pixel's domain into another's:

Forward shift, inverse shift, Jacobian, target pullback, source-density query, a support test, and a tag saying which measure classes this lens is even allowed to serve. You already build six of these seven; this part is about seeing them as one thing — and about the seventh, the part that decides when the lens is lying.

The shift, watched

A reconnection shift keeps the far vertex — here the point on the light — and re-aims the connection from the new shading point. The Jacobian is the price of that re-aiming. The question that decides everything downstream: after the carry, does the source's idea of where the light matters still match the target's?

The plot under the scene shows two contribution densities over the light: what the source pixel finds bright, and what the target pixel finds bright. Drag the target away from the source, then pull roughness down.

One lens isn't enough

Reconnection keeps the world-space vertex. For a broad lobe that's exactly right. For a sharp one it's a disaster, because the thing worth preserving was never the vertex — it was the shape of the bounce: the half-angle that put the sample on the peak in the first place. Flip the lens above to replay. Now the shift preserves that half-angle and re-derives a new light point, dragging the reused sample back onto the target's spike. The curves realign.

But replay has a cost the reconnection lens never pays. Preserve the bounce shape and the re-derived direction may simply miss the light entirely — point at empty space beyond its edge. When that happens the lens has no valid sample to offer at all. That is the support test failing: not a bad value, but no value, because the shift walked off the edge of where the source could have gone. Drag the target far enough in replay mode and watch the fit indicator turn red for exactly this reason.

A diffuse sample wants its vertex kept.
A specular sample wants its bounce kept.
No single lens keeps both.

This is why the classifier of Part 2 ended each verdict with a shift mode. Diffuse → reconnection. Glossy → a hybrid that blends the two. Near-singular → a conservative hybrid that mostly declines. Delta → pure replay, or nothing. Boundary → don't reuse at all; trust the current frame. The lens is chosen by the kind of measure it has to carry.

What's coming

We've watched the carried density line up with the target's, and watched it fail — the peaks drifting apart, the support falling away. So far that judgement has been your eye on two curves. Part 4 replaces your eye with a single scalar: the lens residual, the log-gap between what the source proposed and what the target actually wants. It is near zero when the curves agree and it blows up the instant they don't — and we will put it back on the rotating-camera scene and watch it fire, pixel for pixel, exactly where the white sparks are.

Try it yourself

  1. In reconnection mode, fix roughness at 0.5 and drag the target across its whole range. The curves stay broadly aligned. Now set roughness to 0.1 and repeat — find the smallest target offset at which the reused sample falls completely off the target's spike.
  2. Switch to replay and reproduce the support failure: drag the target until the fit turns red. Read the scene — where did the re-derived connection point, and why is "no sample" the honest answer rather than a dim one?
  3. In the anatomy stepper, identify which single operation the balance-heuristic MIS weight depends on that the final contribution weight does not. Why does only the MIS weight need it?

What is exact, and what is illustrative

The workbench runs the verified engine's geometry on a wide, low area light chosen so both pixels' highlights fall on it. Both contribution densities are the true integrand over the light in arclength measure, p̂(P,y)·(cosθ_y/d). The reconnection shift keeps the light vertex and its Jacobian is the exact planar change-of-variables (cosθ_y/d)_dst ÷ (cosθ_y/d)_src. The replay shift preserves the world-space half-vector — correct for a flat surface of constant normal — and re-derives the connection by reflecting the target's view direction about it; its support test is the honest question of whether that re-derived ray meets the light. We display replay's landing point and validity but not a replay Jacobian, which for a true manifold shift is more involved than this 2D cross-section warrants.

The anatomy stepper is a faithful labelling of the prompt's lens interface; the operations are real, the on-canvas motions are schematic. As throughout, the six-class decomposition and its per-class shift assignments are a computational design, not a theorem; whether the framing is novel is a question for a literature search.
