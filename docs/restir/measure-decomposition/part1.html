The Measure of Light · Part One

The Firefly at the Boundary

Why the brightest mistakes in path reuse cluster exactly where they do.

The symptom

You already know this picture. A static scene, a slowly turning camera, ReSTIR PT humming along — and a scatter of fireflies that bloom, persist for a few frames, and die, always in the same neighbourhoods: the edge of a shadow, the lit rim of a glossy surface.

Here is that picture, running for real. A flatland cross-section: a floor lit by an area light, a thin occluder casting a soft shadow, a camera rotating about a fixed point. The bottom strip is the render — luminance along the floor, accumulated frame to frame the way a real pipeline would. Everything below is a genuine resampling loop: reconnection shifts, change-of-variables Jacobians, a confidence-weighted balance heuristic. Nothing here is scripted.

The usual explanations are all true and all incomplete. Stale history. Disocclusion. Specular under-sampling. A shift map that should have been rejected. Each names a circumstance, not a cause. What they share — what makes the shadow edge and the specular rim the same kind of place — is the thing worth seeing. And to see it, we need one idea you almost certainly already hold, under a different name.

In the language of the textbooks, that is a Radon–Nikodym derivative:

You have never once called it that. You called it the target function, or the integrand, or p_hat. And when you reuse a sample from a neighbour — carry it through a shift map T and multiply by the Jacobian |T′| — you are computing the pushforward of one measure onto another domain. The Jacobian is the change-of-variables term. That is the entire content of the word "pushforward."

So the vocabulary isn't new. What's new is the claim that these measures have types — and that mixing the types is the bug.

A hill, and then a spike

There is exactly one idea in this series that you may not already hold, and it lives in a slider you drag every day. A measure can be spread out or concentrated, and the difference is not cosmetic — it decides whether the measure survives being moved around.

Watch a BRDF lobe as roughness falls. The mass under the curve — the total energy — is conserved. What changes is where that mass lives.

That progression has names, and they are the names this whole series turns on. The broad hill is an absolutely continuous measure: it has a sensible finite density everywhere, and a finite sample budget describes it well. As roughness falls it becomes near-singular — still technically a density, but so concentrated that any real sampler mostly misses it, and the rare hit carries a colossal weight. At the limit of a perfect mirror it is singular: the mass piles onto a set of zero width — a single direction — where no finite density can describe it at all.

A firefly is what a colossal weight looks like
after it survives the journey to your pixel.

Now hold both pictures at once. The spike is the cause — a measure too concentrated for the budget. The firefly in the first figure is the effect — that concentrated weight, carried intact through a reuse step into a pixel where it doesn't belong. The shadow edge and the specular rim are the two places where the path contribution stops being a friendly hill: at the specular rim because the lobe itself is a spike, and at the shadow edge because visibility cuts the measure's support clean in two. The same dial that turned the hill into a spike, back in figure one, is the roughness slider — and you watched the fireflies answer it.

One reservoir, six kinds of light

A real pixel's contribution is never one clean measure. It is a mixture: a broad diffuse base, a glossy bump, the near-singular glint of a tight highlight, the hard delta of a mirror, a region of the domain switched off entirely by an occluder, perhaps a haze of volume scattering. Drawn as a silhouette of where the mass lives, it looks like this — and a single reservoir, transported by a single shift map, has to carry all of it at once.

That mismatch — a sample of one kind forced through transport designed for another — is what this series will make precise, and eventually measurable. The textbook even has a name for splitting a measure into its well-behaved and its concentrated parts; it is called a Lebesgue decomposition, and in Part 2 we will borrow it, unapologetically, as an engineering move rather than a theorem.

Try it yourself

  1. In figure one, find the roughness at which the specular firefly band first appears as a distinct feature, separate from the shadow-edge fireflies. Which shows up first as you lower roughness?
  2. Hold roughness fixed and toggle reuse on and off. The grain pattern changes character completely — describe how the location of the worst errors differs between the two modes.
  3. In the spike figure, set the budget to 8 and lower roughness slowly. Estimate the roughness at which a sampler this small effectively stops finding the peak at all — and connect that to where reuse becomes a liability rather than a help.

What is exact, and what is illustrative

The path-reuse loop in figure one is a real generalized resampling estimator in a 2D cross-section: light sampling on an area light, a reconnection shift that holds the light vertex and recomputes the connection, the exact planar change-of-variables Jacobian, and a confidence-weighted generalized balance heuristic for the MIS weights. It was verified unbiased against a brute-force quadrature reference to within 0.08% maximum relative error before being used here. The fireflies are emergent, not authored.

The stylizations, stated plainly: luminance is Reinhard-tonemapped and exponentially accumulated for display, exactly as a denoiser inlet would be; the occluder is a floating shadow caster that blocks light-path visibility but not the primary camera rays, to keep the cross-section legible; the "measure class" silhouette in the mixture figure is a hand-composed illustration of the shape of a mixed contribution, not a measurement. The decomposition this series proposes is a computational one — a useful sorting of paths by transport behaviour — not a claim to a theorem-exact Lebesgue split of the rendering equation. Where this synthesis is novel rather than a repackaging of known GRIS practice is a question for a literature search, not an assertion to make here.
