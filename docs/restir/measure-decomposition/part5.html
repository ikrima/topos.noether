The Measure of Light · Part Five

A Decomposed Reservoir

Reuse boldly where it's safe. Refuse where it isn't. Change nothing that already works.

From measuring to acting

Four parts of measurement. We named the measure classes, built the lens that carries a sample between pixels, and forged the residual that fires when the lens lies. Not once did we change a pixel's value. Now we do — and the whole difficulty is doing it without breaking the estimator.

There is exactly one rule, and everything else follows from it:

The unbiased estimate is a sample times its weight, full stop. You may never reach in and scale the radiance by a compatibility score, a residual, a "this looks risky" factor — the instant you do, the estimator is biased and the image is wrong in a way no denoiser can undo. Everything the decomposition learned is allowed to influence only the things that don't touch radiance: which class a sample is, which shift carries it, how much we trust history, and whether we reuse at all. Those levers depend on geometry, not on the sampled value — so they steer the estimator without biasing it.

Three buckets, one rule

The reservoir splits in three, by transport risk. Regular holds the well-behaved mass — diffuse and rough glossy, where reconnection just works. Specular-risk holds the near-singular and delta samples, reused conservatively if at all. Boundary-risk holds samples sitting on a visibility discontinuity, which are not reused across the edge at all. The classifier from Part 2 decides the bucket; the bucket decides the shift, the confidence cap, and the reuse policy. Set a candidate's properties and watch it routed.

The intervention

Here is the test that matters. Both strips below render the same moving scene from the same samples. The top runs naive reuse — the thing aggressive real-time systems actually do: cache a sample's shadowing and reuse it without rechecking, let confidence pile up frame after frame. The bottom runs the decomposed reservoir. Drag the occluder through the light and watch the difference.

Measured over the run: the decomposed reservoir roughly halves the transient flares, cuts the peak spike about threefold, and collapses the shadow-edge bias from tens of percent to a couple — while the diffuse error stays pinned at zero. The flares it removes are the boundary-transport ones; the spikes it can't fully remove are the near-singular sampling failures from Part 4, which is why the peak drops but does not vanish.

Why it stays honest

The reason the diffuse image is untouched and the shadow edge converges to the truth is that every lever the decomposition pulls is sample-independent. Which bucket a sample lands in, which shift carries it, how high its confidence may climb, whether a boundary sample is reused — all are decided from geometry the renderer already has, before the sampled radiance is ever looked at. Plug them into the confidence-weighted balance heuristic and the estimator remains a textbook unbiased combination of proposals. Run it longer and the shadow-edge error keeps shrinking toward zero — the mark of variance, not bias.

Contrast the forbidden move. Suppose, seeing a high residual, you scaled that sample's contribution down by e^(−λ|r|) to "calm the firefly." It would calm it — and quietly darken every region where reuse is imperfect, which is everywhere interesting. The residual is a magnificent diagnostic and a legitimate input to confidence; the moment it multiplies radiance it becomes a lie with a physical-looking units label.

Steer with geometry, not with the sample.
That single discipline is the whole of unbiased reuse.

What this is, and isn't

Be clear-eyed about the win. The dramatic numbers above are measured against naive reuse, because that is what the decomposition is for: it lets a system reuse cheaply and aggressively where the measure says it's safe, and refuse where it isn't — provably without bias. Against an already-correct reuse scheme with exact shifts and proper weights, this 2D direct-lighting world has little left to fix; the transport is already handled. The decomposition's real territory is everything this flat slice only gestures at — glossy interreflection, specular chains, participating media — where shifts are necessarily approximate and knowing which approximation is valid where is the difference between a clean frame and a field of fireflies.

So treat this series as the floor plan, not the building. We made the measure-theoretic structure of path reuse visible, gave every part of it a name and an instrument, and showed that acting on it can be both effective and honest. The decomposition is a control surface and a diagnostic; turning it into consistently lower error across a production renderer is the work that begins where this leaves off.

Try it yourself

  1. In the intervention, hold the occluder still at broad roughness. The flare gap between naive and decomposed nearly closes — why does the decomposition's advantage depend on motion at the boundary?
  2. Drop roughness to 0.08 and watch the peak luminance on both strips. The decomposed peak is lower but still large. Which Part-4 failure is responsible, and which lever — if any — in this reservoir addresses it?
  3. In the router, turn on "visibility boundary" and sweep roughness across its whole range. Confirm the bucket, shift, and cap never change. Argue why that invariance is exactly what keeps the shadow edge unbiased.

What is exact, and what is illustrative

The intervention is real and measured, not staged. Both pipelines run the verified engine on the canonical scene; they differ only in policy. The naive baseline reuses each sample's cached visibility without rechecking it after the camera moves, and lets confidence accumulate to a high cap — a faithful stand-in for aggressive real-time reuse. The decomposed reservoir rechecks visibility through the shift, holds boundary-classified pixels to the current frame, and caps confidence per measure class. Over a fixed run the decomposition roughly halves transient flares, lowers the peak spike about threefold, and reduces shadow-edge bias from the tens of percent to low single digits; extending the run drives the decomposed error toward zero (confirming it is variance, not bias) while the naive shadow-edge bias persists. The diffuse band is unbiased in both to within Monte-Carlo noise.

The honest scope is stated in the coda: these gains are over a naive baseline, since the exact-shift direct-lighting engine is already unbiased and well-behaved on its own. The decomposition's value in this series is as a control surface and diagnostic; the production-scale payoff lives in approximate-shift, multi-bounce, and specular-transport regimes beyond this 2D model. The six-class decomposition, the lens residual, and the per-class policies are a computational design; whether the synthesis is novel is a question for a literature search, not a claim made here.
