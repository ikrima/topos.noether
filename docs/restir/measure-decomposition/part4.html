The Measure of Light · Part Four

The Lens Residual

One number that knows when the lens is lying.

The number

In Part 3 you judged a reuse by eye: did the carried density land where the target wanted it? That judgement collapses to a single scalar — the gap, in logs, between what the source proposed and what the target actually finds:

Read it as a sentence. log p̂src is how bright the source thought this sample was. Transport it — apply the shift, correct by the Jacobian — and log p̂dst(Tx) + log|T′| is how bright the target finds the same sample after the carry. When the lens is honest those two agree and r ≈ 0. When it isn't, the sign tells you how it failed:

r ≫ 0 — the target finds far more here than the source predicted: a bright sample arriving under-weighted, the classic firefly setup.   r ≪ 0 — the source over-promised; the carry lands somewhere the target finds dim: wasted reuse, stale history, a slow darkening.   |r| ≫ 0 either way — the lens crossed a wall it had no business crossing: a specular mismatch, a visibility discontinuity, a support failure.

The residual doesn't measure error. It measures disagreement — between two pixels about what a single sample is worth.

The residual fires

Now put it back on the moving scene. Three strips over one floor: the render with its fireflies, and beneath it the residual field — the magnitude of the lens residual for each pixel's temporal carry, frame by frame, as the camera pans. This is the figure the whole series was built to show. Drag the occluder through the light and watch the two strips light up together.

At a broad roughness the only fireflies are these boundary ones, and the residual catches essentially all of them — its |r| at firefly pixels runs several times its value on calm pixels. The disagreement between pixels about a carried sample is, measurably, where the image breaks.

What it can't see

Honesty demands the other half. Pull roughness down toward a needle and a second kind of spark appears — in the bright specular band, not on a red residual column. These are not transport failures at all. They are the near-singular spikes from Part 1: a lobe so tight the canonical sampler simply misses it, and on the rare frame it hits, the weight is enormous. Nothing was carried wrong; the sample was found wrong.

The lens residual is blind to this by construction — there's no source-to-target disagreement to measure when the problem is a sampler that can't see its own target. That isn't a flaw in the residual; it's the residual telling the truth about its own scope. Two different failures:

Transport failure — a sample carried across a wall. Diagnosed by the residual. Fixed by choosing a better lens, or refusing the carry.   Sampling failure — a spike no budget can find. Invisible to the residual. Fixed by capping how much we trust a near-singular reservoir in the first place.

The residual finds the carries that lie.
It cannot find the spikes nobody sampled.

So the residual is necessary, not sufficient — the first of two instruments. It tells us which reuses to distrust. Part 5 builds the second instrument, the class-aware confidence policy, and shows how to act on both without ever multiplying radiance by a fudge factor — the one rule that keeps the estimator honest.

What's coming

We have the diagnostic. We know precisely which samples it catches and which it can't. What we have not done is change a single pixel's value — every figure so far has been measurement, not intervention. Part 5 finally acts: separate reservoirs by class, dispatch the lens the classifier chose, let the residual and the support risk modulate confidence and rejection — and watch the fireflies go quiet, with the diffuse image untouched and the estimator still unbiased.

Try it yourself

  1. In the per-sample workbench, find a target offset and roughness that make r strongly negative. Which physical situation is that — and why is it a darkening risk rather than a firefly risk?
  2. In the field, set roughness to 0.6 (broad) and drag the occluder slowly. Confirm that every white firefly has a red residual column under it. Then drop roughness to 0.08 and find the sparks that don't — those are the sampling failures Part 5 must handle differently.
  3. Hold the occluder perfectly still for several seconds at broad roughness. What happens to the residual field, and why does motion — not the occluder itself — drive the boundary residual?

What is exact, and what is illustrative

The residual is computed exactly as defined: for each pixel's temporal reuse candidate, the previous frame's reservoir sample is transported to the current shading point and r = log p̂_dst(Tx) + log|T′| − log p̂_src(x) is evaluated with the verified engine. The field strip shows |r|; fireflies are detected as pixels exceeding several times their own temporal median. Under this canonical scene — constant-speed camera pan, an area light partially blocked by a finite occluder — the mean |r| at firefly pixels measures roughly three to five times its value at calm pixels at broad-to-moderate roughness, where the firefly population is dominated by boundary transport. As roughness sharpens, an increasing share of fireflies come from canonical under-sampling of the specular spike, which the residual does not predict; this is shown and discussed rather than hidden, and it is the reason Part 5 needs a second, sampling-side instrument.

The per-sample workbench reuses Part 3's local illustrative geometry. As throughout, the six-class decomposition and the residual diagnostic are a computational design; whether the framing is novel is a question for a literature search, not a claim made here.
