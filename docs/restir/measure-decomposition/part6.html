Reading the Instruments

How to use what we built — and an honest account of where it breaks.

Reading the instruments

The five parts built a small set of instruments: a classifier that names a sample's measure class, a lens that carries it, a residual that fires when the carry is wrong, and a reservoir that acts on all three. This part adds no new machinery. It is about judgement — how a practitioner would read these dials in a real renderer, how to set them, and the places where, used carelessly, they quietly lie.

Everything here descends from the one rule of Part 5: compatibility may steer the estimator, but it may never multiply radiance. The interesting questions are all about how to steer — how hard, with which signal, calibrated to what — and they have honest answers and honest limits.

The residual, as a confidence

The residual's first legitimate job is to shape confidence — how much a reused sample is trusted relative to the fresh canonical one, inside the balance heuristic. A workable mapping turns the instruments into a single multiplier on a candidate's confidence weight:

Crucially this scales c, the confidence in the MIS weight — never f(Y). The dial below plots it. On the canonical scene, a calm carry measures a residual of about |r| ≈ 0.25; the carries that become fireflies average near |r| ≈ 1 with a tail running past 70. A good λ keeps confidence near 1 at the calm value and crushes it across the firefly tail — without starving the merely-imperfect reuse that is, in practice, almost everywhere.

Confidence may listen to the sample. Radiance may not. The same number, on the wrong side of that line, turns a diagnosis into a distortion.

Choosing the thresholds

Every lever in the reservoir has a number behind it, and the temptation is to guess. The instruments let you calibrate instead. The near-singular roughness cut, the class confidence caps, the support-risk gate — each can be set from the residual statistics of a representative frame: pick the threshold that separates the calm population of carries from the firefly tail, the same separation the residual was shown to produce. Below is the discipline that keeps the calibration honest — which levers may read the sample, and which may not.

May steer freely — sample-independent

  • Measure-class assignment, from roughness, lobe flags, and G-buffer gradients.
  • Shift selection — reconnection, hybrid, replay, or canonical-only — by class.
  • Confidence caps per class; canonical reinjection every frame.
  • Refusing reuse across a visibility boundary detected from geometry.
  • Candidate count: spend more canonical samples where the class is risky.

Must never touch radiance

  • Multiplying f(Y) by a residual, compatibility, or "risk" factor.
  • Using the residual as a weight on the contribution itself.
  • Any clamp on the estimate dressed up as physical — it darkens, invisibly.

Grey zone — model it or pay for it

  • Hard-rejecting a candidate by its own residual: sample-dependent, so it biases unless the decision is folded into the proposal and MIS.
  • Residual-driven confidence is the safe version of the same instinct — prefer it.
  • Aggressive temporal confidence caps interact with the denoiser; tune them together.

Set from data, not taste

  • kNearSingularRoughness: where the lobe's peak first out-runs the sampler's budget.
  • Residual gate: the knee between the calm carries and the firefly tail.
  • Caps: how many stale frames a class can afford before re-earning trust.

Where v0 breaks

The honest part. The design in this series is a v0, and it fails in named, predictable ways.

The sampling-failure spikes. Part 4 was explicit: the residual sees transport failure, not the near-singular spike a sampler simply never finds. No confidence policy in this reservoir removes those — capping a near-singular reservoir's trust even slightly raises the worst spike, because it removes the temporal averaging that was smoothing it. Those spikes are a sampling problem and want a sampling answer: more candidates on near-singular pixels, guided or learned sampling of the lobe, or a deliberate, declared clamp owned by the denoiser — not by the estimator.

The flat world hides the main event. A 2D direct-lighting scene has a single exact shift. The decomposition's reason to exist — choosing which approximate shift is valid where — only appears with glossy interreflection, specular chains, and media, where shifts must approximate and the wrong one fails silently. Everything here about multi-shift dispatch is therefore argued, not yet demonstrated at the scale that matters.

Calibration drifts. Thresholds tuned on one scene mis-fire on another: a brighter light, a glossier floor, faster motion all move the calm-versus-firefly separation. A shipped system needs the residual statistics computed live and the gates adapted per frame, not baked once.

It is not free. A per-candidate residual is a few transcendental evaluations and an extra shift; the classifier touches the G-buffer. On a path budget that already counts nanoseconds, the instruments earn their place only where the fireflies they catch cost more than the cycles they spend — which is an empirical question this series does not answer.

Open problems

  1. The bias–variance frontier of residual-gated reuse. The unbiased confidence-only policy and the slightly-biased hard-rejection heuristic are two points on a curve. Where on it does perceptual quality, after denoising, actually peak? Nobody here has drawn that curve.
  2. Is six the right cut? The classes are a useful engineering partition, not a theorem. A coarser cut may suffice; a finer one (subsurface, anisotropic, dispersion) may be forced by richer materials. The basis is provisional.
  3. Hand-tuned versus learned policies. Every threshold here is a human guess calibrated to a statistic. A small network reading the same G-buffer features could route and gate better — at the cost of the legibility this whole series was built to preserve.
  4. Validation at production scale. The claims were checked against quadrature in a flatland. A real test is a full path tracer with manifold shifts, measured in converged-reference MSE and in temporal stability under a production denoiser — the work that begins where this ends.
  5. Novelty. Whether this synthesis — measure-class decomposition, the lens framing, the transport residual as a diagnostic — is new, or a renaming of things the field already does under other names, is a question for a literature search, not for this author to assert.

Coda

The series set out to make one claim tangible: that the firefly is not a glitch but a reading — the visible sign of a measure carried through a lens that did not fit it. If that reframing makes the next firefly you see legible — if you find yourself asking not "how bright" but "what kind, and was the carry honest" — then the instruments have done their work, whatever becomes of the particular policies.

What is exact, and what is illustrative

This part introduces no new simulation. The calm and firefly residual magnitudes anchoring the confidence dial (|r| ≈ 0.25 calm, ≈ 1 at fireflies with a tail past 70) are measured from the canonical scene's verified engine at moderate roughness; the dial itself plots the confidence mapping as a function, an exact rendering of the formula, not a simulation. Everything in "where v0 breaks" and "open problems" is argument and acknowledgement, offered in the same spirit as the rest of the series: the decomposition is a computational design and a way of seeing, its production value is unproven at scale, and whether the synthesis is novel is left to a literature search.
