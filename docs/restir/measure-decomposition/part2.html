The Measure of Light · Part Two

What Kind of Measure Is This Path?

Six answers, two of which don't fit on the line.

The question

In Part 1 we watched a roughness slider turn a broad hill into a needle-thin spike, and we gave the endpoints names: absolutely continuous when the contribution is a sensible spread-out density, singular when its mass collapses onto a set of zero width. A path's contribution lands somewhere on that spectrum — and where it lands decides whether reuse can safely move it.

So the operative question for every sample a renderer reuses is not "how bright is it" but what kind of measure is it? This part gives the six working answers, shows where they sit, and builds the small deterministic procedure that decides — all of it grounded, first, in the scene you already know.

Here is Part 1's scene again — same rotating camera, same draggable occluder — but the strip below the geometry no longer shows brightness. It shows, per pixel, the dominant measure class of the contribution there. The white sparks laid over it are the same fireflies as before.

Lebesgue's one good idea

Measure theory hands us, for free, exactly the cut we need. Any measure splits — uniquely — into a part that has a density and a part that doesn't:

The absolutely-continuous part ν_ac lives where there's a finite density to sample and reconnect. The singular part ν_s piles its mass onto sets of measure zero — a mirror direction, a focal caustic — where no density exists and ordinary reconnection is meaningless. Drag the control below to pull a real mixed contribution apart along exactly this seam.

The textbook split is two-way: density, or no density. Rendering wants a finer cut — because "has a density" still contains a spike a sampler will never find.

Six kinds of light, and the two that break the line

The two-way split is the principle; the engineering refinement is six computational classes, ordered by how much trouble they give a generic reconnection shift:

Diffuse (AC). Broad lobe, friendly density. Reconnection just works — this is the case every reuse scheme was secretly designed for.   Glossy (AC). Still a genuine density, but peaked enough that the connection direction matters; a hybrid shift earns its keep.   Near-singular. Technically AC — roughness isn't quite zero — but numerically a spike. The sampler keeps missing it, and reconnection lands off-peak, so reuse here is a gamble.   Delta-specular. A perfect mirror or refraction: genuinely singular, mass on a single direction. There's no density to evaluate; only a replay of the exact chain has any meaning.

Those four lie along the hill-to-spike line. The last two refuse to:

Visibility boundary. This is the subtle one. A hard shadow edge is not a singular measure — the density on either side is perfectly ordinary. What's discontinuous is the measure's support: the set of paths that exist at all. Cross the edge and a path that was valid is suddenly impossible. You can't fix that with a Jacobian; the domain itself changed shape.   Volume. A medium vertex lives in a three-dimensional interior, not on a two-dimensional surface. The contribution is mixed-dimensional — part surface, part volume — and transporting it means reasoning about distance along a ray, not just a point on a surface.

Four kinds sit on a line.
Two of them are a cliff and a fog.

The classifier

Deciding a path's class is not a guess — it's a short, ordered cascade over metadata the renderer already has: lobe roughness, delta flags, medium vertices, and the G-buffer's depth and normal gradients. Order matters. Visibility is tested first, before roughness ever enters, because a sample sitting on a support discontinuity is dangerous no matter how matte the surface is. Toggle a candidate's features and watch the cascade route it.

What's coming

We can now look at any sample and say what kind of measure it carries, and we've seen those kinds appear in the real scene — with the fireflies sitting squarely on the classes that don't transport. The natural next question is mechanical: given a sample of a known kind, how do you move it from one pixel's domain to another's without corrupting it?

That machine is the measure lens — forward shift, inverse shift, Jacobian, target pullback, support test, all bundled and made class-aware. Part 3 builds it. Then Part 4 turns it into an instrument: a single number that fires the instant a sample is forced through the wrong lens — and we'll drag the occluder once more and watch it light up exactly where the white sparks are.

What is exact, and what is illustrative

The recoloured scene runs the same verified resampling engine as Part 1 (unbiased to 0.08% against a quadrature reference); the fireflies are emergent. The per-pixel class is computed deterministically from the real geometry: a pixel is boundary when the area light is partially occluded from it (penumbra), shadowed when fully occluded, and otherwise sorted into diffuse / glossy / near-singular by how strongly the glossy lobe's peak aligns with the light there, thresholded against the diffuse base. That is a faithful stand-in for the prompt's G-buffer-and-lobe classifier, adapted to a 2D cross-section; it is not the production classifier itself.

The separation figure is an illustration of the Lebesgue decomposition's shape, not a measured split of this scene's contribution — the component curves are hand-composed to show how the smooth and singular parts come apart. The classifier cascade mirrors the prompt's classifyCandidate ordering exactly. As stated in Part 1, the six-class decomposition is a computational sorting by transport behaviour, not a claim to a theorem-exact Lebesgue split of the rendering equation; whether the synthesis is novel is a question for a literature search.
