Part I left us stranded on a precise ledge. The target we want to sample, , can be evaluated at any point for the cost of a little arithmetic — but no one knows how to draw samples from it, because its normalization is itself an unsolved integral. Resampled importance sampling refuses to be stopped by that. Its entire idea: generate a pool of cheap candidates from a distribution you can sample, then keep one of them with probability proportional to how much the target wanted it. §1 · The mechanism

The resampling rule

Draw M candidates independently from a source density p we can sample. To each, attach a resampling weight — the ratio of how much the target wants it to how often the source produces it:

Then keep exactly one, choosing index z with probability proportional to its weight:

A candidate the target loves but the source rarely makes gets a large weight and is likely kept; one the source over-produces is weighed down. Spin the selector — each segment's width is one candidate's weight, and the pointer lands proportionally.

§2 · The distribution of the survivor

Why the survivor looks like p̂

With a single candidate (M = 1) there is nothing to choose between — the survivor is just a draw from the source p. As the pool grows, selection-by-weight increasingly favors the points the target prefers, and the distribution of the survivor slides off p and onto . With the correction on, the mean lands on true I for any M and any scaling of p̂ — selection never saw the scale, and ⟨w⟩ absorbs it. Turn the correction off and rescale p̂: the estimate misses by exactly the unknown normalization. That factor is not bookkeeping; it is the whole reason RIS is unbiased.

Two things to notice. First, at M = 1 the estimator reduces algebraically to plain importance sampling from p — RIS contains ordinary Monte Carlo as its smallest case. Second, as M grows the relative variance falls and then plateaus; that floor is the part of f the target cannot represent — here, the shadow V. Exactly the Part I residual, met again from the other side.

§4 · The one condition that must hold

When it breaks: coverage

Resampling can only ever return a candidate it was given. So the source p must be able to produce a sample anywhere the target is nonzero — wherever . Violate it and no amount of M saves you: the survivors simply never appear in the uncovered region, and the estimate converges — confidently — to the wrong number.

This looks like a footnote and is in fact the seed of Part V. The moment we begin reusing a neighbor's sample, we are feeding our resampler candidates drawn for a different target with a different support — and unless we account for it, this same silent bias creeps back in. For the probabilist — RIS is the unbiasing of a ratio estimator

Strip the resampling away and what remains is a classical ratio estimator. We want ; we can sample p and evaluate both f and an unnormalized target . The naïve weighted-importance estimate is consistent but biased at finite sample count — the textbook ratio-estimator bias. The resampling correlates numerator and denominator in precisely the way that cancels the bias term, yielding an estimator that is unbiased at every M. If you have met Bekaert's weighted importance sampling or Heitz's ratio estimators in rendering — or the Hansen–Hurwitz / Horvitz–Thompson family in survey sampling — this is the same animal, domesticated for a target you can only evaluate.

§6 · Make it yours

Exercises & the road ahead

The sandbox is exact. Use it to feel the theorems rather than take them on faith.

i
Watch RIS become exact sampling. In the hero sandbox, step M through 1, 4, 16, 64 and read off the distance to p̂. Each doubling roughly halves it. At what M is the survivor histogram visually indistinguishable from the target?
ii
Prove the factor matters. In the estimator widget, turn off the ⟨w⟩ correction and switch p̂ to ×3. The mean lands near 0.28. Now turn the correction back on. Explain, in one sentence, what the factor knew that alone did not.
iii
M = 1 is plain Monte Carlo. Set the pool to 1 in the estimator widget and confirm the relative variance matches single-sample importance sampling. Why does the ⟨w⟩ factor become trivial here?
iv
Break it on purpose. Switch the coverage widget to the half-support source and stream. The estimate converges — to 0.75. Predict where the missing 0.25 went before you read the caption.

A Single Slot of Memory

We can resample a pool — but storing all M candidates defeats the purpose. Weighted reservoir sampling computes the same survivor and weight from a stream, in O(1) memory, and merges two reservoirs as if they were one. That is what makes ReSTIR fit in a pixel.

Colophon & honesty footer

Every widget here runs the exact RIS procedure live: draw M candidates from a source p, weight each by w = c·p̂(x)/p(x), select one survivor with probability w_z/Σw_i, and estimate with (f(y)/p̂(y))·(1/M)Σw_i. The target is p̂(x) = 2−2x on [0,1] (a shape that happens to integrate to 1); the scale toggle multiplies it by c to expose the role of the normalization factor. The integrand is f = p̂·V with a shadow V=0 on [0.55, 0.80].

Verified against an offline reference (200k trials): survivor→p̂ in L¹ — M=1 0.50, M=4 0.11, M=16 0.028, M=64 0.014; ⟨w⟩ recovers normalization — c=1→1.00, c=3→3.00; estimator unbiased at all M, all c → I = 0.8375, relVar 0.75 (M=1) → 0.21 (M=32, the visibility floor); drop the factor at c=3 → biased to 0.28; half-support source → biased to 0.75 with zero survivors above ½. Explicit selection and the streaming reservoir update give identical survivor densities — the equivalence Part III exploits.
