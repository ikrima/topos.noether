Section 01

Simply-Laced Dynkin Diagrams

Among all connected Dynkin diagrams, there is a distinguished subfamily: the simply-laced diagrams — those with no double or triple edges, hence no arrows. In these diagrams every edge is unlabeled (i.e. \(m = 3\)), which means all the root vectors have the same length.

The simply-laced connected Dynkin diagrams are precisely the ADE diagrams:

Interactive · The ADE Family

These diagrams are ubiquitous in mathematics. They appear as if by magic in at least three completely different contexts: lattice theory, quiver representations, and the symmetries of Platonic solids. Baez calls the last of these "black magic."

Section 02

The ADE Lattices, Explicitly

Because all roots in a simply-laced diagram have the same length (normalized so \(\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = 2\)), the lattices they generate have beautifully explicit descriptions in terms of integer coordinates.

An

All \((n\!+\!1)\)-tuples of integers \((x_1, \ldots, x_{n+1})\) summing to zero:

{ (x₁, …, x_{n+1}) ∈ ℤⁿ⁺¹ : x₁ + ⋯ + x_{n+1} = 0 }

A₂ = hexagonal lattice (penny packing). A₃ = FCC lattice (cannonball packing). Hales proved no packing in 3D beats A₃.

Dn

All \(n\)-tuples of integers whose coordinates sum to an even number:

{ (x₁, …, xₙ) ∈ ℤⁿ : x₁ + ⋯ + xₙ is even }

Equivalently: color an n-dimensional checkerboard; take the centers of all the red cubes. D₄ gives the densest lattice packing in 4D. D₅ is densest in 5D.

E8

All 8-tuples where coordinates are either all integers or all half-integers, with even sum:

{ (x₁,…,x₈) : all xᵢ ∈ ℤ or all xᵢ ∈ ℤ+½, x₁+⋯+x₈ even }

Each point has 240 nearest neighbors (at distance √2). Alternatively: take D₈, pack equal spheres, then slip in another D₈ of spheres in the gaps. Viazovska proved no packing in 8D beats E₈.

E7 and E6

Obtained by slicing E₈:

E₇ = { v ∈ E₈ : v ⊥ some fixed root } (126 nearest neighbors)
E₆ = { v ∈ E₈ : v ⊥ some A₂ sublattice } (72 nearest neighbors)

Sphere Packing Records

The ADE lattices give the densest known lattice sphere packings in dimensions 1 through 8. In dimensions 3 and 8, they are provably optimal among all packings — lattice or not.

Section 04

Witt's Theorem

The ADE lattices are singled out by a clean algebraic property. An integral lattice is one where the dot product of any two lattice vectors is an integer. A lattice has a root basis if it is generated by vectors \(\mathbf{v}\) with \(\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = 2\).

Witt's Theorem (1941): The ADE lattices and their direct sums are the only integral lattices having a basis of vectors \(\mathbf{v}\) with \(\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = 2\).

More generally, any integral lattice with a basis of vectors with \(\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 \in \{1, 2\}\) decomposes as a direct sum of ADE lattices and copies of \(\mathbb{Z}\) (the 1-dimensional "lattice" of ordinary integers).

Section 05

Gabriel's Theorem

A quiver is simply a directed graph — dots with arrows between them. A representation of a quiver assigns a finite-dimensional vector space to each dot and a linear map to each arrow. The representations form a category.

We say a quiver has finite representation type if there are only finitely many indecomposable representations (up to isomorphism). Gabriel proved an astounding result:

Gabriel's Theorem: A quiver has finite representation type if and only if its underlying undirected graph is a simply-laced Dynkin diagram — that is, an ADE diagram.

The direction of the arrows doesn't matter! Any orientation of an ADE diagram gives a quiver of finite representation type.

Interactive · Quiver Orientations on ADE Diagrams

Click any edge to reverse its arrow. The representation type stays finite regardless of orientation. The number of indecomposable representations equals the number of positive roots.

Section 06

The McKay Correspondence

The most mysterious appearance of the ADE diagrams connects them to the Platonic solids. Here is the recipe:

Take a Platonic solid (or more generally, any finite subgroup \(G\) of \(\mathrm{SO}(3)\)). Lift \(G\) to a subgroup \(\widetilde{G}\) of \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\) via the double cover \(\mathrm{SU}(2) \to \mathrm{SO}(3)\). The group \(\widetilde{G}\) is called a binary polyhedral group. Now list all irreducible representations of \(\widetilde{G}\). Draw a dot for each. Tensor each with the standard 2-dimensional representation of \(\mathrm{SU}(2)\) and connect the dots accordingly.

The result is an affine Dynkin diagram — a Dynkin diagram with one extra dot. Remove that dot, and you get an ADE diagram. Every connected simply-laced Dynkin diagram arises this way.
