The Tower of Learning

How polynomial approximation, neural network training, and homotopy theory build the same tower — and what it reveals about how learning works.

In 1885, Weierstrass proved any continuous function can be uniformly approximated by polynomials. In 1989, Cybenko proved a single-hidden-layer MLP can approximate any continuous function. In 2003, Goodwillie completed a framework showing any homotopy functor can be approximated by "polynomial" functors of increasing degree.

These are not three separate results. They are the same construction at three levels of abstraction. Together they tell us something deep about how learning works — and they predict phenomena that deep learning theory has discovered empirically.

§2 — The k-Invariants

Between each tower level lives a k-invariant — a topological obstruction measuring how much genuine new structure the next level adds. When a k-invariant is zero, that level is trivial. When nonzero, the learner must pass through a phase transition.

For deep linear networks, the k-invariants are the normalized spectral gaps between consecutive target singular values.

Prediction (falsifiable): The number of distinct learning phases observable in training equals the number of nonzero k-invariants. A degenerate spectrum (ρ→1) gives simultaneous acquisition — no sequential phases. A steep spectrum (ρ→0) gives maximally separated phases. This is testable with Jamie Simon's eigenlearning framework.

§4 — The Meta-Learning Triality

The Goodwillie tower describes how any functor is approximated — including the training functor Train: HyperParams → Models. At G₃, the human enters.

The meta-learning triality (conjecture): At G₃, three agents interact irreducibly — the primary network, a meta-optimizer learning the training trajectory, and the human learning when to intervene. The Σ₃ standard representation is self-conjugate, making n=3 the unique level where metacircular closure holds. The "zero hyperparameters" goal (Open Direction 6) is P∞Train — the fully converged tower. The k-invariants tell you which hyperparameters are eliminable and which are irreducible.

This is a conversation opener. The correspondences range from proven (Postnikov ↔ Goodwillie for the identity functor) to conjectural (the meta-learning triality). What's needed next is a simple, repeatable experiment — the k-invariant computation on deep linear networks is one candidate. The tower is being built. The question is whether it converges.
