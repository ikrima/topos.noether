The Moduli Sea of Graphs

A voyage into the infinite ocean where every point reveals a different graph, and edges stretch and shrink like living things

What Is a Graph?

In mathematics, a graph is deceptively simple: a collection of points called vertices, connected by lines called edges. You encounter graphs everywhere—social networks, molecular structures, circuit diagrams, subway maps.

But mathematicians care about something deeper: the rank of a graph, which counts its independent loops. A circle has rank 1. A figure-eight has rank 2. The more loops, the richer the topology.

The Explosive Growth

Here lies the first surprise. As rank increases, the number of possible graphs doesn't just grow—it explodes. At rank 2, there are only 3 distinct graphs. At rank 3, there are 15. By rank 10, there are over 2.3 billion graphs.

This super-exponential growth is the first hint that we're dealing with a structure of immense complexity.

The Moduli Space

Now imagine a vast mathematical landscape—a moduli space—where each point corresponds to a graph. As you walk through this space, the graphs transform smoothly: edges stretch and shrink, sometimes vanishing entirely. Nearby points host similar graphs; distant points host radically different ones.

"You can think of the moduli spaces of graphs as mathematical shapes with added decoration. If you stand at any point on the shape, you'll see a graph floating above you—a collection of points, or vertices, connected by edges."
— Leila Sloman, Quanta Magazine

The moduli space for rank-n graphs is denoted MGn. It's not a simple shape like a sphere or torus—it has bizarre features, like regions where three walls pass through one another. Understanding its structure has puzzled mathematicians for decades.

The Hidden Structure

In January 2023, Karen Vogtmann and Michael Borinsky proved something remarkable: the moduli space of graphs contains enormous amounts of hidden structure—mathematical features called cohomology classes that reveal how the space is assembled.

Their proof uses techniques from quantum field theory—the physics of particles and forces—to count configurations in the moduli space. The result is striking: the Euler characteristic (a numerical signature of the space) grows as:

χ(MGn) ~ -e-1/4 · (n/e)n / (n log n)²

The negative sign and super-exponential growth prove that vast amounts of odd-dimensional cohomology must exist. Yet here's the puzzle: only one such class has ever been found, discovered by Laurent Bartholdi in 2015 using enormous computational resources.

"We know there are tons, and we know one. It's ridiculous."
— Nathalie Wahl, University of Copenhagen
