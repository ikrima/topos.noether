The Physicist's Pictures

In the 1940s, Richard Feynman revolutionized theoretical physics with an elegant visual language: Feynman diagrams. These deceptively simple drawings encode complex particle interactions‚Äîelectrons emitting photons, quarks exchanging gluons, particles annihilating and creating new particles.

Each diagram represents one possible way a quantum process can unfold. To calculate the probability of an outcome‚Äîlike two electrons scattering off each other‚Äîphysicists must sum over all possible diagrams that lead to that result.

The Symmetry Subtlety

In QFT, two diagrams that are mirror images of each other represent the same physical process. Formulas automatically include factors to avoid double-counting. But for the Euler characteristic calculation, mirror-image graphs are different.

Borinsky had to "play a little game with the symmetries" to adapt the QFT machinery. With programming help from physicist Jos Vermaseren, they overcame this hurdle.

The Counting Explosion

Both in physics and in graph theory, the number of diagrams/graphs at each "order" grows super-exponentially. This is both a curse (making explicit computation hard) and a blessing (guaranteeing rich structure exists).

A Toy Universe

Borinsky's approach treats graphs as particles in a simplified universe: one type of particle, no masses, no spin. This "toy QFT" strips away the complications of real physics while preserving the combinatorial essence.

The partition function of this toy theory encodes exactly the information needed to compute the Euler characteristic of the moduli space. Physics and mathematics meet at this unexpected intersection.
