PART IV — TOY MODEL C

Curvature as Control

Ambient deformation through metric flow on a fixed strip

The world is not moved, cut, or obstructed. Instead the geometry of the world itself — its metric, its curvature — becomes the primary deformation variable. A fixed coordinate strip \(\Omega = (0,L)\times(-1,1)\) carries a warped metric \(g_t = dx^2 + h_t(x)^2\,dy^2\). The curvature bends the world, and the realized transport law follows.

§ 1

The Warped Product Metric

Fix the coordinate strip \(\Omega = (0,L) \times (-1,1)\). Equip it with a time-dependent ambient metric:

\[g_t = dx^2 + h_t(x)^2\,dy^2, \qquad h_t(x) > 0\]

The underlying set of points never changes. What changes is the local geometry: \(h_t(x)\) is the transverse scale factor. Where \(h\) is large, the transverse direction stretches. Where \(h\) is small, it compresses.

This is not moving walls in Euclidean space. We are changing the geometry of the space itself.

§ 2

Gaussian Curvature of the Ambient

Curvature Formula

For the warped product \(g = dx^2 + h(x)^2\,dy^2\), the Gaussian curvature is

\[K_g(x) = -\frac{h''(x)}{h(x)}\]

\(h'' > 0 \Rightarrow K < 0\) (negatively curved, defocusing).
\(h'' < 0 \Rightarrow K > 0\) (positively curved, focusing).
\(h'' = 0 \Rightarrow K = 0\) (flat).

§ 3–4

The Laplace–Beltrami Problem

Solve \(-\Delta_{g_t} u_t = 0\) on \(\Omega\) with \(u(0,y) = U_{\mathrm{in}}\), \(u(L,y) = U_{\mathrm{out}}\), and insulating walls. The metric-dependent Laplacian reduces to

\[(h_t(x)\,v_t'(x))' = 0\]

yielding the explicit solution:

\[u_t(x) = U_{\mathrm{in}} - (U_{\mathrm{in}} - U_{\mathrm{out}}) \frac{\int_0^x h_t(s)^{-1}\,ds}{\int_0^L h_t(s)^{-1}\,ds}\]

§ 5

Effective Throughput Law

Proposition 1 — Metric-Response Law

For the warped metric family, the effective throughput is

\[Q[g_t] = \frac{2\,\Delta U}{\displaystyle\int_0^L h_t(x)^{-1}\,dx}\]

Throughput depends on the harmonic mean of the transverse metric scale.

The world has not changed as a subset of \(\mathbb{R}^2\). It has changed as a geometry. Throughput is controlled by the metric, not by walls.

§ 6

Curvature-Driven Evolution

Let the metric evolve by curvature. Since \(K = -h''/h\), a natural toy evolution is:

\[\partial_t h_t = -K_t\, h_t = h_t''\]

The transverse scale factor evolves by the one-dimensional heat equation. Curvature smooths itself. Neumann conditions \(h'(0) = h'(L) = 0\) keep the ends unforced.

§ 7

Monotonicity Theorem

Proposition 2 — Curvature Smoothing Increases Throughput

Let \(Q(t) = 2\Delta U / \int_0^L h_t^{-1}\,dx\) and \(\partial_t h_t = h_t''\). Then

\[\frac{d}{dt}Q(t) \geq 0\]

Proof

Set \(I(t) = \int_0^L h_t^{-1}\,dx\). Differentiate:

\[I'(t) = -\int_0^L h_t^{-2}\,h_t''\,dx\]

Integrate by parts (boundary terms vanish by \(h'(0) = h'(L) = 0\)):

\[\int_0^L h_t^{-2}\,h_t''\,dx = 2\int_0^L h_t^{-3}(h_t')^2\,dx \geq 0\]

Therefore \(I'(t) \leq 0\), and since \(Q = 2\Delta U / I\):

\[Q'(t) = -2\Delta U \frac{I'(t)}{I(t)^2} \geq 0\]
∎
monotonicity: Q(t) ≥ 0 as curvature smooths — animated proof

If the ambient metric smooths itself by curvature flow, the realized transport capacity of the world increases. Shaping the ambient world makes the eventual dynamics more permissive.

§ 9

Constant-Curvature Subfamilies

Three canonical families on the same coordinate strip:

Flat: \(h(x) = 1\)

\(K = 0, \quad Q_{\text{flat}} = 2\Delta U / L\)

Positive curvature: \(h(x) = \cos(\lambda(x - L/2))\)

\(K = \lambda^2 > 0\) — focusing world

Negative curvature: \(h(x) = \cosh(\lambda(x - L/2))\)

\(K = -\lambda^2 < 0\) — defocusing world
