Section 3: The Lᵖ Spaces

Lᵖ Spaces & Completeness

The workhorse spaces of analysis. Hölder and Minkowski inequalities give the geometry. Completeness makes them Banach spaces.

The Lᵖ spaces collect functions whose p-th powers are integrable:

Definition 3.5: For p ≥ 1,

Lᵖ = {f measurable : ∫|f|ᵖ < ∞}

with norm ‖f‖ₚ = (∫|f|ᵖ)^(1/p)

Different values of p give different notions of "size". As p → ∞, we approach the supremum norm.

The Fundamental Inequalities

Hölder and Minkowski give Lᵖ its structure

Hölder's Inequality

∫|fg| ≤ ‖f‖ₚ · ‖g‖_q

where 1/p + 1/q = 1

The product of an Lᵖ function and an L^q function is integrable. This is the key to duality.

Minkowski's Inequality

‖f + g‖ₚ ≤ ‖f‖ₚ + ‖g‖ₚ

The triangle inequality for the Lᵖ norm. This makes Lᵖ a normed space.

Completeness of Lᵖ

Cauchy sequences converge

Theorem 3.9

For each p ≥ 1, the space Lᵖ is complete with respect to the metric induced by the Lᵖ norm.

Combined with separability (Proposition 3.14), this makes Lᵖ a Banach space.

The completeness proof uses the σ-finiteness of the integral and the dominated convergence theorem:

Proof Idea:

1. Cauchy in Lᵖ ⟹ Cauchy in measure
2. Extract subsequence converging almost uniformly
3. Use dominated convergence to show full convergence

The key: a Cauchy sequence in Lᵖ converges in measure, allowing us to extract a pointwise convergent subsequence.

Lᵖ as a Banach Space

The complete picture

Proposition 3.14

If I is the complete extension of a positive measure on a locally compact space X, then Lᵖ is a Banach space.

Lᵖ = Separable + Complete

The simple functions (and test functions C(X)) are dense in Lᵖ, giving separability. Theorem 3.9 gives completeness.
