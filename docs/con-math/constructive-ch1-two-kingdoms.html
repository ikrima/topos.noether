A Constructivist Manifesto

The Two Kingdoms of Mathematical Truth

From Constructive Analysis by Errett Bishop & Douglas Bridges

Mathematics, Bishop tells us, is "that portion of our intellectual activity which transcends our biology and our environment." Unlike physics or chemistry, mathematical truth seems universal—any intelligence, anywhere in the cosmos, would discover the same prime numbers, the same geometric relationships.

But this transcendence comes with a question: What does it mean for a mathematical object to exist?

For most of the twentieth century, mathematicians have lived in a Platonic realm—a universe where mathematical objects exist eternally, waiting to be discovered. In this view, the number π exists whether or not anyone computes it. The largest prime exists even if no one ever finds it.

But Bishop saw another possibility: a constructive mathematics where existence means something more demanding—where to prove something exists, you must show how to build it.

The Ideal Realm

Classical Mathematics

Objects exist in a timeless Platonic heaven. We discover truth through logical deduction. The law of excluded middle reigns: every statement is either true or false, whether we know which or not.

The Workshop

Constructive Mathematics

Objects exist when we can build them. Every existence proof is an algorithm. Truth requires witness: to claim something exists, you must exhibit it or show how to compute it.

What Does "Exists" Mean?

The same words, two profoundly different meanings

Classical Existence

"There exists a prime number greater than 10¹⁰⁰"

The statement is true because we can prove—through contradiction—that such a prime must exist. We don't need to find it.

Constructive Existence

"There exists a prime number greater than 10¹⁰⁰"

The statement is true because we can give an algorithm that produces such a prime. Existence means construction.

The Scandal

"Our program is simple—to give numerical meaning to as much as possible of classical abstract analysis. Our motivation is the well-known scandal...that classical mathematics is deficient in numerical meaning."

— Errett Bishop, 1967

The scandal is this: many theorems of classical mathematics, though logically valid, tell us nothing we can compute. They assert existence without providing means. They promise objects we cannot build.

"When a man proves a positive integer to exist, he should show how to find it. If God has mathematics of his own that needs to be done, let him do it himself."

— Bishop
